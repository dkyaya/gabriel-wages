# Sanitation City-Service-Structure Scan — 2026-07-06

**Type:** dated source-acquisition-planning memo. Follow-up to `non_safety_sanitation_solid_waste_mechanism_scope_2026-07-05.md` and `non_safety_sanitation_solid_waste_source_gaps_2026-07-05.md`.

## 1. Purpose and scope

This memo checks, city by city, how residential sanitation/solid-waste service is actually structured for each of this project's nine current cities — municipally staffed, privately contracted, regionalized, mixed, bundled invisibly inside an existing DPW unit, or simply not found. This is **source-acquisition planning, not ingestion**: no document was downloaded into `corpus/` or `inbox/`, no row was added to `data/contracts.csv`, and no causal claim is made about wages. The goal is narrower and more mechanical than the prior mechanism-scoping memo: determine, for each specific city already in this project's comparison set, whether a collectible municipal sanitation bargaining unit plausibly exists at all, before any further mechanism development or source-acquisition effort is spent on this occupation group.

This memo does not make a causal claim about wages, does not recommend a PRR, and does not authorize ingestion of any source identified below — any future collection of a document named in this memo requires its own separate authorization, per this project's standing ingestion-layer rules (`AGENTS.md`/`CLAUDE.md`).

## 4. Cross-city findings

**Sanitation appears contracted, not municipally staffed, in a clear majority of this project's current cities.** Of the nine cities, at least five (Arlington, Boston, Franklin, Georgetown, Newton) show clear, source-confirmed evidence of private-hauler contracting for some or all residential collection, consistent with — and now substantially extending — the four illustrative Massachusetts towns (Dedham, Andover, Marshfield, Brookline) already identified in the prior mechanism-scoping memo as using private-hauler arrangements. This project's own nine cities are not an unusual or unrepresentative sample on this dimension; contracted residential collection appears to be the Massachusetts norm, not the exception, among the cities already in this project's design.

**Sanitation is unlikely to be directly observable in most of this project's already-collected municipal CBAs, for two different reasons depending on the city.** For the five cities with private-hauler contracting (Arlington, Boston, Franklin, Georgetown, Newton), the reason is structural: the collection workforce is simply not a municipal employee population this project's causal corpus (municipal CBAs) is designed to observe, regardless of how thoroughly any existing or future municipal DPW contract for that city is reviewed. For the remaining four cities (Seekonk, Somerville, Wayland, Worcester), the question remains genuinely open and worth further, low-cost investigation, since each shows at least a plausible municipal-staffing signal this session could not fully confirm or rule out from public pages alone.

**Sanitation may be bundled into DPW for at most two, and at minimum zero, of this project's current cities, pending further verification.** Seekonk and Worcester are this scan's two best candidates for a genuinely municipal-staffed (or at least DPW-administered) collection workforce, but for different reasons: Seekonk's existing `public_works` CBA is already collected and simply needs a targeted job-description re-read (no new acquisition needed); Worcester's existing `public_works` row is confirmed clerical-only, so testing this hypothesis for Worcester would require identifying and acquiring a different, not-yet-collected Worcester DPW document entirely.

**Private-hauler contracting removes sanitation wage data from this project's design in a way that is more structural than a simple "go acquire the contractor's CBA instead" workaround would fix.** The July 2025 Republic Services strike finding (Newton) is the clearest illustration: Republic Services' collection workforce is unionized, but as a single private-sector Teamsters bargaining unit spanning at least 14 Boston-area suburbs simultaneously. Even if such a contract were identified and its acquisition were separately authorized in the future, it would not map onto this project's one-city-one-bargaining-unit design (per `CLAUDE.md`'s unit-of-observation rule) the way a municipal CBA does — a single private-hauler contract's wage terms are not specific to any one of this project's cities, undermining the city×time-fixed, occupation-varies comparison logic this project's entire design depends on. This is a genuine design-fit complication for sanitation as a comparison group, not merely a data-availability inconvenience, and it applies with equal force to Arlington, Boston, Franklin, Georgetown, and Newton if their respective haulers (WM, Capitol Waste Services, Casella) are similarly multi-city private operators, which this session did not independently verify for each hauler but has good reason to expect given the national/regional scale of firms like WM and Republic Services.

**Seekonk and Worcester look most promising for further sanitation source-acquisition work, but through different next steps.** Seekonk requires only a re-read of already-collected text (zero-cost, zero-new-ingestion); Worcester requires identifying and, if warranted, separately authorizing acquisition of a not-yet-collected document. Somerville is a distinct, third kind of opportunity: its sanitation ambiguity is entangled with a more consequential, pre-existing project gap (Somerville's total absence of any non-safety comparison unit), making it worth investigating on its own broader merits, not only for the sanitation question specifically.

## 5. Implications for wage-mechanism design

- **`sanitation_contractor_substitution` (H33/SN06):** substantially strengthened and broadened by this scan. What the prior mechanism-scoping memo established via four illustrative Massachusetts towns outside this project's city set is now confirmed to apply directly to at least five of this project's own nine cities. This mechanism is not a hypothetical concern for sanitation as a comparison group — it is this project's dominant, empirically confirmed pattern for its own specific cities.
- **`sanitation_dpw_bundling` (H35/SN08):** narrowed and sharpened to two concrete, city-specific candidates (Seekonk, Worcester) rather than a generic hypothesis about DPW classification systems. This is a more testable, more actionable framing than the prior memo's necessarily abstract treatment, since it now points to a specific next action (re-read Seekonk's existing text) rather than only a general possibility.
- **`sanitation_route_coverage_pressure` (H32/SN01):** this scan does not directly test this hypothesis, since it concerns whether missed coverage generates a wage-relevant response, not who performs the coverage — but the finding that most collection in this project's cities is contractor-performed means any future test of this hypothesis using this project's own municipal-CBA-based causal corpus would need to rely on the two DPW-bundling candidates (Seekonk, Worcester) rather than the private-hauler-served majority.
- **`sanitation_public_health_essentiality`** (folded into SN01/H32): unaffected by this scan's findings; remains a general Massachusetts board-of-health institutional finding independent of any specific city's service-delivery structure.
- **`sanitation_service_frequency_buffering`** (folded into SN07/H34): unaffected directly, though worth noting that a private-hauler contract's own service-level terms (not this project's municipal budget documents) would be the relevant source for testing frequency-buffering in a contracted city — a source type this project's causal corpus does not currently include.
- **`non_safety_wage_restraint` (H17):** this scan adds a genuinely new wrinkle to the general framing — for sanitation specifically, the "restraint" this project's design is built to detect (non-safety wage-setting institutions absorbing pressure rather than translating it into wages) may, for a clear majority of this project's cities, not be observable as an institutional-translation question at all, because the relevant workforce sits entirely outside the municipal wage-setting process this project's causal corpus observes. This is a different, more fundamental limitation than the "buffering channel exists but isn't visible in CBA text" finding already documented for DPW, clerical/admin, and library — for those groups, the workforce exists and is observable, just not fully informative on every mechanism; for sanitation, in most of this project's cities, the workforce whose wages this project would need to observe may not be a municipal employee population at all.

2. **Worcester** — second priority, requires new source identification: determine whether a broader Worcester DPW/field-operations CBA exists beyond the already-collected clerical-only "DPW Clerks" unit, since Worcester's own "Trash & Recycling Division" job postings suggest a genuinely municipal collection workforce may exist and be organized separately from the clerical sub-unit already in this project's corpus.
3. **Somerville** — third priority, but recommended on broader grounds than sanitation alone: Somerville's total absence of any non-safety comparison unit is a pre-existing, higher-priority project gap independent of this scan; any future Somerville DPW/general-government source-acquisition effort should check for sanitation-specific content as part of a broader inquiry, not as a sanitation-only research question.
4. **Arlington, Boston, Franklin, Georgetown, Newton** — **not recommended as near-term sanitation-specific source-acquisition targets.** Each shows clear evidence that residential collection is privately contracted, and (per §4's Newton/Republic Services finding) the relevant private-hauler workforce may not fit this project's one-city-one-municipal-bargaining-unit design even if a contractor's own CBA could be identified and separately authorized for acquisition in the future.

**Which source type any future sanitation acquisition would belong to:** if a genuine municipal sanitation/DPW-collection CBA is identified for Seekonk, Worcester, or Somerville, it belongs in the **causal corpus** (`data/contracts.csv`), per this project's two-corpus rule — it is text that causes wages (an institutional mechanism), not text that explains them. A private-hauler contractor's CBA, if one were ever identified, would not fit either corpus cleanly under this project's current design (see §4) and would require an explicit design decision before any acquisition, not a default causal-corpus classification. News coverage of a labor dispute (e.g., the Republic Services strike) would belong in the **discourse corpus** (`data/discourse.csv`) only if this project's discourse corpus is ever populated (it currently has zero rows) and only as illustrative, attributed explanation — never as a substitute for actual wage data.

**Why no ingestion should occur until user approval:** every source identified in this memo was found via bounded public web search and has not been independently verified for authenticity, completeness, retrieval method, or licensing status, and this project's ingestion-layer rules (`AGENTS.md`/`CLAUDE.md`) require the standard public-portal-vs-FOIA intake discipline and provenance-gate checks before any document becomes a `data/contracts.csv` row — none of that verification work was performed or is authorized by this scoping session.

## 7. Unresolved questions

- **Somerville:** whether the FY21 (and current) DPW Solid Waste budget's referenced "contractual relationship" covers hauling/disposal only (with municipal crews still performing curbside collection) or the entire collection function (municipal crews performing none of it) was not resolved this session.
- **Wayland:** whether the town has any town-wide curbside arrangement at all, or whether curbside service is genuinely a resident-by-resident private choice among several competing haulers, was not conclusively resolved — the pattern of multiple independent haulers each advertising service is suggestive of the latter but not confirmed by a direct town statement.
- **Georgetown:** how the town-program hauler (reported as Casella in one source) relates to the separately-branded G. Mello Disposal transfer station was not resolved — these could be two genuinely separate arrangements (a curbside contract plus an independent drop-off facility) or a source-inconsistency this session could not adjudicate from public pages alone.
- **Worcester and Boston (bulk-waste and litter-basket municipal functions specifically):** whether Worcester's DPW-run regular curbside trash collection is performed entirely by city employees, or partly by a contractor not identified in this session's bounded search, was not conclusively confirmed — the city-branded yellow-bag system and dedicated DPW job listings are suggestive, not definitive; similarly, whether Boston's municipal street-cleaning/litter-basket function is organized as its own distinct bargaining unit (and, if so, whether it is already collectible) was not investigated this session.
- **Every private-hauler arrangement identified (WM in Arlington/Newton/Franklin/Worcester-bulk, Capitol Waste Services in Boston, Casella in Georgetown/Worcester-bulk, Republic Services in Newton):** whether each specific contractor's own workforce is unionized, and if so, whether that union contract is a single-city or multi-city bargaining unit, was only directly confirmed for Newton/Republic Services (via the July 2025 strike coverage) — not independently verified for any other city's contractor this session.

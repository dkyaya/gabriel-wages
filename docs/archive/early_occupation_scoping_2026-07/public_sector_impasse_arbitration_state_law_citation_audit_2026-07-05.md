# Public-Sector Impasse/Arbitration State Law Citation Audit

**Date:** 2026-07-05
**Scope:** bounded citation audit of the national scan's public-sector impasse/arbitration claims. No GABRIEL run, no model/API calls, no OEWS/BLS data build, no ingestion, no metadata edits, no causal claim. This is not legal advice, and it does not attempt a full 50-state legal survey — it is a targeted source audit of the specific claim made in `national_municipal_workforce_mechanism_scan_2026-07-05.md`.

## 1. Purpose and scope

The national scan claimed that public-safety-specific compulsory interest arbitration, or comparable impasse backstops, appear outside Massachusetts, but that the exact institutional form varies by state, and that JLMC itself is a Massachusetts-specific institution. This memo audits that claim directly against primary legal sources (state statutes, state labor-relations-agency guidance) wherever they could be located within a bounded search, and against secondary sources only where primary sources were not efficiently found — with every claim below labeled by source type and confidence so a reader can see exactly how well-supported each state's entry is. This memo does not make a causal claim about how any of these institutions affect wages, and it does not attempt to resolve every open legal question about every state's public-sector labor law — it audits the 12 states the task specified as a minimum, plus two additional states (Wisconsin and Illinois) chosen for their direct relevance to the police/fire-versus-non-safety institutional distinction this project has been developing.

## 2. Bottom-line findings

**Confirmed, with primary-source support:**
- Compulsory or state-invocable binding interest arbitration specifically for police and/or fire bargaining exists, via a specific named statute, in New Jersey, New York, Michigan, Rhode Island, Pennsylvania, Wyoming (fire only — see correction below), Oklahoma, and Illinois. Massachusetts's JLMC is one instance of this pattern, not the pattern itself.
- North Carolina's prohibition on all public-sector collective bargaining (including police, fire, and teachers, with no exception) is confirmed via a specific, primary, currently-in-force statute (G.S. 95-98).
- Virginia's 2020/2021 shift from a bargaining prohibition to a local-option authorization statute is confirmed via primary source (Va. Code §40.1-57.2).
- Iowa has a **general, all-covered-public-employee** binding, final-offer-selection arbitration statute (Iowa Code §20.22) that has existed since the 1970s and is **not** police/fire-specific — this is confirmed via primary source and is a materially different institutional model than every other state audited here, including Massachusetts.

**Narrowed or corrected from the national scan's original framing:**
- **Wyoming's compulsory arbitration statute (W.S. §§27-10-101 to -109) applies to fire fighters only.** This session found no comparable Wyoming statute for police. The national scan's phrasing ("Wyoming similarly provides for compulsory arbitration in fire department disputes") was actually accurate on this specific point, but this audit makes the fire-only scope explicit and confirms it via the correct statutory title (Title 27, not Title 1, which is Wyoming's general commercial arbitration act and is unrelated).
- **Iowa's institutional story is more specific, and in one respect the reverse of a simple "teacher exception," than the national scan's original framing implied.** Iowa's general binding-arbitration statute is genuinely available to teachers (confirming the national scan's core point that Massachusetts's voluntary-only model does not generalize), but Iowa's 2017 reform (House File 291) simultaneously introduced its **own** public-safety-favoring asymmetry — not by restricting arbitration *availability* for non-safety units, but by narrowing bargaining *scope* (mostly wages only) and arbitration *decision criteria* (removing consideration of the employer's ability to pay/levy taxes and past contracts, adding a mandatory public/private wage comparison) specifically for units that are not at least 30 percent Iowa-defined "public safety employees." Iowa is therefore both a genuine counterexample to "non-safety never gets compulsory arbitration" *and* an additional example of a state building its own safety-favoring institutional asymmetry, just through a different lever (bargaining scope and arbitration criteria, not arbitration availability).
- **Wisconsin's public-safety exemption from Act 10 is currently under active legal challenge.** A Dane County judge ruled in December 2024 that exempting public-safety unions from Act 10's general restrictions violates the Wisconsin Constitution's equal protection clause, striking down that distinction (and, per some reporting, much of the surrounding law). This means Wisconsin's institutional landscape, as of the most recent reporting located in this session, is unsettled, not a stable "confirmed" data point the way Massachusetts's JLMC-versus-Chapter-150E split is.

**Left uncertain, flagged for follow-up rather than asserted:**
- South Carolina's public-sector bargaining prohibition could not be traced to a single, clean, currently-codified statute comparable to North Carolina's G.S. 95-98 within this session's bounded search. Multiple secondary/tertiary sources (Ballotpedia, a CEPR policy report, multi-state legal trackers) affirm the prohibition exists in practice; the closest primary-adjacent finding was a South Carolina Code Title 8, Chapter 11 payroll-deduction restriction for bargaining-related associations and a 1972 Attorney General opinion referencing a since-superseded 1962 code section. This is flagged as needing follow-up, not treated as equivalently well-sourced as North Carolina's entry.
- Whether New Jersey's, New York's, Michigan's, Pennsylvania's, and Rhode Island's *non-safety* public employees (teachers, DPW, clerical/admin) lack the police/fire-specific compulsory arbitration mechanism was **not directly re-verified this session with a primary source confirming the negative** for each state — this is inferred from the fact that each state's arbitration statute is explicitly titled and scoped to police/fire (and, for Illinois, security employees and fire-department paramedics), consistent with the general national pattern already established, but a primary source explicitly stating "teachers in New Jersey do not have this" was not separately pulled for every state. Pennsylvania is a partial exception: general background knowledge (not verified via primary source this session) suggests Pennsylvania's separate Public Employe Relations Act extends a limited strike right to most non-safety public employees, including teachers, as a different institutional trade-off than the no-strike-for-arbitration model — this is flagged explicitly as unverified this session, not asserted as confirmed.

**Implications for the project's national-vs-Massachusetts framing:** the core framing survives this audit — the *institutional pattern* (a public-safety-specific compulsory arbitration backstop, paired with a strike prohibition) recurs across a genuine, if bounded, set of states, while the *specific vehicle* (JLMC) is Massachusetts's own. But the audit adds real texture the original scan did not have: at least one state (Wyoming) implements the pattern for fire only, not police; at least one state (Iowa) achieves a safety/non-safety asymmetry through a different institutional lever entirely (bargaining scope and arbitration criteria, not arbitration availability); and at least one state's version of the pattern (Wisconsin) is presently being challenged as unconstitutional on the very safety/non-safety distinction this project has been treating as an institutional fact. This last point is worth carrying forward carefully: it does not undermine Massachusetts's own JLMC finding (which is not under any legal challenge identified in this session), but it is a reminder that the safety/non-safety institutional distinction is not a legally settled, uncontested design choice everywhere it appears.

## 3. Terminology

- **Interest arbitration:** a process for resolving disputes over the *terms of a new or renewal collective bargaining agreement* — what the parties could not agree to themselves (typically wages, hours, and other economic terms). This is the process relevant to this project's wage-mechanism question.
- **Grievance arbitration:** a process for resolving disputes over the *interpretation or application* of an already-existing, already-negotiated agreement (e.g., "did the employer violate the seniority clause?"). Present in nearly every CBA, safety and non-safety alike, and not itself evidence of a wage-setting mechanism.
- **Mediation:** a neutral third party helps the parties reach their own voluntary agreement; the mediator has no power to impose an outcome.
- **Fact-finding:** a neutral third party investigates the dispute and issues a report with recommendations; whether those recommendations are binding or merely advisory is a separate, state-specific design choice (see below).
- **Advisory vs. binding:** advisory means the third party's recommendation or award does not legally obligate either party to accept it; binding means it does, subject to whatever narrow judicial review the relevant statute allows (e.g., Pennsylvania's Act 111 allows only "narrow certiorari" review).
- **Voluntary vs. compulsory:** voluntary means both parties must agree before a given resolution mechanism (often arbitration) applies; compulsory means the mechanism applies, or can be triggered by one party alone, regardless of the other party's consent, once statutory preconditions (e.g., a certified impasse) are met. Iowa's system, where either party alone can request binding arbitration once an impasse persists ten days after mediation, is compulsory in this specific sense even though it is not "automatically ordered" by a standing commission the way JLMC orders police/fire arbitration in Massachusetts.
- **Public-safety-specific impasse institution:** an impasse-resolution mechanism whose statutory scope is limited to police and/or fire (or, in some states, a broader but still safety-adjacent category, as in Illinois's inclusion of "security employees" and fire-department paramedics). JLMC, NJ's Police and Fire Public Interest Arbitration Reform Act, NY's Civil Service Law §209.4, Michigan's Act 312, Rhode Island's Chapters 28-9.1/28-9.2/28-9.5, Pennsylvania's Act 111, Wyoming's fire-fighter-specific chapter, Oklahoma's Fire and Police Arbitration Law, and Illinois's IPLRA §14 are all public-safety-specific (or safety-plus-a-narrow-adjacent-category) impasse institutions in this sense.
- **Ordinary public-sector bargaining route:** the general impasse-resolution process available to public employees not covered by a public-safety-specific institution — in Massachusetts, this is Chapter 150E Section 9 (mediation, advisory fact-finding, potential unilateral last-best-offer implementation, rare voluntary arbitration). Iowa's Chapter 20 arbitration is a notable exception to the general pattern that the "ordinary route" lacks compulsory arbitration, since Iowa's ordinary route includes it for essentially all covered public employees.

## 5. State-by-state notes

**Massachusetts.** Source: this project's own prior institutional-verification sessions (Mass.gov DLR pages, M.G.L. c. 150E §9 text, and Mass.gov's JLMC pages), cross-corroborated by this project's own corpus (every police/fire row's `binding_arbitration_statute` field cites MA G.L. c. 1078; every `public_works` and `clerical_admin` row, where filled, cites MA G.L. c. 150E). No new research was needed this session; this entry is restated for completeness of the comparison table. Effect on the project's claim: this is the baseline case the rest of the table is measured against.

**New Jersey.** Source: NJ PERC's own Police and Fire Interest Arbitration program page (primary agency) and Justia's aggregation of the codified statute (N.J.S.A. 34:13A-14 to -21), which is itself a faithful republication of the state's own enacted law, not an independent legal interpretation. The statute establishes conventional binding arbitration with nine statutory criteria and an economic cap added by 2010 reform legislation. Limitation: this session did not separately confirm that NJ's non-safety public employees lack an equivalent compulsory mechanism — the inference rests on the statute's explicit title and scope ("Police and Fire"), which is suggestive but not a direct confirmation of the negative for teachers/DPW/clerical-admin. Effect on the project's claim: strongly supports the core national-scan claim for police/fire.

**New York.** Source: the New York State Senate's own published law text for Civil Service Law §209 (a primary legislative source) plus PERB's own FAQ page (primary agency), corroborated by Empire Center for Public Policy's historical account (a fiscally conservative policy organization — its framing of the law as "costly" should be read as advocacy commentary, not treated as a neutral legal fact, even though its factual description of the statute's mechanics is consistent with the primary sources). Limitation: the claim that NY teachers lack this mechanism is background knowledge, not independently re-verified with a primary NY source this session. Effect on the project's claim: strongly supports the core claim for police/fire; the non-safety comparison remains an inference.

**Michigan.** Source: the Michigan Legislature's own published text of Public Act 312 of 1969 (a primary source, already used in the original national scan). No new research was needed this session beyond re-confirming the citation. Limitation: same non-safety-negative caveat as New Jersey and New York.

**Rhode Island.** Source: the Rhode Island General Assembly's own statute website and Justia's aggregation of the current General Laws, confirming three separate chapters (28-9.1 firefighters, 28-9.2 municipal police, 28-9.5 state police) with a consistent structural pattern (full labor rights except the right to strike, arbitration as the alternative). Genuinely new finding this session: Rhode Island's granularity (separate chapters for municipal police, state police, and fire) is more detailed than any other state audited, which raises a real, unresolved question about whether Rhode Island also has a separate chapter extending similar treatment to teachers or another public employee group — this was not confirmed or ruled out this session and is listed as a follow-up item in Section 8.

**Pennsylvania.** Source: the Pennsylvania General Assembly's own published Act 111 of 1968 text (primary) and the codified citation (43 P.S. §§217.1-217.10), corroborated by Pennsylvania Municipal League commentary (a municipal-government advocacy organization, whose framing of the law as a burden on municipalities should be read as advocacy, not a neutral fact) and multiple Pennsylvania appellate case citations confirming the "narrow certiorari" judicial-review standard. Limitation: the claim that Pennsylvania extends teachers a limited strike right instead of compulsory arbitration is genuinely useful background knowledge but was not verified against a primary Pennsylvania source this session — it is flagged, not asserted, and is a high-value target for a future follow-up search given how directly relevant a strike-instead-of-arbitration trade-off would be to this project's institutional-comparison framing.

**Wyoming.** Source: Justia's aggregation of the current Wyoming Statutes, Title 27 ("Labor and Employment"), Chapter 10 ("Collective Bargaining for Fire Fighters"), specifically W.S. §27-10-102 (bargaining right) and §27-10-105 (arbitration for unresolved issues). This corrects the original national scan's imprecise sourcing: an earlier bounded search had found only Wyoming's general Uniform Arbitration Act (Title 1, Chapter 36), which is a generic commercial/civil arbitration-enforcement statute unrelated to public-sector labor relations, and had not located the actual fire-fighter-specific chapter. This session's more targeted search found the correct statute. No comparable Wyoming statute for police was found in either session's searches — this should be treated as "not found," not as "confirmed absent," since a police-specific Wyoming statute may exist under a different title not searched this session.

**Oklahoma.** Source: Justia's aggregation of Title 11 O.S. §51-101 (reused from the original national-scan session's research, not re-searched this session). The statute establishes arbitration as the "public policy" alternative to a denied strike right for permanent police and fire employees. Limitation: this session did not read the full statute's mechanics (e.g., whether arbitration is automatically compulsory upon impasse or requires a party's election) in the same depth as the New Jersey, New York, Michigan, Rhode Island, or Pennsylvania statutes; the core fact is confirmed, the fine mechanics are not.

**Iowa — the most consequential finding of this audit.** Source: the Iowa Legislature's own published Iowa Code Chapter 20 text (primary, both current and historical versions via Justia's year-by-year archive) and the official enrolled text of House File 291 (primary, 2017). Iowa Code §20.22 establishes binding, final-offer-selection arbitration, available to either party upon request once an impasse has persisted ten days following mediation, for essentially all public employees covered by Chapter 20 — this is not police/fire-specific and has existed in this general form since the 1970s (the statute predates, and in modified form survives, the 2017 reform). House File 291 (2017) narrowed the *scope of bargaining* (largely to base wages) and the *arbitration decision criteria* (removing consideration of the employer's ability to levy taxes and past contracts, adding a mandatory public/private wage comparison) specifically for bargaining units that are not composed of at least 30 percent Iowa-defined "public safety employees" (a broader category than just police and fire, explicitly including state troopers, narcotics enforcement, fire marshals, conservation officers/park rangers, and Iowa DOT motor vehicle enforcement officers, per the statute's own definition). This is genuinely the most nuanced finding in this audit: Iowa is simultaneously (a) the clearest confirmed exception to "non-safety public employees never get compulsory arbitration," since teachers remain covered by the general Chapter 20 mechanism, and (b) an additional example of a state building a safety-favoring institutional asymmetry, just through a different lever (bargaining scope and arbitration criteria, not arbitration availability) than any other state in this table. Effect on the project's claim: strengthens, rather than weakens, the national scan's caution against treating "non-safety lacks compulsory arbitration" as a universal rule — and adds a genuinely new institutional pattern (asymmetry via scope/criteria rather than via availability) that Massachusetts's JLMC-versus-Chapter-150E-§9 comparison does not itself illustrate.

**North Carolina.** Source: the North Carolina General Assembly's own official statute page (ncleg.gov), a primary source, for G.S. 95-98 and its companion strike-prohibition statute G.S. 95-98.1. The statute, enacted in 1959 in response to a Mecklenburg County police unionization attempt, declares any public-sector collective bargaining agreement in North Carolina "against the public policy of the State... illegal, unlawful, void and of no effect," with no carve-out for police or fire. This is one of the cleanest, most directly on-point statutory findings in this entire audit. Effect on the project's claim: directly confirms that in at least one state, the entire premise of a CBA-based mechanism map — for any occupation, safety or non-safety — does not apply, because there is no legally recognized collective bargaining agreement to analyze in the first place.

**South Carolina — flagged as the weakest-sourced entry in this audit.** This session found consistent secondary-source agreement (Ballotpedia's public-sector union policy page, a CEPR-published policy report on state public-sector bargaining regulation, and a New Mexico public employment relations board's own multi-state legal compilation) that South Carolina does not recognize public-sector collective bargaining in practice, but did not locate a single, clean, currently-codified South Carolina statute stating this as directly as North Carolina's G.S. 95-98 does. The closest primary-adjacent findings were a South Carolina Code Title 8, Chapter 11 provision restricting payroll deductions for organizations that engage in collective bargaining or encourage strikes (a mechanism that discourages bargaining practically without declaring it void outright, a meaningfully different legal architecture than North Carolina's), and a 1972 South Carolina Attorney General opinion referencing a superseded 1962 code section number. This should be treated as an open item for a future, more targeted South Carolina-specific legal search, not as equivalently confirmed to North Carolina's entry.

**Virginia.** Source: the official Code of Virginia website (law.lis.virginia.gov), a primary source, for §40.1-57.2, effective May 1, 2021, which permits (but does not require) localities to adopt an ordinance authorizing collective bargaining for local public employees, explicitly including public school employees but excluding constitutional officers and their employees, with no ordinance permitted to restrict a locality's budget/appropriation authority, and with the strike prohibition remaining in force regardless. Limitation: the specific legal basis for Virginia's pre-2020 prohibition is commonly described in general legal commentary as resting on a 1977 Virginia Supreme Court decision rather than a direct statutory ban, but this was not independently re-verified against the actual case text this session. Effect on the project's claim: directly relevant to the DPW/clerical-admin generalizability caveat already flagged in the national scan — Virginia is the clearest example found of a state where the underlying premise (bargaining rights exist at all) was recently and materially changed by state law, and where the change is itself locality-optional rather than automatic statewide.

**Wisconsin (contrast state, not in the task's minimum list).** Source: secondary/journalistic reporting only this session (Wikipedia's summary of 2011 Wisconsin Act 10, and multiple Wisconsin news outlets — PBS Wisconsin, Wisconsin Public Radio, the Wisconsin Examiner — covering the ongoing legal challenge). This session did not pull the primary Act 10 statutory text or the December 2024 Dane County court opinion directly. The pattern (public-safety employees exempted from Act 10's severe restrictions on general public-employee bargaining) is well-corroborated across multiple independent, reputable news sources, which is a reasonable basis for including it here as a contrast case even without primary-text confirmation, but it should not be cited as confirmed with the same confidence as the primary-sourced states in this table. The active legal challenge (a December 2024 ruling finding the safety/non-safety distinction itself unconstitutional under Wisconsin's equal protection clause) is directly relevant to this project's own framing and is flagged prominently in Section 2 above.

**Illinois (contrast state, not in the task's minimum list).** Source: Justia's aggregation of 5 ILCS 315 (the Illinois Public Labor Relations Act) and Illinois Labor Relations Board decision documents (a primary state agency), confirming §14's mandatory interest-arbitration procedure for "security employees, peace officers, firefighters, and paramedics employed by fire departments or fire protection districts" — a broader, more explicitly defined covered group than most other states in this table, and a more procedurally automatic process (mediation triggered automatically 30 days before contract expiration, rather than only upon a party's petition) than any other state audited. This is a useful contrast case showing that even among states with a police/fire-specific compulsory-arbitration model, the precise scope (who counts as "safety" for these purposes) and procedural triggers vary meaningfully.

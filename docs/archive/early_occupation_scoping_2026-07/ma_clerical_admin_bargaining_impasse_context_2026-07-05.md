# Massachusetts Clerical/Administrative Bargaining and Impasse Context

**Date:** 2026-07-05
**Scope:** bounded institutional verification only. No GABRIEL run, no model/API calls, no OEWS/BLS data build, no ingestion, no causal claim.

## 1. Purpose and scope

The prior clerical/admin mechanism refinement session (`non_safety_clerical_admin_wage_mechanism_refinement_2026-07-05.md`) flagged, but did not verify, whether Massachusetts clerical/administrative municipal bargaining has any special impasse backstop comparable to police/fire JLMC, or whether it follows the same general Chapter 150E route already confirmed for teachers (`ma_teacher_bargaining_school_finance_institutional_context_2026-07-04.md`) and DPW (`ma_dpw_bargaining_impasse_context_2026-07-04.md`). This memo verifies that question using Mass.gov Department of Labor Relations (DLR) materials, Mass.gov's own JLMC pages, and the Massachusetts Municipal Association's Select Board Handbook, and cross-checks the finding against this project's own already-collected `clerical_admin` corpus documents (see the companion memo, `non_safety_clerical_admin_existing_corpus_scan_2026-07-05.md`).

This memo compares clerical/admin's institutional position to teachers, DPW, and police/fire, but it does not make a causal claim about how any of these institutional arrangements actually affect clerical/admin wages. It establishes the institutional facts only.

## 2. Massachusetts public-sector bargaining baseline

As established in the prior teacher and DPW institutional memos, Massachusetts public-sector collective bargaining generally proceeds under M.G.L. Chapter 150E. When bargaining reaches a genuine impasse, Section 9 of Chapter 150E provides the default resolution route, administered by the Department of Labor Relations:

1. **Mediation** — the DLR appoints a mediator to help the parties reach agreement once an impasse is certified.
2. **Fact-finding** — if mediation fails, either party may petition for fact-finding; the fact-finder issues a written report with recommendations that are explicitly **advisory and do not bind the parties**.
3. **Certification and unilateral implementation** — if the impasse remains unresolved after the Section 9 process, the DLR Director may certify that bargaining is complete, at which point the employer may implement its own last, best, final offer.
4. **Voluntary interest arbitration** — technically available under Section 9 if both parties agree and the appropriate legislative body authorizes it, but not compulsory and, per Mass.gov's own practitioner-facing guidance, not the ordinary practice for non-police/fire units.

## 3. Clerical/admin application

**Clerical/admin units fall under the ordinary Chapter 150E public-sector route.** This is now confirmed with unusual directness for clerical/admin specifically: the Massachusetts Municipal Association's own Select Board Handbook states plainly that "clerical and administrative employees do not qualify for JLMC services," and separately confirms that "administrative and clerical employees" are a defined, common category of Massachusetts municipal union membership, distinct from police and fire ([Massachusetts Select Board Handbook, Chapter 8: Human Resources, Personnel and Labor Relations](https://www.mma.org/members/msa/massachusetts-select-board-handbook/chapter-8-human-resources-personnel-and-labor-relations/)). This is a more specific and direct confirmation than the general "non-police/fire" framing already used for teachers and DPW — this source names "clerical and administrative employees" explicitly as an excluded category, rather than requiring an inference from a general rule.

**No special clerical/admin-specific backstop was identified in the reviewed sources.** No Massachusetts statute, DLR guidance page, or Mass.gov/MMA summary reviewed this session describes a clerical/admin-specific impasse-resolution body, compulsory arbitration process, or other institutional backstop analogous to JLMC. Clerical/admin bargaining, like teacher and DPW bargaining, falls under the general Chapter 150E Section 9 route described in Section 2 above.

**A further institutional wrinkle specific to clerical/admin, not previously documented for teachers or DPW:** the same Select Board Handbook notes that "administrative employees assigned to the office of the Select Board and town manager or administrator are considered 'confidential' employees under Chapter 150E and should not be union members." This means some clerical/administrative-adjacent positions — specifically those closest to municipal executive leadership — are not merely ineligible for JLMC; they are excluded from collective bargaining coverage entirely, and their pay is set unilaterally by the municipality (typically via a personnel bylaw or non-union classification-and-compensation plan) rather than through any negotiated process, impasse-resolution route included. This is a distinct, additional wage-setting channel not present for police, fire, teachers, or (per this project's DPW corpus review) DPW field/operational staff, and it is worth flagging precisely rather than folding into the general "no JLMC access" finding, since it describes an entirely different wage-setting mechanism (unilateral municipal personnel administration), not merely a weaker collectively-bargained impasse process.

**This is independently corroborated, though only partially and with one important correction, by this project's own already-collected corpus.** Of the three `clerical_admin` occupation-class rows in `data/contracts.csv`, only Seekonk's row has a filled `binding_arbitration_statute` field, which cites **MA G.L. c. 150E** — consistent with the expected finding. Worcester's row has no filled value in that field (its document is a short successor MOA that does not restate the underlying arbitration clause).

Boston's row requires a direct correction. A field-by-field read of the raw CSV row shows an apparent field-count shift starting around the `longevity_detail`/`total_comp_note` columns: the text "MA G.L. c. 1078 (JLMC)" — the police/fire JLMC statute citation — appears in Boston's `total_comp_note` field, and the row's actual `binding_arbitration_statute` field contains the word "clean" (a `text_quality`-style value, not a statute citation), consistent with every field from that point onward being shifted by one position. Read naively, the stray "MA G.L. c. 1078 (JLMC)" text could be misread as claiming Boston's clerical/admin unit has JLMC coverage. **It does not.** The actual contract text, read directly for the companion corpus-scan memo, shows Boston's clerical/admin unit has three separate, narrow-scope arbitration rights — a Compensation Grade Appeals arbitration limited to an "arbitrary or capricious" standard; ordinary grievance arbitration; and a bargaining-unit-integrity arbitration specific to Principal Administrative Assistant grade disputes — and no JLMC reference or JLMC-style compulsory interest-arbitration backstop of any kind anywhere in the document. The primary source document is authoritative here, not the shifted metadata field. This session does not correct `data/contracts.csv` (review-only scope), but flags the anomaly precisely in Section 7 below so it is not mistaken for evidence of JLMC coverage in any future use of this row's metadata.

## 4. Police/fire JLMC contrast

Police and fire bargaining, by contrast, is subject to the Joint Labor Management Committee (JLMC), established under Chapter 589 of the Acts of 1987. When a municipality and its police or fire union cannot break an impasse, the JLMC has the power to **order** the parties into interest arbitration — a compulsory step — and the resulting award is binding on the union and the executive branch of the employer, subject to the municipal legislative body's appropriation of funding. This is the same compulsory-vs-voluntary distinction already established for teachers and DPW, and it applies identically to clerical/admin: JLMC is a **Massachusetts-specific, police-and-fire-specific** institution, not a general public-sector backstop that other municipal occupations can access by analogy or by operational similarity. This memo does not overstate the contrast beyond what the reviewed sources support — it is a state-specific institutional arrangement, not evidence about how other states structure public-safety versus non-safety bargaining.

## 5. Teacher and DPW comparison

Clerical/admin is institutionally **in the same position as teachers and DPW** on the impasse-process dimension: all three fall under the general Chapter 150E Section 9 route (mediation, advisory fact-finding, potential unilateral implementation of the employer's last, best offer, and only a rarely-invoked voluntary arbitration option), and none has JLMC access. This is now the third occupation class in this project's sequence for which this exact institutional finding has been independently verified, strengthening confidence that this is a genuine, general feature of the Massachusetts non-safety public-sector bargaining landscape rather than an artifact specific to any one occupation.

**Important differences worth noting rather than collapsing:**

- Teacher bargaining sits inside a further budget structure (Chapter 70 state aid, net school spending, Proposition 2½) not directly applicable to clerical/admin, which is funded through the ordinary municipal general-fund budget process.
- DPW bargaining documents in this project's corpus show reclassification/out-of-classification pay tied to specific operational credentials (CDL, hoisting, water/wastewater licenses); Boston's clerical/admin reclassification mechanism (the Compensation Grade Appeals process) is instead tied to a duties-based, procedurally formal administrative review with an explicitly narrow arbitration standard ("arbitrary or capricious" only) — a materially more restrictive dispute-resolution scope than the grievance arbitration documented for DPW.
- Clerical/admin has the "confidential employee" exclusion described in Section 3, which removes some administrative positions from collective bargaining entirely — a wage-setting channel not documented for teachers, DPW, or police/fire in this project's corpus.

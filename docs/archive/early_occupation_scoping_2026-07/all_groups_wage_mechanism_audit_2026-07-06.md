# All-Groups Wage Mechanism Audit — 2026-07-06

**Type:** dated cross-group synthesis and audit memo. This is the key working document feeding the next-stage synthesis (a PI-facing report, format TBD, and/or a source-acquisition plan). It is **not** the final PI-facing report.

## 1. Purpose and scope

This memo audits every safety and non-safety occupation group this project has reviewed to date — police, fire, teachers/school employees, DPW/public works, clerical/admin, library, sanitation/solid waste, transit, nurse_health/public health, custodial/facilities, and dispatchers — across five dimensions: mechanism strength, evidence basis (current corpus vs. external/national context), design fit (does the group's wage-setting process sit inside this project's city-level matched-CBA design at all), source needs, and national/Massachusetts nuance. It draws on roughly twenty prior memos, the living checklist (`wage_mechanism_evidence_checklist.md`), the project checkpoint (`wage_mechanism_project_checkpoint_2026-07-05.md`), the national scan (`national_municipal_workforce_mechanism_scan_2026-07-05.md`), the citation audit, and this session's own remaining-groups scoping memo. It makes no causal claim about why police/fire wages rise faster than other municipal occupations — every finding below is a mechanism hypothesis, an institutional fact, or a description of available evidence, exactly as every prior memo in this project has been careful to state. **This is not the final PI-facing report; that report's format and content will be decided separately, in a later stage.**

## 2. Current design frame

This project's core comparison is not simply "dangerous jobs vs. safe jobs." Every group examined so far has at least one plausible upward wage-pressure mechanism (recruitment strain, hazard, credentialing, coverage obligations); the emerging distinction that actually organizes this project's findings is **upward pressure plus strong translation institutions for police/fire, versus upward pressure that runs into weaker, alternate, or entirely absent translation channels for every other group**. Concretely:

- **Police/fire** combine several upward-pressure mechanisms (recruitment strain, overtime/staffing spirals, hazard, credentialing, political salience) with a genuinely unique institutional backstop in this project's Massachusetts corpus: JLMC's compulsory interest arbitration, confirmed via the citation audit to be Massachusetts's implementation of a recurring (not universal) multi-state policy pattern.
- **Teachers, DPW, clerical/admin, library, and (by structural extension) sanitation** all independently confirm the same negative finding — no JLMC-equivalent compulsory-arbitration backstop, only the ordinary Chapter 150E §9 route (mediation, advisory fact-finding, potential unilateral last-best-offer implementation). Their upward pressures (real, in some cases substantial — teacher shortages, DPW's water/wastewater retirement wave, sanitation's fatal-injury-rate comparison) instead translate through classification/reclassification systems, staffing substitution (long-term substitutes, contractor use), service buffering (deferred maintenance, service-backlog absorption), or simple budget competition.
- **Transit** sharpens this further: its wage-setting sits entirely outside any of this project's nine cities' own municipal government, via either a state authority (the MBTA, with its own distinct c. 161A arbitration statute) or a regional authority whose workforce is, by state-law requirement, employed by a private operating contractor. This is a *governance-distance* finding, not merely a weaker-translation finding.
- **Dispatchers** complicate the frame in the opposite direction from transit: they sit organizationally inside this project's own municipal corpus (bundled into a DPW-classified unit in Arlington) and show real, corpus-confirmed operational pressure (a 24/7 minimum-staffing rule) that structurally resembles police/fire's own overtime-spiral mechanism — but their civilian status and (very likely, though not yet independently re-verified) Chapter 150E coverage place them on the non-safety side institutionally. The unresolved national SOC classification fight over whether telecommunicators are "dispatchers" or "first responders" is a live illustration of exactly this ambiguity.
- **Nurse_health and custodial/facilities** are the least developed: nurse_health's main obstacle is a genuine, still-open corpus/design question (an unread, unOCR'd fragment) compounded by a national-evidence population mismatch (hospital nursing vs. municipal nursing); custodial/facilities' main obstacle is a pure schema gap (two already-collected, correctly-documented CBAs sitting under the wrong controlled-vocabulary label).

The city-cycle matched-CBA design (12 healthy matched pairs; 9 exact-cycle, 3 overlap-cycle; 3 unmatched safety rows) remains this project's central scaffolding, but it is not a neutral instrument: it is affected by **source-type production effects** (JLMC-equivalent award documents preserve comparator/ability-to-pay reasoning because a statute directs the arbitrator to write about it; ordinary CBAs do not, regardless of occupation) and by occupations (transit, and to a lesser extent sanitation for a majority of this project's cities) that sit partly or wholly outside municipal-CBA governance altogether. Both limitations are structural, not fixable by collecting more of the same document type.

## 5. Massachusetts institutional audit

- **Police/fire — JLMC / M.G.L. c. 1078 (Chapter 589 of the Acts of 1987).** Compulsory binding interest arbitration upon certified impasse; confirmed in current corpus (every police/fire row's `binding_arbitration_statute` cites it) and externally (Mass.gov). Citation-audited as one instance of a recurring, not universal, multi-state pattern (NJ, NY, MI, RI, PA, OK, IL confirmed; WY fire-only).
- **Teachers — ordinary Chapter 150E §9, plus Chapter 70/Proposition 2½ school-finance institutions.** Voluntary-only arbitration route, rarely invoked (Mass.gov DLR: "no arbitration process" for non-police/fire bargaining generally). A genuine two-sided budget constraint (state-mandated spending floor plus the same municipal levy ceiling every department faces) that no other occupation group in this project shares in the same form.
- **DPW — ordinary Chapter 150E.** Confirmed via every `public_works` row's `binding_arbitration_statute` field and every arbitration clause being grievance/discipline-scoped (Seekonk's explicit "Final binding arbitration will prevail on grievances only"). Massachusetts prevailing wage law is a real, DPW-specific wrinkle on the contractor-substitution mechanism.
- **Clerical/admin — ordinary Chapter 150E, plus classification-appeal channels.** The most directly confirmed of any non-safety group (MMA Handbook names "clerical and administrative employees" explicitly). A newly-discovered "confidential employee" bargaining exclusion (Select Board/town-manager-office staff) is a wage-setting channel not documented for any other group.
- **Library — ordinary Chapter 150E, plus library-specific governance/budget nuances.** MMA Handbook groups library with clerical explicitly. MBLC's Municipal Appropriation Requirement is a real but weaker/waivable budget-floor analog to Chapter 70, unique to this occupation group.
- **Sanitation — often contracted/bundled; ordinary Chapter 150E only if municipal.** No JLMC-equivalent identified, treated as a high-confidence structural extension of the already-4x-verified rule, not independently re-tested. The dominant Massachusetts institutional fact for this group is not an arbitration statute but a service-structure one: private-hauler contracting confirmed for 5 of this project's 9 cities.
- **Transit — mostly MBTA/RTA/state/regional/private; MBTA has a separate regime (M.G.L. c. 161A); not a city-municipal design fit.** A genuinely new three-way finding this session: MBTA employees have their own compulsory-arbitration statute, distinct from both JLMC and Chapter 150E — but this governs a state authority's employees, not any of this project's city governments. RTAs (GATRA, MeVa, MWRTA, WRTA) are required by M.G.L. c. 161B to contract operations to a private company (directly confirmed for WRTA/CMTM).
- **Nurse_health — likely ordinary Chapter 150E or school/health-department channels if municipal/school.** No nurse-specific institutional finding identified this session; assumed by extension, not independently verified, mirroring the caution already applied to sanitation.
- **Custodial/facilities — likely ordinary Chapter 150E or school/municipal channels if in-house.** Georgetown's confirmed unit is school-committee-employed; Franklin's and Arlington's appear general-municipal. No JLMC-eligibility question raised for either.
- **Dispatchers — may be civilian/public-safety-adjacent and may vary by city/regionalization/unit placement.** The most institutionally unsettled group in this audit: assumed Chapter 150E coverage by extension, not independently re-verified for either dispatcher-bearing row; a real, additional Massachusetts-specific layer exists via the State 911 Department's RECC/grant infrastructure and two dated, concrete "recognition without automatic wage consequence" examples (Boston's 2023 mayoral executive order; the 2019 Group 1-to-Group 2 pension-reclassification bills).

## 6. National nuance audit

**Appears broadly national:** the compulsory-arbitration-as-essential-services-tradeoff pattern for police/fire (not JLMC specifically); the foundation-formula school-finance architecture; DPW's water/wastewater retirement-wave concern; the civil-service classification-and-appeal architecture for clerical/admin; the national dispatcher SOC-classification debate (civilian vs. first-responder status); general post-pandemic transit-operator staffing pressure.

**Strongly state-dependent:** whether non-safety public employees have bargaining rights at all (false in NC, SC, pre-2020 VA); whether teachers have compulsory or voluntary arbitration (Iowa vs. Massachusetts); whether police/fire's institutional privilege is treated as constitutionally uncontested (Wisconsin's active equal-protection challenge to Act 10); how many states have reclassified telecommunicators (25 of 50, per NCSL, as of October 2024).

**Massachusetts-specific and should not be generalized:** JLMC itself, its specific statutory criteria, and Chapter 589; Chapter 70's 82.5% cap and exact net-school-spending definition; Proposition 2½'s specific 2.5% mechanics; the MMA Select Board Handbook's specific language; Boston's specific "arbitrary or capricious" classification-appeal standard; M.G.L. c. 161A (MBTA arbitration) and c. 161B (RTA private-operator requirement); MBLC's Municipal Appropriation Requirement; 560 CMR 5.00's specific telecommunicator-certification mechanics.

**Needs citation audit before any PI-facing claim:** South Carolina's bargaining prohibition (secondary-source-only, the weakest-sourced entry in the existing citation audit); Wisconsin's Act 10 legal status (unsettled, under likely appeal as of the most recent reporting reviewed); whether the private-operator model confirmed for WRTA specifically (CMTM/First Transit) extends to GATRA, MeVa, and MWRTA (a reasonable inference from M.G.L. c. 161B's general language, not independently confirmed for each); the federal Enhancing First Response Act/9-1-1 SAVES Act's current enactment status (passed the Senate in 2025; House/final status not confirmed this session).

## 7. Source-type and evidence-quality audit

- **CBAs vs. arbitration/factfinding/JLMC awards.** CBAs are strong for wage schedules, classifications, credential-tied premiums, overtime/callback rules, and management-rights language; they are weak for recruitment/retention framing, peer-comparability reasoning, and workload/backlog narratives — a pattern independently replicated across the DPW, clerical/admin, and library corpus scans. Arbitration/JLMC award documents are the only document type in this project's corpus that states explicit ability-to-pay and peer-comparator reasoning, precisely because the enabling statute (JLMC's) directs the arbitrator to weigh and write about them — a document-production effect, not proof comparator reasoning is absent from non-safety bargaining in practice.
- **Budget books.** Identified across nearly every occupation-specific memo as a near-term, realistic build for overtime/vacancy line-item comparison across occupations in the same city — not yet built for any group.
- **HR/vacancy reports.** Flagged as `likely_hard` across every occupation-specific source-gap memo produced so far — internal HR data is not typically published in a form this project's public-source desk research can reach.
- **Classification/pay plans.** Where already collected (Boston's Compensation Grade Appeals process; Franklin's DPW/library stipend tables), these are this project's richest classification-mechanism evidence; not yet reviewed for police/fire.
- **Union contracts** (i.e., CBAs) remain this project's primary source type by construction (32 of 32 causal-corpus rows).
- **School committee materials.** Identified as a likely-richer source than the Seekonk teacher CBA itself for comparator/budget reasoning, per the source-document-production-effects finding — not yet acquired for any city.
- **Library/trustee materials.** M.G.L. c. 78 (the general public-library-trustees statute) has been identified but not read in depth; would clarify whether library trustees have any independent wage-setting authority.
- **Sanitation/transit service pages and contractor documents.** The single most productive new source family developed in the sanitation and transit sessions — municipal trash/DPW pages and transit-authority "about us" pages directly answered governance-structure questions that CBA text alone could not.
- **News/discourse sources.** Used only as illustrative context throughout this project (the July 2025 Republic Services strike; the November 2025 MBTA Green Line suspension); never as a substitute for corpus or institutional evidence.
- **Why the discourse corpus remains empty.** `data/discourse.csv` has zero rows as of this session. Every mechanism finding to date rests entirely on the causal corpus (CBAs/awards) or external desk research; no news, budget-narrative, or academic-commentary text keyed to this project's own cities has been collected. This is a genuine, structural gap for any mechanism (political salience, public framing of a specific staffing crisis) that would primarily live in discourse rather than causal text.
- **Which claims can be made from current corpus, and which require new source acquisition.** Police, fire, DPW, clerical/admin, and library claims are substantially corpus-grounded. Teacher claims are mostly external-sources-grounded (thin corpus: 1 row). Sanitation and transit claims are entirely external/desk-research-grounded (zero corpus rows for either). Nurse_health, custodial/facilities, and dispatcher claims are a mix — dispatchers have a genuine, if partial, corpus foothold (Arlington); custodial/facilities has two full but mislabeled corpus rows; nurse_health has only an unread fragment.

## 10. Recommended narrative for safety vs. non-safety

Police and fire combine multiple genuine upward wage-pressure mechanisms — recruitment strain (real, though not uniquely severe among local-government occupations), overtime/staffing spirals, hazard and credentialing burdens, and political salience — with a distinctive institutional translation mechanism: JLMC's compulsory interest arbitration, which converts bargaining impasse directly into a binding wage-setting process. Every other occupation group this project has examined faces real pressures of its own — DPW's water/wastewater retirement wave, sanitation's fatal-injury-rate comparison to police, teachers' documented national shortage, dispatchers' own 24/7 minimum-staffing rule — but those pressures do not translate into wages the same way. Instead, they run into classification-and-reclassification systems that absorb pressure administratively (clerical/admin, DPW, library), staffing substitution that defers wage response (contractor use, long-term substitutes), service buffering that shows up as reduced service rather than higher pay (deferred maintenance, service-backlog absorption), outright governance distance that removes the wage-setting process from municipal view entirely (transit, and for a majority of this project's cities, sanitation), or simple civilian-status classification that keeps genuinely public-safety-adjacent work (dispatch) on the non-safety side of every institutional line that matters. The sharp contrast this project's evidence supports is not "safety is hard, non-safety is easy" — it is that police and fire have both real pressures *and* a strong, direct pressure-to-wage conversion channel, while every other group examined so far has real pressures running through weaker, alternate, partial, or entirely absent conversion channels.

## 11. Risks and caveats

- **Current corpus is small (32 rows) and Massachusetts-heavy.** Every institutional finding (JLMC, Chapter 150E, Chapter 70) is directly verified for Massachusetts only; national generalizability has been separately, and only partially, tested via the national scan and citation audit.
- **Discourse corpus is empty (0 rows).** No political-salience, public-framing, or attributed-explanation evidence exists anywhere in this project outside bounded desk research summarized in memos.
- **Source-type production effects are real and corpus-wide**, not a Massachusetts artifact — the same absence pattern (no recruitment/retention or peer-comparability language) has now been independently found in DPW's and clerical/admin's full document-by-document scans.
- **Three unmatched safety rows remain dead weight** for the main matched-comparison design (`ma_somerville_police_spsoa_2012`, `ma_somerville_police_spea_2012`, `ma_newton_police_2015`) — unresolved by any mechanism-development work in this project's history to date.
- **National generalization risk** is real and uneven by mechanism — some patterns generalize cleanly (foundation-formula school finance), others are genuinely state-varying (teacher arbitration access), and at least two Massachusetts-specific vehicles (JLMC, Chapter 70) should never be cited as if they were the national mechanism itself.
- **Occupation composition risk** — DPW, clerical/admin, teachers, and library all show real internal composition spreads (laborers vs. licensed operators; general clerks vs. specialized municipal-knowledge roles; grade/subject/experience mix; librarians vs. pages) that a single "occupation wage" figure would blend and mislead.
- **Contracted/regionalized services sit outside municipal-CBA data** for a majority of sanitation cities and all nine transit cities — this is a design-fit limitation, not a data-collection failure, and no amount of additional municipal-CBA collection will close it.
- **Danger/credentialing is not automatically wage power** — sanitation's high fatal-injury rate, library's MLS/MLIS credential, and dispatchers' operational stress have not been shown, in this project's own corpus or in national context, to reliably produce a wage premium.
- **Public esteem is not automatically wage power** — the identical caution has now been applied and confirmed consistently for firefighters, teachers, librarians, and dispatchers.
- **No causal claims have been made or are authorized by this memo.** Every finding above is a mechanism hypothesis, an institutional fact, or a description of available evidence.

# DPW / Public Works Existing Corpus Scan

**Date:** 2026-07-04
**Scope:** review of already-collected corpus documents only. No new documents were ingested, no rows were added to or edited in `data/contracts.csv`, and no GABRIEL run or model scoring occurred. This is not a comprehensive statewide DPW review — it covers only the seven `public_works` occupation-class rows already present in this project's corpus (five Massachusetts cities/towns), plus one closely related mixed-unit document that includes DPW employees among several other job types.

## 1. Purpose and scope

The DPW mechanism refinement memo (`non_safety_dpw_public_works_wage_mechanism_refinement_2026-07-04.md`) developed the DPW/public-works mechanism map largely from national and regional background sources, and explicitly flagged as a gap whether this project's *own already-collected* `public_works` CBAs actually contain the classification, licensing, overtime/emergency, and contractor-substitution language the mechanism map hypothesizes. This memo closes that gap by reading the actual corpus files.

This is a review, not a data build: no new rows were added to `data/contracts.csv`, no files were added to `corpus/`, and no ingestion pipeline was run. Two of the eight files reviewed (both Wayland-associated PDFs) had no extractable text in their stored form — consistent with their `ocr_messy`/`partial` quality flags in `data/contracts.csv` — so this session ran an ad hoc, read-only OCR pass (`pdftoppm` + `tesseract`) purely to make their content legible for this review. That OCR output was not written back into the corpus or into `data/contracts.csv`; it exists only as a temporary working file for this session.

## 3. Cross-document findings

### Classification/pay-grade structure

Every reviewed document has an explicit, granular classification and pay-grade system, and none treats "DPW worker" as a single title:

- Arlington's two fully-readable contracts (FY2016-2018, FY2018-2021) use an "MC" grade system (e.g., Laborer = MC-1, Equipment Repairman = MC-7) and explicitly tie Motor Equipment Operator grade to CDL class: an MEO who drives a vehicle requiring a Class II license is graded MC-04 (Motor Equipment Operator Grade II), distinct from MC-02 (Grade I) — a direct, textual example of a credential determining classification and pay, not just a hiring minimum.
- Franklin's contract has a dedicated "Working out of Classification" section (14.6), lays off by classification and seniority, and separately tracks classification for a "Laborer/Maintenance" title.
- Seekonk's contract states the "Laborer/Maintenance job classification shall be eliminated upon vacancy" — a classification being formally phased out over time, direct evidence of classification-plan change independent of any wage-schedule change.
- Wayland's DPW unit lists at least fourteen distinct titles in its recognition clause: Sr. Foreman, Grounds Worker 1/2/3-equivalent tiers, Highway Working Foreman, Maintenance Worker, Parks Working Foreman, Water Worker 1/2/3, Water Working Foreman, Sr. Grounds Worker, Heavy Equipment Operator, Heavy Equipment Operator-TS, Lead Mechanic, Maintenance Mechanic/Welder, Medium Equipment Operator, Mechanic/Welder — the most granular classification list found in this scan.

### Credential/license language

This is the richest and most consistent cross-document finding. License/certification language appears in every fully-readable document, and in several it is tied directly to pay:

- **CDL:** appears in Arlington (CDL A/Hoisting = $400 incentive; CDL B/Hoisting = $350; CDL B = $300, per Article XXV), Franklin (CDL A Endorsement = $28.85/biweekly; CDL Tanker Endorsement = $7.69/biweekly; a "CDL Trainer" role with its own $38.47/biweekly stipend and a formal two-person-per-year CDL training program), and Seekonk (Town reimburses "CDL related training expenses"). Franklin's contract states base pay already includes CDL compensation ("Compensation for the CDL license is included in base"), a different design choice from Arlington's separate CDL incentive-pay table — a genuine, source-grounded cross-city variation in how the same credential is compensated.
- **Hoisting license:** appears in Arlington (paired with CDL in the incentive table) and Franklin (employees responsible for hoisting license costs; Town pays for maintenance classes if pre-approved).
- **Water/wastewater licensure:** Arlington requires a "Drinking Water Supply Facility Operator" 2D or 3D license for specific titles (Working Foreman of Water and Sewer needs 2D; Water Systems Maintenance Craftsman needs 1D), with the Town paying for license maintenance and a stipend for obtaining it ($400 for 2D, $200 for 1D). Franklin has the most detailed license-pay schedule found: Drinking Water Operator Licenses, Collection Systems Certification, Backflow Prevention Device Inspector/Tester Certification ($23.08/biweekly), Cross Connection Control Surveying Certification ($23.08/biweekly), and a Massachusetts Wastewater Treatment Plant Operator (WWTPO) License at all levels ($30.77/biweekly). Wayland's DPW contract separately provides for employee assignment to and training for its Wastewater Treatment Plant.
- **Other trade credentials:** Franklin also pays for Pesticide Applicator licenses (Core, and Commercial Category 37/40), Massachusetts Certified Arborist and Tree Warden certifications, and ASE mechanic certifications on a tiered scale (three ASE Passenger Vehicle certifications = $28.85/biweekly, up to "ASE Certified Master Truck Technician" = $57.69/biweekly). Wayland's mixed-unit contract separately compensates DPW superintendents for "special licenses" (HVAC, Scuba Diver, Licensed Electrician, Licensed Plumber) used for Town work, at the DPW Director's discretion.

### Overtime/callback/snow/storm/emergency language

Also near-universal and, in several places, unusually specific:

- Every document has a standard time-and-a-half/double-time overtime structure and a minimum call-back-pay guarantee (typically 4 hours).
- Seekonk explicitly distinguishes call-back from standby ("Call back shall not be interpreted as Stand-By in this Agreement") and has a specific 16-continuous-hour threshold rule for a "weather event" or "snow storm."
- Arlington has a dedicated snow-storm incentive program: employees who work a snow/ice overtime event between November 1 and April 30 receive $100 per four storms worked, scaling in $100 increments, and separately provides that drivers of snow equipment requiring a CDL or hoisting license who work alone receive a higher (MEOIII) pay rate during snow/ice removal specifically.
- Wayland's DPW contract exempts snow/ice overtime opportunities from its ordinary seniority-rotation overtime-list procedure (i.e., snow response has its own separate assignment rule), and separately allows the Town to contact "non-union employees or outside contractors" during emergencies if the qualified-employee list is exhausted.
- Franklin's Side Letter A ("Storm Water Related Activity") ties expanded catch-basin-cleaning and street-sweeping work to anticipated Massachusetts DEP/EPA stormwater regulatory mandates (MS4 permit, phosphorus-level requirements) — a regulatory-compliance-driven work-scope and outsourcing trigger distinct from ordinary weather emergencies.

### Contractor/substitution language

Present, in varying forms, in most of the fully-readable documents:

- Arlington's management-rights article states plainly that "the Town reserves and retains the right to contract out work, or subcontract out work" as a general management prerogative, with no further procedural conditions found in the extracted text.
- Wayland's mixed "other" unit contract has a dedicated "Subcontracting Procedures" article (Article 12) requiring the Town and Union to "cooperate to minimize the impacts of any such subcontracting" when unit work is subcontracted — a cooperative, impact-mitigation framing rather than an outright prohibition.
- Wayland's DPW contract restricts non-bargaining-unit individuals from performing unit work, with explicit carve-outs for "snow removal, seasonal employment, and in cases of emergency," and allows Division Superintendents to personally operate snow equipment if all bargaining-unit members are already deployed.
- Franklin has the most procedurally detailed outsourcing language found in this scan, across two side letters: Side Letter A (stormwater-driven outsourcing, tied to the DEP/EPA regulatory mandate above) and Side Letter B (landscaping/grounds outsourcing, explicitly framed as an efficiency measure "to allow existing staff to be better utilized on other tasks... without impacting staffing or budgets," and time-bounded in the contract text as a right running "from July 1, 2019 through November 30, 2022" — whether that specific window was renewed or superseded in the current 2022-2025 contract term is not resolved by the extracted text alone). Both Franklin side letters impose the same four conditions on any outsourcing: no bargaining-unit layoffs solely due to outsourcing, a bargaining obligation over demonstrable impacts, disclosure of bid documents/costs, and — most directly relevant to the wage-mechanism question — an explicit requirement that "outsourcing will not impede annual overtime," meaning outsourcing and in-house overtime are treated in the contract as substitutes that the union has specifically protected against trading off against each other.
- Franklin also has a striking overtime-contractor interaction not anticipated in the prior mechanism memo: during Sunday snow/ice work, employees are paid double time, and separately, "once contractors are called to begin snow and ice removal operations," in-house employees move to double time for the remainder of that shift — a direct, explicit contractual link between contractor mobilization and an in-house pay premium, rather than contractor use simply substituting away from in-house labor.

### Recruitment/retention or comparability language: notable absences

Two categories from the task's requested keyword list were searched for explicitly and were **not found** in any of the seven documents' extracted text:

- **Peer-community/surrounding-town wage comparability language.** No document contains language comparing DPW wages to "surrounding," "comparable," "neighboring," or "peer" communities' pay. The only hits for the word "comparable" in the full-text search were a health-insurance "comparable plan(s)" contribution-parity clause (Wayland) and a "comparable medical provider" reference in a workers'-compensation continuation clause (Arlington) — neither is peer-jurisdiction wage comparability. This is a genuine absence, not a search-method limitation, and it is a precise correction to the general expectation (carried over from the teacher and police/fire mechanism memos) that comparator language would appear in municipal bargaining documents by default. It is worth flagging that `data/contracts.csv`'s own `comparability_clause_flag` is set to `1` for both fully-readable Arlington rows, but the flagged `comparability_text` snippet in each case is the same workers'-compensation continuation clause found above, not a wage comparator — the flag appears to have been coded for a different kind of "comparability" language (medical-provider/plan comparability) than the peer-jurisdiction wage comparability this project's DPW mechanism map is looking for. This is noted here as an observation about how existing metadata should be read, not as a suggested correction to `data/contracts.csv`, which this session does not modify.
- **Explicit recruitment/retention framing.** No document uses the words "recruit," "retention," or "hard-to-fill" to frame any of its credential stipends, incentive pay, or classification structure. The stipends and incentives described above (CDL pay, license stipends, snow incentives) function in a way consistent with a recruitment/retention rationale, but none of the reviewed documents states that rationale explicitly in the contract text itself — the *mechanism* may be present without the *framing language* being present, which is an important distinction for future textual-signal design (Section 6 below).

**Seasonal/temporary employee language**, by contrast, **was found**: Wayland's DPW contract has explicit seasonal-position provisions, including layoff-recall priority over new seasonal hires, notice requirements for seasonal job availability to laid-off members, and a specific carve-out allowing non-unit individuals to perform unit work for "snow removal, seasonal employment, and in cases of emergency." Franklin also references "summer help" hired "to perform seasonal jobs for the Town" at the Employer's discretion. This confirms the seasonal-staffing buffering channel hypothesized in the mechanism memo is a real, contractually-recognized practice in at least two of the five cities reviewed.

**Wage reopener/market adjustment language** was found in one document: Arlington's FY2018-2021 contract includes a scheduled "market adjustment" of 35 cents to the top step (effective January 2019) and 20 cents (effective January 2020) for a specific classification group, applied on top of (and after) the contract's standard percentage increases. This is a pre-negotiated, scheduled market-adjustment amount written into the contract at signing, not an open-ended reopener triggered by unforeseen future conditions — an important precision, since "market adjustment" language could otherwise be misread as evidence of a responsive, mid-contract wage-reopening mechanism.

## 4. Document-by-document notes

**`ma_worcester_public_works_2017` (Teamsters Local 170, DPW Clerks).** This is a short successor Memorandum of Agreement (not a full restated CBA) covering only a DPW *clerical* sub-unit ("DPW Clerks"), not field/operational DPW staff. It amends only a handful of discrete articles (union recognition classification list, separation-of-employment payout, sick-leave conversion rules) and contains essentially no operational DPW content (no CDL, licensing, snow/storm, or contractor language was found). This is a useful, concrete illustration of the classification-fragmentation point: this project's own `public_works` occupation class already includes at least one row that is administrative/clerical support staff embedded within a DPW department, not equipment operators or laborers.

**`ma_arlington_public_works_2015` and `ma_arlington_public_works_2018` (AFSCME Council 93 Local 680).** These are successive, structurally near-identical contracts for a broad "Labor Service" bargaining unit (per Article II, this unit spans DPW labor service, custodial service, civil engineers grades 1-3, general clerical personnel, administrative personnel, Community Safety Dispatchers, data entry operators, and parking control officers — a genuinely fragmented, multi-function municipal support unit, not a DPW-only unit). Both contain the richest CDL/hoisting/water-license incentive-pay language found in this scan (Section 3 above), the classification-fragmentation examples (MC-grade system tied to CDL class), a general management right to contract out or subcontract work, and (in the FY2018-2021 contract only) the scheduled market-adjustment language.

**`ma_arlington_public_works_2021`.** Same bargaining unit and near-identical structure (confirmed by table of contents), but the stored corpus file's OCR recovered only the table of contents and the first two articles; the substantive licensing, snow, and overtime articles present in the two predecessor contracts could not be directly re-confirmed for this specific contract cycle from the stored text.

**`ma_seekonk_public_works_2023` (AFSCME Council 93 Local 1701 DPW).** A DPW-specific unit contract with a detailed overtime/call-back article, an explicit call-back-versus-standby distinction, a specific 16-continuous-hour "weather event"/"snow storm" overtime-computation rule, a phased-out "Laborer/Maintenance" classification, CDL training-expense reimbursement, and an arbitration clause stating "final binding arbitration will prevail on grievances only" — directly relevant to Task D's impasse-process question, since this confirms in the union's own contract language that arbitration here is limited to grievances, not interest/wage arbitration.

**`ma_franklin_public_works_2022_2025` (AFSCME AFL-CIO Local 1298 DPW).** The richest single document found in this scan, covering "all employees of the Water, Sewer, Highway, Grounds, Stormwater" divisions in one unit. Contains the most detailed license-stipend schedule (Section 3), the contractor-mobilization double-time trigger, and two substantive outsourcing side letters (stormwater-regulatory-driven and landscaping/grounds-driven), each with the four-part outsourcing-conditions structure described above.

**`ma_wayland_public_works_2020` (Teamsters Local 170, Wayland DPW).** Read via this session's ad hoc OCR (no native text in the stored file). A DPW-specific unit with an unusually granular classification list (Section 3), detailed seasonal/layoff-recall interaction rules, a snow/ice overtime carve-out from ordinary seniority rotation, and an emergency contractor/non-union-labor substitution provision limited to cases where the qualified-employee list is exhausted.

**`ma_wayland_other_2021` (mixed unit including DPW, reviewed for context only, not a `public_works`-classified row).** Read via this session's ad hoc OCR. Contains a dedicated Subcontracting Procedures article and detailed classification/reclassification/vacancy-posting rules; because this unit mixes DPW with clerical, dispatch, and nursing staff, its content should not be attributed to DPW specifically without noting that mixture, consistent with the project's two-corpus and composition discipline.

## 5. Implications for DPW hypotheses

- **`dpw_operational_essentiality` (H18):** weakly-to-moderately supported. The corpus confirms DPW work spans physically demanding, licensure-gated, weather-driven functions (water/sewer, snow/storm response), but the same corpus also shows DPW bargaining units routinely include clerical, custodial, and administrative titles alongside field staff — meaning "DPW" in this project's own data is already a mixed, not purely operational, category in several cities. The hypothesis's core claim (DPW shares real operational features with public safety) is supported; its implicit framing of DPW as a cleanly "operational" category is only partially supported.
- **`dpw_cdl_equipment_operator_scarcity` (H19):** the corpus supports the *institutional mechanism* (CDL is tied to classification and/or paid as a stipend in multiple cities) but provides no evidence either way on whether CDL labor is actually scarce for these specific employers — no vacancy, applicant, or hiring-difficulty language was found tied to CDL roles specifically. The hypothesis's credential-to-pay linkage is now corpus-confirmed; its scarcity claim remains untested by this corpus.
- **`dpw_water_sewer_license_scarcity` (folded into H19):** more strongly supported than the CDL case in one specific respect — the license-to-classification and license-to-pay linkage is even more detailed for water/wastewater credentials (Franklin's tiered stipend schedule, Arlington's required-license-by-title table) than for CDL. As with CDL, no direct vacancy or applicant-scarcity evidence was found in the corpus itself.
- **`dpw_overtime_emergency_response` (folded into H4):** strongly supported and sharpened. The corpus confirms overtime, not base-wage growth, is the primary contractual mechanism for snow/storm response, and reveals a previously unanticipated nuance: in at least one city (Franklin), contractor use during an emergency is explicitly linked to a pay premium for in-house staff, rather than being purely substitutive.
- **`dpw_contractor_substitution` (H20):** strongly supported and sharpened. Every fully-readable contract in this scan addresses contracting-out or subcontracting in some form, ranging from an unconditional management right (Arlington) to a heavily conditioned, time-bounded, bargained arrangement (Franklin's two side letters) to a narrow emergency-only carve-out (Wayland). The Massachusetts prevailing-wage-law wrinkle identified in the prior DPW mechanism memo was not directly testable from this corpus (none of the documents reference prevailing wage law explicitly), so it remains a hypothesis pending separate verification, not a corpus-confirmed finding.
- **`dpw_service_deferral` (H21):** not directly supported or contradicted by this corpus. No document contains language about deferred maintenance, service backlogs, or infrastructure condition; this remains a hypothesis about municipal operational practice outside the scope of what a wage/classification-focused CBA would typically document.
- **`dpw_classification_fragmentation` (H22):** strongly supported. Every document reviewed shows a granular, multi-title classification structure, and at least one bargaining unit (Arlington) explicitly spans DPW alongside custodial, clerical, administrative, and dispatch titles in a single contract — an even more fragmented picture than the mechanism memo anticipated, since fragmentation here occurs not only *within* DPW job titles but *across* municipal department lines within one bargaining unit.
- **`dpw_lower_public_salience` (folded into H7):** not directly testable from CBA text, which does not typically contain political-salience framing; no change to this hypothesis is supported or contradicted by this corpus.
- **`non_safety_wage_restraint` (H17):** indirectly supported by the interest-arbitration finding in Section 3/Task D — every `public_works` row's arbitration-related text (where present) describes grievance or discipline arbitration, never interest/wage arbitration, and every row's `binding_arbitration_statute` field (where filled) cites MA G.L. c. 150E rather than the JLMC statute (MA G.L. c. 1078) used across every police/fire row in this corpus. This is a clean, internally-consistent, corpus-level confirmation of the institutional-translation story this hypothesis proposes, independent of the external verification in the companion memo (`ma_dpw_bargaining_impasse_context_2026-07-04.md`).

## 6. Implications for future GABRIEL/source extraction

**Signals confirmed present in this existing corpus** (safe to design an extraction attribute around, since real examples exist to calibrate against): CDL/hoisting/water-operator/wastewater-operator license-to-pay linkage language; classification and out-of-classification pay language; snow/storm-specific overtime computation rules distinct from ordinary overtime; call-back-versus-standby distinctions; contractor/subcontracting/outsourcing clauses, including conditions attached to outsourcing rights (no-layoff protection, bargaining-impact obligations, bid-disclosure requirements, and overtime-protection clauses); seasonal-position and seasonal-labor-substitution language.

**Signals that remain source gaps** (not found in this existing corpus, so any future extraction attribute targeting them would need new source acquisition, not just prompting against what is already collected): peer-community/surrounding-town wage comparability language specific to DPW; explicit recruitment/retention framing language; hard-to-fill classification language; vacancy-duration or applicant-count data; delayed-maintenance/service-backlog language; and explicit prevailing-wage-law references tied to any specific outsourcing clause.

# Massachusetts Teacher Bargaining and School Finance: Institutional Context

**Date:** 2026-07-04
**Scope:** bounded institutional verification only. No GABRIEL run, no model/API calls, no OEWS/BLS data build, no ingestion, no causal claim.

## 1. Purpose and scope

The prior non-safety mechanism refinement memo (`non_safety_teacher_wage_mechanism_refinement_2026-07-04.md`) raised two Massachusetts-specific institutional claims but explicitly flagged both as **background/analyst judgment, not yet source-verified**: (1) how Chapter 70 state aid, required local contribution, and Proposition 2½ actually interact with school district budgeting and teacher wage bargaining, and (2) whether Massachusetts teacher/school-committee bargaining lacks a JLMC-like binding wage-arbitration backstop the way police/fire have. This memo verifies both claims against primary and near-primary Massachusetts sources (DESE, Mass.gov Department of Labor Relations pages, Massachusetts General Laws) and corrects or sharpens the prior framing where the verified facts differ from what was assumed.

This memo does not draw a causal conclusion about how these institutions affect teacher wage levels or growth. It establishes the institutional facts so that later mechanism and evidence work can build on a verified foundation rather than an assumed one.

## 2. Why this matters for the project

Teachers remain the project's strongest non-safety comparison group precisely because they are public-facing, unionized, credentialed, and politically salient — the same features, on the non-safety side, that make firefighters a hard test case on the safety side. The open question this memo addresses is not whether teacher wage pressure exists (the prior refinement memo already established real, if unevenly distributed, staffing difficulty using NCES/IES and Learning Policy Institute evidence). The question is whether that pressure, when it exists, is translated into wages through institutional channels that are structurally different from the channels available to police and fire — and if so, precisely how they differ, not just that they differ in some vague sense.

## 3. Chapter 70 and school finance basics

In plain English, Massachusetts's Chapter 70 program works as follows, per the Massachusetts Department of Elementary and Secondary Education's own School Finance pages ([DESE Chapter 70 Program](https://www.doe.mass.edu/finance/chapter70/default.html)):

- **Foundation budget.** DESE calculates a minimum adequate spending level for each district — the foundation budget — by multiplying enrollment counts across roughly fourteen student categories (preschool, kindergarten, elementary, middle, high school, English language learners, vocational, special education in- and out-of-district, low-income, and others) by per-pupil cost rates across roughly eleven functional spending categories (teachers, instructional materials, administration, and so on). This produces a district-specific spending target, not a single statewide number.
- **Required local contribution.** The state separately estimates each city or town's "ability to contribute" toward its schools from local revenue, based on local property values and income (the "aggregate wealth model"). No municipality is required to fund more than 82.5 percent of its foundation budget from local sources, even where the wealth model would otherwise imply a higher share ([Overview of Chapter 70 Funding, Massachusetts Municipal Association webinar, presented by Brian E. Allen](https://www.mma.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/MMA-webinar-Ch70-Foundation-Budget-Presentation-3.20.24.pdf) — a secondary/explanatory source, used here only to restate a specific numeric parameter, not as a substitute for DESE's own program pages).
- **Chapter 70 state aid.** The gap between the foundation budget and the required local contribution is filled by state Chapter 70 aid, subject to the program's own minimum-aid and other statutory adjustments (not detailed further here, since they are not directly load-bearing for this project's wage-mechanism question).
- **Net school spending.** Net school spending is defined as Chapter 70 aid plus the required local contribution; each district must actually spend at least this combined amount on schools, and DESE publishes annual net-school-spending requirements and compliance figures by district ([DESE FY2026 Final Chapter 70 Aid and Net School Spending Requirements](https://www.doe.mass.edu/finance/chapter70/fy2026/chapter-2026.html)).
- **Local school committee / municipal budget relationship.** In Massachusetts, the school committee generally has authority over how the district's budget is spent once the total appropriation is set, but the city or town's legislative body (town meeting, city council, or equivalent) controls the overall appropriation, including how much above the net-school-spending floor the municipality chooses to fund. This creates a two-stage constraint: a state-set spending floor (net school spending) and a municipally-set actual budget, which may exceed that floor but is capped from above by the municipality's overall fiscal capacity.
- **Proposition 2½.** As established in the prior workforce refinement memo, Proposition 2½ limits how fast a municipality's overall property-tax levy can grow (an automatic 2.5 percent increase plus new-growth revenue each year, or more only via a voter-approved override). Because net school spending is a floor, not a ceiling, and because schools are typically the largest single line in a Massachusetts municipal budget, the levy limit is the binding constraint on how much a municipality *can* spend above the Chapter 70 floor, including on teacher compensation.

**Why this matters for salary bargaining, stated precisely:** a school committee negotiating a teacher contract is not simply choosing what teachers are worth. It is choosing within a budget envelope whose floor is set by a state formula (net school spending) and whose ceiling is set by municipal levy capacity (Proposition 2½) and the town's or city's willingness to fund above the state-required floor, including through an override vote. This is a materially different budget-setting structure than a municipal general fund department (like police or fire) negotiating within the same overall levy limit, because schools have both a state-mandated spending floor and considerable local political control over how far above that floor the district is actually funded.

## 4. How school finance can mediate teacher wage pressure

Several school-finance features can shape *how* a teacher staffing shortage or wage-pressure signal gets absorbed, independent of the underlying labor-market pressure itself.

- **State aid and local contribution constraints.** A district facing a shortage-driven case for higher pay must find room either within its existing net-school-spending envelope (by reallocating from another budget line) or through new revenue (state aid growth, local override, or new local revenue growth). Because the required local contribution and Chapter 70 aid formulas move on the state's schedule and timeline, not the district's bargaining calendar, a district cannot generally accelerate its own funding in response to an urgent mid-cycle staffing problem the way a police/fire department might request a supplemental appropriation or rely on an arbitration award that a legislative body is statutorily pressured to fund.
- **Municipal budget tradeoffs.** Because schools compete with police, fire, and other municipal departments for the same overall levy capacity, a raise for teachers can mean less room for other departments' raises (or vice versa) unless a district successfully secures an override specifically tied to school funding. This is the direct mechanism behind the `school_budget_constraint` hypothesis already added to the hypothesis matrix.
- **Class-size and staffing tradeoffs.** Consistent with the shortage-buffering discussion in the prior refinement memo, a district facing a binding budget constraint has multiple non-wage margins available — larger class sizes, unfilled specialist positions, increased reliance on long-term substitutes or emergency-certified hires, or program cuts — before it needs to resort to a base-wage increase. None of these buffering channels are available in the same way to police or fire coverage, where minimum-staffing and 24/7 response obligations more directly convert a staffing shortfall into overtime spending rather than a service-level reduction.
- **How this differs from police/fire municipal bargaining.** Police and fire compensation is also constrained by the same overall municipal levy limit, but (per Section 6 below) a JLMC interest-arbitration award is binding on the union and the employer's executive branch once authorized, and the relevant legislative body faces real institutional and political pressure to fund it, since failing to do so does not make the underlying legal wage obligation disappear. Teacher compensation, by contrast, is constrained by both the levy limit and the absence of an equivalent compulsory backstop (Section 5), meaning a school committee has comparatively more practical latitude to hold a line in negotiations and let a dispute resolve into whichever non-wage buffering channel is available, up to and including implementing its own last, best offer (Section 5).

## 5. Massachusetts teacher bargaining and impasse process

Teacher bargaining in Massachusetts proceeds under the general public-sector collective bargaining law, M.G.L. Chapter 150E, with the school committee as the employer-side party. The impasse procedure that applies to teachers (and to most other Massachusetts public employees who are not police or fire) is Section 9 of Chapter 150E, administered by the state Department of Labor Relations (DLR):

1. **Mediation.** After a petition establishing that a genuine impasse exists, the DLR appoints a mediator to help the parties reach agreement ([Mass.gov, Contract (Interest) Mediation and Fact Finding](https://www.mass.gov/contract-interest-mediation-and-fact-finding); [M.G.L. c. 150E, §9](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter150E/Section9)).
2. **Fact-finding.** If mediation does not resolve the impasse, either party may petition for fact-finding. A fact-finder holds hearings and issues a written report with recommendations for resolving the disputed issues. Critically, **these recommendations are explicitly advisory and do not bind the parties** ([Mass.gov, Contract (Interest) Mediation and Fact Finding](https://www.mass.gov/contract-interest-mediation-and-fact-finding)). If the impasse remains unresolved ten days after the fact-finder's report, the DLR must make the report public — a transparency/pressure mechanism, not a binding one.
3. **Certification and unilateral implementation.** If the parties still cannot reach agreement after the Section 9 process, the DLR Director may certify that the collective bargaining process is complete, at which point the employer (the school committee) is permitted to implement its own last, best, final offer unilaterally ([Mass.gov, G. Impasse](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/g-impasse); [M.G.L. c. 150E, §9](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter150E/Section9)).
4. **Voluntary interest arbitration (rare, not compulsory).** The statute does technically allow interest arbitration to resolve an impasse, but only if both parties voluntarily agree to it, and — in the case of school employees specifically — only if that arbitration proceeding is authorized by the appropriate school committee; any resulting award is binding on the parties and the relevant legislative body only once authorized in this way, and funding still depends on the legislative body's appropriation ([M.G.L. c. 150E, §9](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXXI/Chapter150E/Section9)).

**Is fact-finding binding? No.** It is explicitly advisory, per Mass.gov's own description, confirmed above.

**Is interest arbitration typical or available for teachers? Available in principle, but not typical, and not compulsory.** Mass.gov's own practitioner-facing FAQ on impasse procedures states this plainly and should be treated as the operative, practice-level answer for this project: "if unions other than police or firefighter unions reach an impasse in negotiations, they can petition the Massachusetts Department of Labor Relations for mediation or fact-finding. There is, however, no arbitration process to resolve unsettled contract disputes with non-police and fire unions" ([Mass.gov, G. Impasse](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/g-impasse)). The narrower statutory text in Section 9 does leave open a voluntary arbitration pathway, so "no arbitration process" should be read as "no compulsory arbitration process in ordinary practice," not as an absolute statutory impossibility — but the practical distinction from JLMC (Section 6) is the same either way: nothing forces a school committee into arbitration the way JLMC forces a municipality into arbitration for police/fire.

**What should and should not be compared to police/fire JLMC:** it is accurate to say that teacher bargaining lacks a JLMC-equivalent *compulsory* binding-arbitration backstop. It is not accurate to say that Massachusetts law categorically bars any form of arbitration for teacher bargaining, since a voluntary route exists on paper. The precise, defensible claim is an institutional-design difference in whether arbitration is *ordered upon impasse* (JLMC) versus *available only by mutual consent, rarely invoked in practice* (general Chapter 150E Section 9 employees including teachers) — not a difference between "has a backstop" and "has literally no process at all."

## 6. Police/fire JLMC comparison

The Joint Labor Management Committee (JLMC) is a specialized Massachusetts impasse-resolution body created for municipal police and fire bargaining, under Chapter 589 of the Acts of 1987 ([Mass.gov, D. The Joint Labor Management Committee](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/d-the-joint-labor-management-committee); [Mass.gov, History of the JLMC](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/history-of-the-jlmc)). In plain English:

- **What makes it special.** When a municipality and its police or fire union cannot break a bargaining impasse, the JLMC has the power to **order** the parties into interest arbitration — this is a compulsory step, not something either party must agree to first, in sharp contrast to the voluntary-only arbitration route available to teachers and other non-police/fire employees under Chapter 150E Section 9 ([Mass.gov, G. Impasse](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/g-impasse)).
- **What the award does.** The arbitration panel's decision is binding on the union and on the executive branch of the municipal employer. Funding still requires the municipal legislative body (city council, town meeting, or equivalent) to appropriate the necessary funds, so a JLMC award is not entirely immune from the local budget process — but the underlying legal obligation to pay the award exists regardless of that appropriation vote, which is a materially stronger position than a school committee's unilaterally implemented last-best offer, which was never legally validated by a neutral third party in the first place.
- **Statutory decision criteria.** The law establishing the JLMC directs the arbitration panel to weigh factors such as the employer's ability to pay given local fiscal conditions, how the employer compensates other employees (particularly other police/fire employees) historically and currently, and how demographically comparable communities compensate their police/fire employees ([Mass.gov, D. The Joint Labor Management Committee](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/d-the-joint-labor-management-committee)). This is the statutory hook behind the project's existing `comparability_emphasis` (v9) attribute: comparator reasoning shows up explicitly in JLMC awards partly because the statute directs arbitrators to consider it, not only because police/fire bargaining is inherently more comparison-driven than other municipal bargaining.
- **Why reasoning-rich awards and settlement pressure follow.** Because arbitration is a real, compulsory possibility once an impasse is certified, both sides have a strong incentive to prepare and present comparability and ability-to-pay evidence well before any actual award — and settlements reached in JLMC's shadow may reflect anticipated arbitration outcomes even when no award is ever issued (the "shadow-of-arbitration" model already discussed in the prior workforce refinement memo). This compulsory structure is also why JLMC-adjacent documents are a rich source of explicit wage-setting reasoning: the process itself forces the reasoning onto the page.
- **Why this does not automatically apply to teachers.** Because Chapter 150E Section 9 does not order arbitration the way JLMC does, a school committee facing a difficult negotiation does not face the same institutional pressure to reach a defensible, criteria-based settlement before a neutral third party. It can instead let the process run through fact-finding (advisory only) and then implement its own last, best offer. This is the concrete institutional mechanism behind why teacher bargaining documents are less likely than JLMC-adjacent police/fire documents to contain the kind of explicit, criteria-referenced wage-setting reasoning that the project's `comparability_emphasis` and `arbitration_or_impasse_backstop` attributes are designed to detect — not because teacher bargaining lacks any comparator reasoning at all, but because there is no equivalent compulsory-arbitration process forcing that reasoning into a formal written record as often.

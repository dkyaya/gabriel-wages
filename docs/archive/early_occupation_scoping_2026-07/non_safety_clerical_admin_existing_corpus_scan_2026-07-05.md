# Clerical / Administrative Existing Corpus Scan

**Date:** 2026-07-05
**Scope:** review of already-collected corpus documents only. No new documents were ingested, no rows were added to or edited in `data/contracts.csv`, and no GABRIEL run or model scoring occurred. This is not a comprehensive statewide clerical/admin review — it covers only the three `clerical_admin` occupation-class rows already present in this project's corpus (three Massachusetts cities/towns), plus a brief note on one closely related mixed-unit document that includes clerical staff among several other job types.

## 1. Purpose and scope

The clerical/admin mechanism refinement memo (`non_safety_clerical_admin_wage_mechanism_refinement_2026-07-05.md`) developed the clerical/admin mechanism map from a light, illustrative read of this project's corpus plus national and Massachusetts-inclusive desk research, and explicitly flagged a full document-by-document corpus review as a follow-up task, mirroring the dedicated DPW corpus-scan session. This memo closes that gap by reading the actual corpus files in full, against the same discipline established for police/fire, teachers, and DPW.

This is a review, not a data build: no new rows were added to `data/contracts.csv`, no files were added to `corpus/`, and no ingestion pipeline was run. One of the three files reviewed (Seekonk) had no extractable text in its stored form, consistent with its `ocr_messy` quality flag; this session reused a prior session's ad hoc, read-only OCR pass (`pdftoppm` + `tesseract`) to make its content legible, rather than re-running OCR. That OCR output was not written back into the corpus or into `data/contracts.csv`.

## 3. Cross-document findings

### Classification/pay-grade structure

Boston's contract has by far the most extensive classification structure found in this scan, and it is considerably broader than a simple "clerical" title list. The contract uses an "MM" grade system for Principal Administrative Assistant positions (e.g., MM-7, MM-8, MM-11 observed), with detailed rules governing which grade levels remain inside the bargaining unit versus are excluded as confidential/managerial. Separately, the BCYF-derived portion of the unit has its own grade tables organized into numbered "Groups," and the titles in those tables extend well beyond clerical work: Administrative Coordinator (Grade 8), Executive Secretary (Grade 8), Unit Manager (Grade 7), Program Manager (Grade 6), Network Administrator (Grade 8), Grants Manager (Grade 7), Personnel Officer (Grade 7), Executive Assistant/Public Relations (Grade 6), Facilities Manager (Grade 7), Aquatics Manager (Grade 7), Pool Manager (Grade 4), and Assistant Pool Manager (Grade 3), among others. Worcester's contract (a short successor MOA) references only a general "salary schedule" and a "maximum step" without a visible grade table in the extracted text. Seekonk's contract contains no visible pay-grade table in the extracted text; its only compensation-adjacent structure found is the longevity schedule (Section on longevity below).

### Reclassification or classification-review language

Boston's contract contains the single richest reclassification mechanism found across this project's entire corpus to date, across police/fire, teachers, DPW, and clerical/admin alike: **Article 33, "Compensation Grade Appeals (CGA)."** This article is stated to be "the exclusive procedure for changing the compensation grade for any position that this Agreement covers, notwithstanding any prior arbitration awards, agreements, or practice," and it explicitly strips arbitrators of authority to change a position's grade through an ordinary grievance. The procedure has a specific sequence: the Union submits a CGA application to the Office of Labor Relations (OLR); OLR forwards it to the "OHR Classification and Compensation Unit" and the employee's department within 5 days; that unit reviews and must decide within 60 days; a denial can be appealed to the City's Director of Human Resources (a further 90-day review); a further denial can be grieved to arbitration, but **the arbitrator's authority is explicitly limited to whether the City's determination was "arbitrary or capricious"** — not a full merits review of whether the job actually deserves the higher grade — and no remedy may predate the filing date. This is a materially narrower dispute-resolution scope than the interest-arbitration processes documented for police/fire (JLMC) or the ordinary grievance arbitration documented for DPW.

Two further, directly on-point provisions sharpen this finding. First, the same CGA article states that "the review shall not consider perceived changes in job duties related to new technology, state or federal mandates, and/or to increases in the volume of work or duties" as grounds for a grade upgrade — an explicit contractual exclusion of workload growth and technology-driven duty changes as reclassification triggers. Second, Article 30 ("Technology and Assets") independently states that "perceived changes in job duties related to new technology are not a basis for reclassification," cross-referencing and reinforcing the same exclusion. Worcester's and Seekonk's contracts contain no reclassification or classification-review language in the extracted text.

### Compression/internal-equity language

No document in this scan contains explicit "pay compression," "salary inversion," "internal equity," or "minimum wage compliance" language. This is a genuine absence, not a search-method limitation, and it means the pay-compression mechanism discussed in the mechanism-refinement memo remains supported only by the national/multi-state Century Foundation estimate, not by anything found in this project's own corpus.

### Workload/backlog/service-coverage language

No document contains "backlog," "reduced hours," "cross-training," "shared services," or "office coverage" language in the extracted text. The closest adjacent finding is Boston's explicit statement (Section above) that increased workload/duty volume is *not* an accepted basis for a compensation-grade appeal — which is evidence about how workload growth does *not* translate into pay under this contract's rules, rather than evidence about how workload shortfalls are absorbed operationally. This is a notable absence relative to the richer overtime/storm-response and contractor-substitution language found in the DPW corpus scan.

### Management rights/reorganization language

Boston's Article 8 ("Management Rights") is a standard, broad management-rights clause reserving the right "to transfer, suspend, discipline, discharge, lay off and recall personnel; to establish, create [job] classifications... to reclassify existing positions based on assigned duties." Article 32 ("BCYF Merger") documents an actual, named example of unit reorganization in progress: the contract records that the BCYF merger will proceed upon ratification, and separately states "the City will continue to meet its impact bargaining obligations with regard to the potential merger of the Public Works and Transportation Departments after an agreement has been reached in successor contracts negotiations" — a live, named example of cross-departmental reorganization affecting this bargaining unit, with an explicit impact-bargaining obligation attached. Worcester's and Seekonk's contracts contain no comparable management-rights-to-reorganize or merger/consolidation language in the extracted text.

### Recruitment/retention or comparability language: notable absences

As with the DPW corpus scan, no document in this scan contains "recruit," "retention," "hard-to-fill," "surrounding," "peer," or "comparable communities" wage-comparison language. This confirms, for a second non-safety occupation class, that peer-jurisdiction wage comparability language is not a default feature of this project's Massachusetts municipal bargaining corpus — it was not found in any of the seven `public_works` documents in the prior DPW scan, and it is not found in any of the three `clerical_admin` documents in this scan either.

### Other notable findings not anticipated by the mechanism-refinement memo

- **A CDL stipend inside a "clerical/admin" unit.** Boston's Article 34 provides a $30 weekly stipend to any SENA bargaining-unit member who "possess[es] a valid Commercial Driver's License (CDL) and consistently use[s] their CDL in the course of their employment." This shows the occupational boundary between "clerical/admin" and "DPW/operational" work is not clean in practice — this project's own corpus includes a nominally clerical/admin unit with some CDL-holding, driving members, an occupational-composition wrinkle not anticipated in the mechanism-refinement memo's occupation map.
- **A narrow-scope, unit-integrity arbitration right, distinct from the CGA process.** Boston's recognition clause (covering Principal Administrative Assistant positions specifically) allows the Union to "file for arbitration on the issue of whether the City has utilized the MM-8 Principal Administrative Assistant position to undermine the bargaining unit" if a joint Labor-Management Committee cannot agree on a specific position's inclusion/exclusion. This is a third, distinct, narrow-scope arbitration right (about bargaining-unit integrity, not about compensation grade or interest terms), further illustrating how fragmented and procedurally specific this unit's dispute-resolution rights are compared with a single, general interest-arbitration backstop.
- **Extensive civil-service-linked bumping and layoff provisions**, closely paralleling the DPW corpus's Arlington findings, tied to job classification under G.L. c. 31.

## 4. Document-by-document notes

**`ma_worcester_clerical_admin_2017` (NAGE Local 490, SEIU, Worcester Clerical Association).** A short successor Memorandum of Agreement, not a full restated CBA, amending only Article 5 (Wages: a plain 2 percent annual base-wage increase for three years, plus a new maximum step 2 percent above the prior maximum, effective July 1, 2017), Article 10 (Personal Leave), Article 17 (Administrative Leave), and Article 25 (contract duration). No classification, reclassification, compression, workload, management-rights, or recruitment/retention language was found. This is the same document pattern already observed for Worcester's DPW clerks unit (`ma_worcester_public_works_2017`) in the same contract cycle — both amend a handful of discrete articles rather than restating a full agreement, and both received an identical 2 percent annual wage pattern, consistent with a citywide bargaining-pattern practice.

**`ma_boston_clerical_admin_2023` (SENA Local 9158).** The richest document found in either the DPW or clerical/admin corpus scans to date. Contains: detailed MM-grade and BCYF-Group grade tables spanning far more than clerical titles (Section 3); the most procedurally detailed reclassification mechanism found in this project's corpus (the Compensation Grade Appeals process, with its narrow arbitrary-or-capricious arbitration scope and explicit technology/workload exclusion); a broad management-rights clause; a documented, named departmental-merger/reorganization history with an attached impact-bargaining obligation; a CDL stipend for members who use a CDL in their work; a narrow bargaining-unit-integrity arbitration right; extensive civil-service-linked bumping/layoff provisions; and standard overtime/compensatory-time provisions administered at "the discretion of the Central Office" rather than through a storm/emergency-driven structure like DPW's.

**`ma_seekonk_clerical_admin_2021` (AFSCME Local 1701, Administrative Secretaries).** Read via a prior session's ad hoc OCR (no native text in the stored file). A school-based unit (Seekonk School Committee) with a comparatively simple structure: seniority-based vacancy/transfer/layoff-recall rules, a flat longevity-payment schedule ($700 at 10 years, $900 at 15, $1,200 at 20, $1,500 at 30), a sick-day-donation provision limited "to another employee in the same classification," and a single named, district-wide position (Administrative Assistant for Nursing and Student Services) with a defined 37.5-hour workweek. No reclassification, classification-review, compression, management-rights-to-reorganize, or CDL/credential-stipend language was found.

## 5. Implications for clerical/admin hypotheses

- **`clerical_admin_classification_restraint` (H23):** strongly supported and sharpened for Boston specifically. The CGA process is not merely a formal review body (as the mechanism-refinement memo hypothesized from a lighter read); it is an explicit, multi-stage, narrow-remedy procedure that forecloses a full-merits arbitration review and explicitly excludes workload/technology-driven duty changes as valid grounds — a stronger and more precisely restrictive mechanism than the original hypothesis anticipated. Not evidenced at all for Worcester or Seekonk, which have no reclassification apparatus visible in their (much shorter) contracts.
- **`clerical_admin_reclassification_pressure` (H24):** supported for Boston in the same way; the hypothesis that clerical/admin wage pressure surfaces through reclassification rather than across-the-board bargaining is corpus-confirmed for this specific unit, though the corpus cannot show how often CGA applications are actually filed or how they are typically resolved.
- **`clerical_admin_pay_compression` (H25):** not supported or contradicted by this corpus; no compression, minimum-wage, or internal-equity language was found in any of the three documents. This hypothesis remains supported only by the national/Massachusetts-inclusive Century Foundation estimate cited in the mechanism-refinement memo, not by this project's own corpus.
- **`clerical_admin_service_backlog_absorption` (H26):** not directly supported or contradicted; no backlog, reduced-hours, or cross-coverage language was found. The CGA process's explicit exclusion of "increases in the volume of work" as a reclassification ground is adjacent but distinct evidence — it shows how workload growth does *not* convert into pay under Boston's rules, which is consistent with, but not direct evidence for, the hypothesis that workload strain is absorbed rather than compensated.
- **`clerical_admin_lower_public_salience` (H27):** not directly testable from CBA text, which does not typically contain political-salience framing; no change to this hypothesis is supported or contradicted by this corpus.
- **`clerical_admin_specialized_municipal_knowledge`:** weakly supported by the breadth of specialized, non-interchangeable titles found in Boston's BCYF-derived grade tables (Grants Manager, Network Administrator, Facilities Manager, Personnel Officer) — these are specialized administrative-support roles, not generic clerical positions, reinforcing the composition point but not directly evidencing a wage-pressure claim.
- **`clerical_admin_automation_reorganization`:** more strongly and more precisely supported than the mechanism-refinement memo anticipated. Boston's contract does not merely make automation *plausible* as a headcount-reduction mechanism (the memo's original framing); it contains an explicit, doubly-stated contractual rule that technology-driven duty changes cannot be used to justify a reclassification-based pay increase — a direct, textual restraint mechanism, not just an inferred one. The BCYF/DPW-Transportation merger language is direct evidence that reorganization/consolidation is a live, real, currently-operating process for this unit, not merely hypothesized.
- **`non_safety_wage_restraint` (H17):** indirectly strengthened by the same interest-arbitration-scope finding already established for teachers and DPW (Section 6/Task D below): Boston's CGA arbitration is explicitly limited to an "arbitrary or capricious" standard, a materially narrower remedy than either JLMC's compulsory interest arbitration or even ordinary grievance arbitration over contract interpretation.

## 6. Implications for future GABRIEL/source extraction

**Signals confirmed present in this existing corpus** (safe to design an extraction attribute around): reclassification/Compensation-Grade-Appeal application-and-review-process language, including the specific procedural steps and the narrow "arbitrary or capricious" arbitration standard; explicit exclusion-of-workload/technology-as-reclassification-grounds language; management-rights-to-reclassify/reorganize language; merger/consolidation language with attached impact-bargaining obligations; civil-service-linked bumping/layoff/classification language; longevity-schedule language; and credential-tied stipend language (the CDL stipend example, even though it appears in a nominally clerical/admin unit).

**Signals that remain source gaps** (not found in this existing corpus, so any future extraction attribute targeting them would need new source acquisition): pay-compression, minimum-wage-compliance, and internal-equity language; workload/backlog/reduced-hours/cross-training/shared-services language; recruitment/retention framing language; hard-to-fill classification language; peer-community/surrounding-town wage comparability language; and vacancy-duration or applicant-count data.

A clerical/admin-specific GABRIEL/source-extraction attribute should not be designed yet; the textual-signal list in Section 6 is a starting point for that design only once the project is ready to prototype a non-safety attribute more broadly, which should follow, not precede, the recommended national qualitative scan.

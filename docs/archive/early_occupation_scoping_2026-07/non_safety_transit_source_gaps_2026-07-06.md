# Transit Source Gaps — 2026-07-06

**Type:** dated source-gap memo. Companion to `non_safety_transit_governance_mechanism_scope_2026-07-06.md`. Tracks what remains unknown or unacquired for transit as a potential comparison group, and how hard each gap would be to close.

Scope note: none of the gaps below authorizes ingestion. Every source named is a target for a future, separately-authorized acquisition decision, not a document to collect now.

## 1. City-operated vs. regional/state/private governance

**Why it matters:** this is transit's central, load-bearing question for this project's design. If any of this project's nine cities directly employed its own municipal transit workforce, that workforce could in principle be collected as an ordinary `transit`-classified row under the existing schema, no different from any other occupation class. This scan found zero such cases.

**Likely source families:** city/town charter and organizational-chart materials; MBTA/RTA enabling statutes (M.G.L. c. 161A, c. 161B); each authority's own "about us" and service-area pages.

**Feasibility:** desk-research feasible — this scan already closed this question for all nine of this project's current cities (all state-authority or regional-authority governed, none city-operated). Confirming it for any additional city beyond these nine would also be desk-research feasible.

**Gap category:** governance/institutional, foundational to every other gap below.

## 2. Operator vacancy/turnover evidence

**Why it matters:** the MBTA's 2024 wage-raise-then-hiring-surge finding (bus-operator starting pay raised from $22.21 to $30/hour, followed by a reported 365% increase in applications) is the sharpest quantified wage-to-staffing link this scan identified for any occupation group in this project to date, safety included. Confirming whether this pattern holds at any RTA (not just the MBTA) would meaningfully strengthen or qualify it.

**Likely source families:** MBTA/RTA board packets and annual reports; news coverage of specific hiring campaigns; a national transit-workforce survey (e.g., APTA), not yet identified with a specific citation this session.

**Feasibility:** desk-research feasible for a second MBTA-adjacent data point (more MBTA-specific hiring/wage news); likely_hard for RTA-specific vacancy/turnover data, since RTA operating contractors' internal HR data is not typically published externally, mirroring the `likely_hard` HR-vacancy-data gap already flagged for every other occupation group in this project (checklist §11).

**Gap category:** wage levels, staffing supply, operator shortages.

## 3. Canceled trips/missed service evidence

**Why it matters:** would let a future analysis test whether a documented missed-trip or service-cancellation event ever converts into a wage-relevant response (overtime, a new hiring initiative, a route redesign), the transit analog to sanitation's untested route-coverage hypothesis (SN01/H32).

**Likely source families:** MBTA/RTA service-alert archives; news coverage (the November 2025 MBTA Green Line suspension identified this session is one concrete, recent example); transit-authority board packets.

**Feasibility:** desk-research feasible for identifying specific disruption events; data-build feasible (not attempted this session) for a systematic count of disruption frequency; likely_hard to link any specific disruption causally to a wage or staffing-policy change without a dedicated, document-by-document review.

**Gap category:** route/service coverage, service cuts.

## 4. Operator assault/safety evidence

**Why it matters:** public-facing conflict and safety-sensitive work are named task-brief mechanisms; this scan did not independently investigate assault or safety-incident data for any of this project's nine cities' transit entities.

**Likely source families:** MBTA/RTA safety reports (this session's search results identified a WRTA "Public Transportation Agency Safety Plan" document exists, though its contents were not reviewed); national transit-safety data (e.g., FTA National Transit Database); news coverage of specific incidents.

**Feasibility:** desk-research feasible for locating existing safety-plan documents; data-build feasible for a systematic incident count; not attempted this session, consistent with the bounded scope of this run.

**Gap category:** safety, public salience.

## 5. CDL/commercial driving requirements

**Why it matters:** bus/paratransit operation nationally requires a commercial driver's license with passenger endorsement, a direct structural parallel to DPW's CDL-linked classification/stipend mechanism (H19) and sanitation's CDL-driver discussion (SN03); whether any Massachusetts transit entity ties CDL possession to a distinct pay tier (the way Franklin's DPW CBA does for CDL/hoisting/water-license credentials) remains untested, since no transit CBA is in this project's corpus.

**Likely source families:** a future transit-adjacent CBA (MBTA ATU Local 589; CMTM/First Transit's ATU Local 22 agreement at WRTA); Massachusetts RMV commercial-license administrative pages.

**Feasibility:** desk-research feasible for the general CDL/passenger-endorsement requirement (already well-established, general knowledge, not independently re-verified with a fresh citation this session); data-build feasible only once a specific transit CBA is acquired and reviewed for classification-tied pay language.

**Gap category:** wage levels, classification.

## 6. Split shifts/scheduling burden

**Why it matters:** split-shift and irregular scheduling are commonly reported nationally for bus operators; confirming whether this appears as an explicit, bargained scheduling provision (e.g., "run," "pick," or "board" language, per transit-industry labor-agreement convention) in any Massachusetts transit CBA would be a genuinely new mechanism finding for this project.

**Likely source families:** a future transit-adjacent CBA (none currently collected); transit-industry labor-relations reference materials describing standard scheduling-clause conventions.

**Feasibility:** likely_hard without acquiring an actual transit CBA; not testable from any document currently in this project's corpus or from general web search alone.

**Gap category:** scheduling, staffing.

## 7. Overtime evidence

**Why it matters:** fixed-route transit service requires continuous coverage, plausibly generating an overtime/staffing-spiral dynamic similar to what this project has already confirmed for DPW (XC03) and expects, but has not yet confirmed, for police/fire; untested for any transit entity in this scan.

**Likely source families:** a future transit-adjacent CBA; transit-authority payroll/budget reports.

**Feasibility:** likely_hard without acquiring an actual transit CBA or payroll dataset; not attempted this session.

**Gap category:** overtime, staffing.

## 8. Mechanic/maintenance skill shortages

**Why it matters:** MBTA's ATU Local 589 explicitly includes maintenance workers alongside operators; a CDL/skilled-trades-adjacent credentialing and possible scarcity story, structurally close to DPW's mechanic-classification mechanisms (DP09, DP10), untested for any RTA in this scan.

**Likely source families:** a future transit-adjacent CBA; trade-press reporting on transit-maintenance workforce trends (not identified with a specific citation this session).

**Feasibility:** desk-research feasible for general national context (not pursued in depth this session, per the bounded-scope instruction against comprehensive national transit-labor coverage); data-build feasible only once a specific transit CBA is reviewed.

**Gap category:** maintenance skills, staffing.

## 9. Contractor/vendor arrangements

**Why it matters:** this is the single best-evidenced gap this scan closed, not opened — WRTA's operating-contractor identity (First Transit, Inc., incorporated in Massachusetts as Central Mass Transit Management) was directly confirmed this session. Whether the same private-operator model applies with the same specific contractor identity to GATRA, MeVa, and MWRTA remains unconfirmed.

**Likely source families:** each RTA's own procurement/contract-award records; each RTA's own "about us" or governance pages; Mass.gov's RTA-oversight materials (the Mass.gov "Overview of the [RTA name]" page format, already used for MeVa this session, appears to exist for other RTAs too and would be a natural next stop).

**Feasibility:** desk-research feasible — a short, targeted follow-up search per RTA (GATRA, MeVa, MWRTA) could likely resolve this gap without any new tooling or ingestion.

**Gap category:** contracting, wage levels (since the private-operator identity determines which labor-law regime, and which specific CBA, would govern any future wage data for that RTA's workforce).

## 10. Farebox/subsidy/operating budget constraints

**Why it matters:** WRTA's fare-free policy (in place since March 2020) is a genuinely distinct fiscal-capacity story from any municipal tax-levy constraint this project has documented for police/fire, DPW, or teachers; whether a similar or different subsidy/farebox structure applies at MBTA, GATRA, MeVa, or MWRTA, and whether any of them ties operating-budget constraints to a documented wage-setting consequence, remains untested.

**Likely source families:** each authority's own annual/operating budget documents; state RTA-funding-formula materials (not reviewed this session); MBTA's own capital/operating budget materials.

**Feasibility:** data-build feasible for a systematic multi-authority budget-structure comparison; not attempted this session, consistent with the bounded scope of this run and the explicit instruction not to build any wage-trend or OEWS-adjacent panel.

**Gap category:** budget capacity, fiscal constraint.

## 11. Service cuts/route redesign

**Why it matters:** the MBTA's own ongoing "Bus Network Redesign" project (identified this session as actively restructuring routes serving Arlington and elsewhere) is a concrete, current example of large-scale route/schedule restructuring; whether such redesigns are ever explicitly tied to a budget or staffing rationale, and whether they have any documented wage-adjacent consequence, remains untested.

**Likely source families:** MBTA Bus Network Redesign project materials; RTA board packets describing any comparable local redesign effort.

**Feasibility:** desk-research feasible for identifying redesign projects (already partially done this session for the MBTA); likely_hard to link any specific redesign decision to a wage or staffing outcome without a dedicated review.

**Gap category:** service cuts, route/service coverage.

## 12. Whether transit workers are observable in municipal CBAs

**Why it matters:** this is the master question this entire scan was designed to answer, and the answer is now closed for this project's nine current cities: no. Every city's transit governance sits outside its own municipal government, via either the MBTA (state authority) or a regional transit authority whose operating workforce is, per M.G.L. c. 161B and this session's WRTA-specific confirmation, employed by a private operating company. This is a structural, not merely an unexplored, absence.

**Likely source families:** already closed this session via `non_safety_transit_governance_mechanism_scope_2026-07-06.md` and its companion CSV; no further desk research needed to re-answer this specific question for these nine cities.

**Feasibility:** closed — desk-research complete for this project's current city set.

**Gap category:** wage levels, contracting/governance (the master gap underlying every other gap above).

## 13. Massachusetts examples vs. national context

**Why it matters:** this memo and its companion memo have been careful to keep Massachusetts-specific findings (the MBTA's c. 161A arbitration statute; the RTA private-operator model under c. 161B; WRTA's specific fare-free policy and CMTM contractor identity) analytically separate from bounded national context (general post-pandemic transit-operator staffing-shortage reporting; the general national pattern of RTA-style private-operator contracting, which First Transit's own multi-state operations suggest is not a Massachusetts-only practice). Confirming how far the Massachusetts-specific c. 161A/c. 161B institutional model generalizes to other states' transit-governance structures was explicitly out of scope for this bounded session (per the task's instruction not to attempt comprehensive national transit-labor coverage) and remains an open question for any future, more expansive national-context memo.

**Likely source families:** a future national municipal/transit workforce scan, mirroring the existing `national_municipal_workforce_mechanism_scan_2026-07-05.md` pattern but transit-specific; American Public Transportation Association (APTA) materials; Federal Transit Administration background materials.

**Feasibility:** data-build/desk-research feasible for a future, dedicated national transit-governance scan; explicitly not attempted in this session, consistent with the task's scope boundary.

**Gap category:** all categories above, at the generalizability level rather than the Massachusetts-specific level.

# Police and Fire Wage Mechanism Synthesis for PI Review

**Date:** 2026-07-03  
**Purpose:** short PI-facing synthesis based on the current mechanism-context memo and source QC pass  
**Scope:** planning memo only; no GABRIEL run, no ingestion, no causal claim

## 1. Why we are stepping back from a single comparability hypothesis

The current project started from a sensible mechanism candidate: police and fire wages may rise faster partly because wage setting in those units leans heavily on peer-jurisdiction comparability. The existing corpus gives that idea some descriptive support. In particular, the strongest explicit comparator reasoning in the current public corpus appears in safety-side award-style documents.

At the same time, the current evidence base is too narrow to treat comparability as the whole story. The main limitation is not just sample size. It is that the current corpus is still Massachusetts-heavy, still small, and still shaped by source-type asymmetry. The clearest reasoning documents are disproportionately police/fire arbitration or impasse materials, while many non-safety rows are ordinary CBAs that preserve outcomes more than bargaining logic. That means a high `comparability_emphasis` score may reflect how some safety wage-setting processes are documented, not only how wages are actually determined.

For that reason, it is useful to step back from a single comparability hypothesis and treat comparability as one candidate mechanism inside a larger menu. This does not weaken the project. It clarifies it. The empirical question is not simply whether police/fire documents mention comparators. It is whether police and fire face a broader set of labor-market, institutional, operational, and political conditions that can generate different wage pressure than other municipal occupations.

## 2. Main candidate mechanisms

The current mechanism map suggests at least six broad families.

First, labor scarcity and recruitment/retention pressure. Police and fire may face hiring constraints that are more persistent or more operationally costly than those in some comparison units. This could reflect applicant shortages, retirements, resignations, slow hiring pipelines, qualification screens, or competition from alternative employers.

Second, compensating differentials for hazard, stress, and working time. Public-safety work often combines physical risk, trauma exposure, irregular schedules, and round-the-clock coverage. These burdens may affect wage setting directly, or indirectly through retention and overtime.

Third, overtime and staffing spirals. If police or fire staffing falls below operational needs, employers may substitute overtime and callback. That can raise realized compensation, worsen burnout, and create pressure for higher negotiated pay or stronger staffing protections.

Fourth, comparator ratchets. Police/fire bargaining may rely heavily on peer-city comparisons, parity claims, or regional wage ladders. This remains a live mechanism, but it needs to be separated from the fact that arbitration and impasse documents tend to record comparator logic more explicitly than ordinary contracts do.

Fifth, arbitration and institutional backstops. Police and fire may bargain under stronger impasse-resolution regimes, including interest arbitration, JLMC-style processes, mediation, factfinding, or bargaining in the shadow of those institutions. This may affect wages directly, or it may primarily affect how wage-setting reasoning gets documented.

Sixth, political salience, union leverage, and fiscal allocation. Elected officials may view visible public-safety deterioration as politically costly. At the same time, police/fire unions may operate under favorable institutional arrangements, and richer municipalities may choose to allocate more fiscal capacity to safety units than to other units.

Two additional mechanisms matter especially for fire. Fire departments sit at the intersection of career and volunteer systems, and firefighter work often includes EMS burdens plus credentialing requirements that do not necessarily show up as high median base wages. Weakening volunteer pipelines may also push some jurisdictions toward more paid coverage, changing budgets or staffing models before visibly changing contract wages.

## 3. What evidence would distinguish these mechanisms

The most important analytical move is to ask what evidence would separate one mechanism from another.

If recruitment pressure is the main story, we should see evidence such as vacancies, longer time-to-fill, lower applicants-per-opening, failed academy classes, retention problems, or more aggressive hiring incentives. If instead applicant pools remain large, the issue may be processing, standards, or local institutional bottlenecks rather than compensation.

If hazard and work burden are the main story, we should see the burden translated into bargaining language, schedule provisions, retention outcomes, or compensation structures. Otherwise, risk may be important as job context without being the main driver of wage growth.

If overtime spirals matter, vacancy and leave strain should track overtime use, callback dependence, and perhaps burnout or exits. If overtime is merely a symptom, then it should rise without necessarily changing the wage-setting logic.

If comparability is central, explicit peer-jurisdiction wage reasoning should appear in mechanism-rich documents, and ideally the comparator references should line up with observed wage patterns across nearby jurisdictions. If comparability language appears mainly where arbitration creates a written record, then the project may be observing document production rather than the full causal mechanism.

If arbitration backstops matter, we should see a difference between bargaining environments with and without strong impasse institutions, or between otherwise similar settings where wage disputes do and do not move into award-style processes. If arbitration mainly changes documentation, then it may inflate visible comparator language without being the dominant cause of wage outcomes.

If political salience or union leverage matters, the evidence should appear in budget choices, public bargaining narratives, statutory arrangements, or settlement patterns, not just in abstract statements about safety being important.

## 4. Why police and fire may differ

Police and fire should not be treated as a single labor market with two labels.

Police wage pressure may be more exposed to post-2020 legitimacy conflict, public scrutiny, morale narratives, retirement waves, and competition from other law-enforcement employers. Police also face operational burdens such as court time, patrol coverage, and certain liability exposures that may create distinctive staffing stress.

Fire may differ in several ways. Public esteem does not necessarily produce abundant qualified supply at the career margin. Firefighter work often combines long shifts, injury risk, protective gear, and a large EMS component. But firefighter median pay is still nationally lower than police median pay, which suggests that training and credential burdens do not map mechanically into higher wages. Some fire-side pressure may instead operate through overtime, minimum staffing, volunteer recruitment/retention, or transitions from volunteer to more paid coverage.

This distinction matters for the project. A mechanism that helps explain police need not explain fire, and vice versa. Treating them separately may improve both the conceptual model and the eventual measurement strategy.

## 5. Why non-safety comparison is essential

The non-safety comparison is not optional. It is the core discipline that prevents the project from relabeling general municipal labor-market problems as public-safety mechanisms.

Vacancies, overtime, training burdens, turnover, and political salience also exist in non-safety units. Teachers, nurses, transit workers, sanitation crews, dispatchers, and public works staff can all face hard schedules and service constraints. The key question is whether police and fire face these pressures differently, more persistently, or under institutions that make their wage consequences stronger.

This is why within-city comparison remains so important. The project is strongest when it asks whether police/fire wage-setting differs from non-safety wage-setting while holding place and time roughly fixed. Mechanism claims should therefore be written comparatively from the start. For example, the right question is not "do absences matter in public safety?" but "are absences more operationally costly, less deferrable, or more politically salient in police/fire than in comparison units in the same city and period?"

## 6. Recommended next empirical steps

The next step should remain mechanism development, not a scaled model run.

First, do a careful descriptive wage-data pull from BLS OEWS and Census ASPEP. This will not produce bargaining-unit wages, but it can establish a broad national/state/metro descriptive baseline and clarify what public functional payroll context is available.

Second, tighten the source base behind the mechanism memo. In particular, replace illustrative secondary items where possible with exact primary citations, and clean up citation ambiguity for pages that are really portals or landing pages rather than year-specific outputs.

Third, build a small broader-state snippet set for mechanism discovery. Massachusetts and JLMC should remain one institutional case, but not the whole conceptual frame. The goal is to see whether other states surface different combinations of comparability, staffing strain, arbitration rules, political salience, or volunteer-to-career fire pressure.

Fourth, only after that refinement should the project decide which new GABRIEL attributes are worth prototyping. The candidate list now includes recruitment/retention pressure, overtime/staffing pressure, hazard or working conditions, training/credentialing burden, public-safety service necessity, arbitration or impasse backstop, comparability emphasis, fiscal capacity/ability to pay, political salience, union leverage, and post-2020 policing climate.

The core recommendation is simple: do not force the project into a single-mechanism interpretation too early. Comparability remains important, but the better PI-facing framing is that police/fire wage growth may reflect multiple interacting mechanisms, and the next empirical task is to distinguish among them rather than to assume the answer in advance.

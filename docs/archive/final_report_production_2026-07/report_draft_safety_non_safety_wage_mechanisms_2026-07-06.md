# Deeper Look Into Safety & Non-safety Wage Mechanisms
## Mechanisms, Counterpoints, and Source Needs Across Municipal Occupations

*Working evidence map — internal research draft. Not a final causal analysis.*

---

## 1. Executive Summary

This report synthesizes the current state of a research effort examining why public-safety wages (police and fire) may rise faster than other municipal occupations, using a matched-city, cross-occupation comparison design. The project has now developed mechanism maps and reviewed available evidence for eleven occupation groups: police, fire, teachers, DPW/public works, clerical/administrative staff, library workers, custodial/facilities staff, dispatchers, nurse/public-health staff, sanitation workers, and transit workers. This document is a working evidence map, not a finished causal analysis — it organizes what the project's current corpus and bounded desk research can and cannot show, and it identifies what additional sourcing would be needed before any claim here could be stated more strongly.

The central finding to date is not that public-safety work is simply harder, more dangerous, or more essential than other municipal work. Several non-safety occupations examined in this project show real, evidenced pressures of their own: documented national teacher shortages, a genuine credential-to-pay linkage for DPW's licensed trades and for custodial/facilities maintenance staff, a fatal-injury rate for sanitation work that recent national data places above police's own, and a 24/7 minimum-staffing obligation for municipal dispatchers that closely resembles police and fire's own coverage requirements. What distinguishes police and fire in this project's evidence so far is not the presence of pressure, but the presence of a distinctive institutional channel that converts that pressure into wages: Massachusetts's Joint Labor Management Committee (JLMC), which orders compulsory, binding interest arbitration for police and fire specifically, unlike any other occupation group this project has reviewed. Every other group's pressures instead appear to move through different, generally weaker or more indirect channels — administrative classification systems, service buffering, contractor substitution, governance structures that place the workforce outside municipal control entirely, or a voluntary, advisory bargaining-impasse process rather than a compulsory one.

This report should be read as a status report on evidence quality as much as a summary of findings. The underlying corpus is currently 32 rows, drawn entirely from nine Massachusetts municipalities, and several occupation groups are represented by a single document or a single city. Findings are therefore graded explicitly throughout this report: some are grounded directly in already-collected contract text, some rest on bounded external or national research, and some are flagged as requiring further sourcing before they can support a stronger claim. Readers should treat "current corpus shows" and "external or national context suggests" as materially different levels of evidentiary weight, and should not read any finding in this report as evidence of a causal relationship between an occupation's characteristics and its wage outcomes.

Occupation groups are grouped here by their current standing in the project rather than by a single ranking. Police, fire, teachers, and DPW/public works are treated as central comparison groups, each with either extensive corpus documentation or a well-developed external evidence base. Clerical/administrative staff, library workers, and custodial/facilities staff are treated as strong comparison groups with solid, if more limited, corpus support. Dispatchers occupy a distinct public-safety-adjacent category, reflecting their civilian bargaining status alongside an operational profile that closely resembles sworn public-safety work. Nurse/public-health staff are treated as a secondary finding — genuinely evidenced but resting on a single city's contract. Sanitation workers illustrate an important source-design problem: the function is contracted to private haulers in a majority of this project's cities, which structurally limits what a municipal-contract-based corpus can show. Transit is deferred entirely as a direct city-level comparison, because transit service in every city this project has examined is governed by a state authority or a regional authority whose workforce is not municipally employed.

## 2. Main Takeaways

1. **Police and fire combine real upward wage pressure with a stronger pressure-to-wage conversion institution than any other group examined.** The Joint Labor Management Committee's compulsory interest arbitration is confirmed, across every police and fire document in this project's corpus, as a mechanism with no equivalent for any non-safety group reviewed to date.

2. **Non-safety groups show real pressures of their own — the difference is not "pressure exists vs. does not."** Documented findings include DPW's licensed-trades credential-to-pay linkage, custodial/facilities' comparable maintenance-license pay differential, library's uneven professional-credential stipend, and sanitation's national hazard-rate comparison to police.

3. **Collective bargaining agreements show mechanics more than wage-setting reasoning.** Across every occupation group with a substantial corpus presence, contract text is consistently rich in classification schedules, overtime rules, and management-rights language, but consistently thin on explicit recruitment-difficulty or peer-community wage-comparison language.

4. **Arbitration, factfinding, and JLMC-style award documents are a materially better source for explicit wage-setting reasoning than ordinary contracts.** The only documents in this project's corpus that state peer-community wage comparisons and ability-to-pay reasoning directly are the two Somerville JLMC arbitration awards, precisely because the enabling statute requires an arbitrator to weigh and write about those criteria.

5. **Contracted-out or regionally governed services can disappear from a municipal-contract corpus entirely.** Sanitation service is privately contracted in a majority of this project's own cities, and transit service in all nine of this project's cities sits under a state authority or a regional authority whose workforce is not a municipal employer's own — both are design-fit limitations, not simply gaps to be filled by collecting more of the same document type.

6. **Public esteem, physical danger, professional credentialing, and service essentiality do not automatically translate into wage power.** This project's own evidence includes a credentialed occupation with an uneven wage stipend (library's MLS/MLIS pay), a hazardous occupation with no confirmed wage premium in any collected document (sanitation), and a nationally unresolved debate over whether a genuinely public-safety-adjacent occupation (dispatch) should even be classified as protective-service work.

7. **Institutional findings are more numerous and more precise than a simple police/fire-versus-everyone-else binary.** This project has now identified three distinct Massachusetts wage-impasse regimes — JLMC for police/fire, the ordinary Chapter 150E process for most other municipal employees, and a separate compulsory-arbitration statute (M.G.L. c. 161A) specific to the Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority's own employees.

8. **Meaningful source needs remain before any of these findings can support a stronger claim.** Several groups rest on a single city, a single document, or bounded national context rather than a matched, multi-city evidence base; a structured list of source needs, organized by priority, follows in Section 7.

## 4. Group-by-Group Findings

### Police

**Status:** Central.

**Main wage-pressure mechanisms:** Recruitment and retention strain is documented, though comparative national survey data places policing as a real but not uniquely severe local-government staffing challenge. Overtime and minimum-staffing dynamics, hazard and credentialing burdens, and political salience around visible public-safety failures are all plausible upward-pressure channels this project's corpus and prior mechanism work have developed.

**Restraint/buffering mechanisms:** None specific to police in this project's evidence — police sits on the upward-pressure side of the project's overall framing, without an identified restraint channel comparable to those found for non-safety groups.

**Current evidence basis:** Current corpus shows JLMC compulsory arbitration cited directly in every collected police contract's statutory reference field, and two full arbitration award documents (from Somerville) contain explicit peer-community wage-comparator language, since the enabling statute directs arbitrators to weigh and record that reasoning.

**Massachusetts-specific institutional notes:** JLMC, established under Chapter 589 of the Acts of 1987, orders compulsory binding interest arbitration for police and fire bargaining impasses specifically.

**National-generalizability caveat:** A citation-audited review confirms comparable police/fire compulsory-arbitration statutes exist in several other states, but this is a recurring pattern, not a majority-of-states finding, and each state's specific vehicle differs from Massachusetts's own.

**Source needs:** A direct, non-secondary staffing-survey citation with a multi-year applicant trend; minimum-staffing clause text from this project's own collected police contracts.

### Fire

**Status:** Central.

**Main wage-pressure mechanisms:** EMS/paramedic credential growth is documented directly in one city's contract as a pay stipend. A national, quantified decline in volunteer firefighting is documented in external sources and is understood to create indirect pressure toward paid or combination staffing over time, though this transmission is expected to be lagged rather than immediate.

**Restraint/buffering mechanisms:** The career-versus-volunteer structural split itself functions as a restraint on treating "firefighter wages" as a single national figure, since volunteer departments have no negotiated base wage to observe at all.

**Current evidence basis:** Current corpus confirms the same JLMC backstop as police in every collected fire contract, and a full JLMC stipulated arbitration award (from Wayland) exists in the corpus, though its comparator-specific language has not yet been extracted in detail.

**Massachusetts-specific institutional notes:** Same JLMC finding as police.

**National-generalizability caveat:** Most identified multi-state compulsory-arbitration statutes cover police and fire jointly, though at least one state's statute (Wyoming) applies to fire only, with no comparable police statute identified.

**Source needs:** Detailed comparator-language extraction from the collected JLMC award; EMS credential-scarcity evidence beyond the single city currently documented.

### Teachers / school employees

**Status:** Central.

**Main wage-pressure mechanisms:** National data documents real hiring difficulty (a majority of surveyed schools reporting difficulty filling at least one vacancy in the most recent reporting cycle), concentrated in specific subject areas such as special education and STEM fields. At least one state (Iowa) is documented, via primary statutory text, to extend compulsory arbitration to teacher bargaining, unlike Massachusetts.

**Restraint/buffering mechanisms:** Step-and-lane salary schedules do not automatically differentiate pay by subject-area shortage absent a deliberate add-on; national data suggests most, but not all, surveyed districts add some form of shortage-area incentive. Shortage pressure is more likely absorbed through long-term substitutes, emergency certification, or larger class sizes than through base-wage growth — a buffering channel that is not wage-adjacent, unlike police and fire overtime.

**Current evidence basis:** Current corpus is thin for this group (one contract, from one city), confirmed to keep teacher aide positions outside the bargaining unit. The strongest evidence for this group comes from external national sources rather than from this project's own collected contract.

**Massachusetts-specific institutional notes:** Confirmed, via direct primary-source verification, that Massachusetts teacher bargaining follows the ordinary Chapter 150E Section 9 process — mediation and advisory fact-finding, with a voluntary arbitration route that exists on paper but is rarely used — rather than JLMC's compulsory route. A two-sided budget constraint (a state-mandated school-spending floor combined with the same municipal levy-limit ceiling other departments face) applies specifically to school budgets.

**National-generalizability caveat:** The general architecture behind Massachusetts's school-finance formula (a state spending floor filled by state aid after a local-capacity-based contribution) is confirmed as the dominant national model, but the specific Massachusetts parameters should not be assumed to transfer numerically to another state.

**Source needs:** Composition data (grade level, subject area, experience) for this project's specific cities, needed before any raw wage comparison to police or fire; direct review of the collected teacher contract for comparator language.

### DPW / public works

**Status:** Central.

**Main wage-pressure mechanisms:** A richly confirmed credential-to-pay linkage exists for commercial driving licenses, hoisting licenses, and water/wastewater operator certifications across multiple cities' contracts, with at least one city's contract paying a detailed, tiered stipend schedule across several distinct trade credentials. A national, regionally documented retirement wave among certified water/wastewater operators is understood as the strongest genuine credential-scarcity case this project has identified for any non-safety group, though no direct vacancy data confirms scarcity at the level of any specific city's own workforce.

**Restraint/buffering mechanisms:** Contractor substitution is confirmed across nearly every collected contract, ranging from an unconditional management right in one city to heavily conditioned, time-bounded arrangements in another. Overtime, rather than base-wage growth, is the primary contractual mechanism for storm and emergency response.

**Current evidence basis:** Current corpus is the richest non-safety corpus in this project — seven contracts across five cities, each reviewed in full. Explicit recruitment-difficulty language and peer-community wage-comparability language are both confirmed absent from every document reviewed, a genuine finding rather than an unexplored gap.

**Massachusetts-specific institutional notes:** Confirmed, via every collected contract's statutory-reference field and via a separate institutional review, that DPW bargaining follows the ordinary Chapter 150E route, with every arbitration clause found being grievance-scoped rather than wage-setting. Massachusetts's prevailing-wage law is understood to complicate a simple "contracting out suppresses wages" reading, since covered contracted work must still be paid at locally bargained rates.

**National-generalizability caveat:** A national commercial-driver "shortage" narrative is directly contradicted by federal labor-market analysis showing no evidence of a secular shortage and largely flat real driver wages since 2010 — this caution should be read into any future discussion of DPW's or any other group's commercial-driving-credential mechanism.

**Source needs:** Actual vacancy or applicant data for any DPW classification in this project's cities; a same-city comparison of DPW overtime spending against police and fire overtime spending.

### Clerical / administrative

**Status:** Strong comparison.

**Main wage-pressure mechanisms:** General labor-market and cost-of-living pressure shared with most municipal occupations; a confirmed reclassification-request channel through which a specific title's pay concern can be raised outside ordinary bargaining.

**Restraint/buffering mechanisms:** One city's contract documents the most procedurally restrictive internal classification-appeal process found anywhere in this project's corpus, limiting any arbitrator's review to a narrow standard and explicitly excluding workload or technology-driven duty changes as valid grounds for a reclassification. A documented, multi-state minimum-wage-driven compression pressure exists at a modest estimated cost share of payroll, though this project's own corpus does not yet span enough contract cycles per city to test a within-city compression trend directly.

**Current evidence basis:** Current corpus is complete for this group (three contracts, fully reviewed), though richness is concentrated almost entirely in one city's document; the other two cities' documents are comparatively thin. Peer-community wage-comparability language and pay-compression language are both confirmed absent from all three documents.

**Massachusetts-specific institutional notes:** Confirmed, via a municipal association's own guidance, that clerical and administrative employees are explicitly ineligible for JLMC services — the most direct confirmation of this pattern found for any non-safety group. A newly identified institutional wrinkle: certain administrative positions closest to municipal executive leadership are excluded from union membership entirely, with pay set unilaterally rather than through any bargaining process.

**National-generalizability caveat:** The general architecture of a formal classification-and-appeal system is confirmed as a national civil-service norm, but whether the specific restrictiveness found in this project's one richly-documented city is typical or unusual elsewhere has not been tested.

**Source needs:** Outcome data for reclassification requests (approval rates, dollar impact) — internal administrative data not published in contract text.

### Library

**Status:** Strong comparison.

**Main wage-pressure mechanisms:** A professional library-degree credential is confirmed to carry a pay stipend in one of this project's three library-collecting cities, absent in the other two — direct evidence that credentialing does not automatically translate into a uniform wage premium even within this project's own small sample.

**Restraint/buffering mechanisms:** Part-time and page-level staffing function as a structural, permanent bargaining-unit category in every collected library contract, distinct from an occasional buffering response. A volunteer-labor-substitution hypothesis is directly contradicted, not confirmed, by the strongest available evidence: one city's contract explicitly bars replacing bargaining-unit hours with volunteer labor without union agreement.

**Current evidence basis:** Current corpus is complete for this group (three contracts, fully reviewed, including one document requiring an image-to-text conversion pass). Peer-community wage-comparability language is confirmed absent across all three documents, consistent with the same finding already documented for DPW and clerical/administrative staff.

**Massachusetts-specific institutional notes:** Confirmed, via the same municipal-association guidance cited for clerical/administrative staff, that library employees follow the ordinary Chapter 150E route. A distinct, library-specific budget-floor mechanism exists (a state library-agency requirement that municipal library appropriations not fall meaningfully below a rolling three-year average), a weaker and waivable analog to the teacher budget-floor mechanism.

**National-generalizability caveat:** No national comparison was performed for this group beyond the sources already cited in the underlying mechanism memo; national public-library staffing and compensation data exist but have not been benchmarked against this project's specific cities.

**Source needs:** Vacancy and turnover data, and staffing-composition data (professional librarian versus assistant versus page headcount), for this project's three library cities.

### Custodial / facilities

**Status:** Strong comparison.

**Main wage-pressure mechanisms:** A confirmed, direct pay differential exists between licensed and unlicensed maintenance classifications in one city's contract — a close parallel to DPW's credential-to-pay linkage. A parallel to police officers' paid-detail income exists in another city's contract, where custodial staff working a vendor-paid detail assignment receive a premium rate.

**Restraint/buffering mechanisms:** Outsourcing rights exist in every collected custodial contract, but in each case are conditioned — limited to emergencies, or explicitly barred from replacing routine duties or from being used to avoid overtime, rather than an unconditional management prerogative.

**Current evidence basis:** Current corpus now includes two complete, multi-step salary schedules with genuine classification tiers, plus supplementary bundled evidence (a dedicated vacancy-procedure appendix, shift-premium pay, and a narrow, task-specific outsourcing clause) from a third city where custodial staff are bundled into a broader municipal bargaining unit rather than organized as their own unit.

**Massachusetts-specific institutional notes:** All three cities' custodial contracts cite the ordinary Chapter 150E route, consistent with every other non-safety group.

**National-generalizability caveat:** No national custodial/facilities-specific labor-market data was reviewed for this group; the two confirmed salary schedules are Massachusetts-specific findings.

**Source needs:** None outstanding for the core evidentiary question — this group's evidentiary base is now considered largely complete pending a formal classification decision so its two fully-documented contracts can be recorded under their own occupation category rather than a general one.

### Dispatchers

**Status:** Public-safety-adjacent (a distinct category, not merged into ordinary non-safety comparison or treated as safety-equivalent).

**Main wage-pressure mechanisms:** A confirmed, explicit minimum-staffing complement rule exists in one city's contract, requiring a defined number of dispatchers on duty at all times outside emergencies — a direct structural parallel to police and fire's own coverage-obligation logic. A second city's contract confirms a full wage-grade table for its dispatch classification, along with shift-differential pay and a state-administered emergency-dispatch certification requirement.

**Restraint/buffering mechanisms:** Dispatchers' civilian, non-sworn employment status may cap wage translation of genuinely public-safety-adjacent pressure below what a comparable sworn classification would receive — this is not a claim unique to this project; a national debate over whether 911 telecommunicators should be reclassified as protective-service or "first responder" workers is currently active and unresolved at the federal level, with a majority of states having taken some reclassification action while the underlying federal occupational classification has not changed.

**Current evidence basis:** Current corpus confirms dispatch-specific contract language in two cities. In neither case is a direct, same-document wage comparison between dispatcher pay and sworn police or fire pay fully available: one city's contract has no per-classification base-wage table at all (only unit-wide percentage increases, with actual base rates set in an external document not in this project's corpus), while the second city's dispatcher wage grade has not yet been cross-referenced against that same city's police or fire wage data.

**Massachusetts-specific institutional notes:** Confirmed directly, via the statutory-reference field in both dispatcher-bearing contracts, that dispatchers follow the ordinary Chapter 150E route rather than JLMC, despite their operational similarity to public-safety work. A state agency responsible for 911 services maintains grant and certification programs that may create a distinct regional-governance layer for dispatch operations in some cities, not yet tested against this project's own city set.

**National-generalizability caveat:** A recent multi-state legislative trend toward reclassifying telecommunicators as first responders is documented, alongside a federal decision not to make the equivalent occupational-classification change — this is an active, unresolved policy question, not a settled national pattern.

**Source needs:** A direct wage-tier comparison between dispatcher pay and sworn police/fire pay, same city, same tenure — the clearest remaining test of whether civilian status caps wage translation for this group.

### Nurse / public health

**Status:** Secondary.

**Main wage-pressure mechanisms:** A confirmed professional credential stipend exists for a graduate-level nursing or public-health degree in the one city where this occupation is documented. National workforce data documents real, though geographically uneven, nursing-shortage pressure concentrated more heavily outside metropolitan areas than within them.

**Restraint/buffering mechanisms:** A directly documented instance of administrative wage-setting neglect exists in this project's one nurse-health-bearing contract: the affected positions' wages are explicitly noted as excluded from a recent municipal compensation study due to their unusual, cross-departmental job structure.

**Current evidence basis:** Current corpus for this group rests entirely on one city's contract, in which public-health nursing positions are embedded within an unusually broad bargaining unit that also includes senior public-works management titles, clerical staff, dispatch personnel, and several other distinct professional and technical municipal roles.

**Massachusetts-specific institutional notes:** The same contract confirms the ordinary Chapter 150E route for this bargaining unit as a whole.

**National-generalizability caveat:** National nursing-shortage data is overwhelmingly generated from hospital-sector reporting and should not be assumed to describe a small municipal or school-based public-health nursing population without a distinct, population-specific source.

**Source needs:** Whether any other city in this project's set has a genuinely municipal (not hospital-affiliated, not purely school-embedded) public-health nursing bargaining unit.

### Sanitation / solid waste

**Status:** Source-design case.

**Main wage-pressure mechanisms:** National data, drawn from federal fatal-occupational-injury statistics as reported in trade press, places refuse and recycling collection's fatality rate above police's own in recent reporting years — the sharpest quantified hazard comparison identified anywhere in this project to date. Waste-industry trade reporting documents high driver turnover for commercial-driving roles in this sector, though this should be read with the same skepticism this project applies to any industry-association staffing narrative.

**Restraint/buffering mechanisms:** Private-hauler contracting is confirmed, via each city's own public information, as the residential collection arrangement in a majority (five of nine) of this project's current cities — a materially different, more complete form of contractor substitution than the seasonal or supplemental contractor use documented for DPW. A 2025 multi-city labor dispute involving a private hauler's unionized workforce illustrates that even where sanitation labor is unionized, it is typically organized as a private-sector, multi-city bargaining unit that does not fit this project's one-city-one-unit comparison design.

**Current evidence basis:** Current corpus holds zero sanitation-classified contracts. A re-read of an already-collected public-works contract from one city (undertaken specifically to test whether sanitation duties are folded into an existing job title) found no explicit sanitation-specific language, though two suggestive but unconfirmed signals were identified (a distinctly scheduled transfer-station function and a commercial-driving training-reimbursement benefit).

**Massachusetts-specific institutional notes:** No sanitation-specific bargaining-impasse institution has been identified; the ordinary Chapter 150E route is assumed to apply by extension from the pattern confirmed for every other non-safety group, though this has not been independently re-verified for sanitation specifically.

**National-generalizability caveat:** The hazard-rate comparison is drawn from national data and has not been verified against any Massachusetts-specific source; it should not be read as a Massachusetts-specific finding.

**Source needs:** Confirmation of whether any specific city's public-works staff perform collection duties without a distinct job title, and identification of a genuinely municipal (not privately contracted) sanitation workforce in any of this project's cities, before any further contract acquisition is pursued for this group.

### Transit

**Status:** Deferred as a direct city-level comparison.

**Main wage-pressure mechanisms:** A documented, substantial wage increase for one regional transit authority's bus operators in 2024 was followed by a large reported increase in job applications — the most directly quantified wage-to-staffing relationship identified anywhere in this project to date, though this describes a state authority's own workforce, not any of this project's city governments.

**Restraint/buffering mechanisms:** Transit service in every one of this project's nine cities is governed either by a state transit authority or by a regional transit authority whose operating workforce is, under state law, employed by a private management company rather than by the public authority itself — a structural, not merely administrative, removal of transit wage-setting from any of this project's city governments.

**Current evidence basis:** Current corpus holds zero transit-classified contracts, and none are expected to exist under the current research design, since no city in this project's current set operates its own municipal transit service.

**Massachusetts-specific institutional notes:** The regional transit authority whose governance structure was most directly examined confirms its operating workforce is employed by a private contractor, not the public authority. Separately, the Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority is confirmed to have its own distinct compulsory-arbitration statute for its own employees — a third Massachusetts wage-impasse regime, alongside JLMC and the ordinary Chapter 150E process, though one that applies to a state authority's employees rather than to any municipal government.

**National-generalizability caveat:** No comparison against other states' transit-governance structures was performed; the private-operator requirement and the state-authority arbitration statute are both confirmed as Massachusetts-specific institutional facts only.

**Source needs:** Not applicable at this stage — transit is deferred as a direct city-level comparison group given the governance mismatch described above; any future work would more appropriately treat transit as a separate institutional case study rather than a matched-city occupation.

## 5. Mechanism-by-Mechanism Synthesis

### Interest arbitration / impasse backstop

**What it means:** whether a bargaining impasse is resolved through a compulsory, binding third-party process, a voluntary process rarely invoked, or no formal process at all.

**Strongest groups:** police and fire, via JLMC's compulsory arbitration, confirmed directly in every collected contract's statutory citation.

**Counterpoints:** the underlying policy pattern (pairing a strike prohibition with compulsory arbitration for public safety) recurs in several other states, so it is not a uniquely Massachusetts phenomenon, though most states examined were not confirmed to have an equivalent statute. At least one state extends compulsory arbitration to teachers as well, a documented exception to the general non-safety pattern.

**What current evidence can show:** that every non-safety group examined so far (teachers, DPW, clerical/administrative staff, library workers, and by direct confirmation this session, dispatchers) follows the same ordinary, voluntary-arbitration route rather than JLMC's compulsory route.

**What evidence is still needed:** whether any additional Massachusetts occupation group has an undiscovered exception to this pattern; a systematic count of which other states extend compulsory arbitration beyond police and fire.

### Staffing shortages, recruitment, and retention

**What it means:** whether an occupation faces a documented difficulty filling open positions, and whether that difficulty is a volume problem (too few applicants) or a qualification problem (applicants lacking a required credential).

**Strongest groups:** teachers (national survey data), DPW's water and wastewater trades (a documented, regionally-confirmed retirement wave), and dispatchers (an explicit minimum-staffing rule implies persistent coverage pressure).

**Counterpoints:** comparative national survey data shows police staffing difficulty is real but not the most severe local-government staffing challenge — several other public-sector occupations rank as harder to fill in the same survey data.

**What current evidence can show:** that staffing pressure, where documented, is not unique to public safety, and that comparative survey data exists for several occupations at the national level.

**What evidence is still needed:** matched, same-city, same-period vacancy or applicant data across occupation classes — not currently available anywhere in this project's corpus for any group.

### Overtime and staffing spiral

**What it means:** whether understaffing converts into overtime spending, which raises realized compensation without necessarily changing negotiated base wages.

**Strongest groups:** DPW, via confirmed storm- and emergency-specific overtime rules across nearly every collected contract, and dispatchers, whose explicit minimum-coverage rule structurally implies an overtime dynamic whenever staffing falls below the required complement.

**Counterpoints:** DPW's contractor-substitution option makes its overtime dynamics structurally different from police and fire, which generally lack a comparable substitution option for core response duties.

**What current evidence can show:** that overtime, not base-wage growth, is the primary documented mechanism for DPW's storm and emergency response.

**What evidence is still needed:** minimum-staffing clause text from this project's own police and fire contracts, not yet directly reviewed; dispatcher-specific overtime spending data.

### Continuous coverage and minimum staffing

**What it means:** a contractual requirement that a minimum number of staff be present at all times, independent of ordinary overtime rules.

**Strongest groups:** dispatchers, via a confirmed, explicit rule requiring a specific minimum complement at all times outside emergencies — the clearest evidence in this project's non-safety-adjacent corpus of a police/fire-style coverage obligation.

**Counterpoints:** most non-safety groups examined (teachers, clerical/administrative staff, library workers) show no comparable continuous-coverage obligation.

**What current evidence can show:** that dispatchers' operational coverage requirements closely resemble the coverage logic already documented for police and fire.

**What evidence is still needed:** whether a comparable minimum-staffing obligation exists in any other city's dispatch contract, or in any other public-safety-adjacent function this project has not yet examined.

### Public salience and political pressure

**What it means:** whether elected officials face greater political pressure to maintain staffing or service levels in a visible occupation than in a less visible one.

**Strongest groups:** no group in this project has directly tested this mechanism with comparative data; it remains a framing hypothesis across every occupation-specific memo produced to date.

**Counterpoints:** salience appears to be function-dependent rather than uniformly tied to any one occupation — a routine clerical function may draw little attention while an election-administration failure in the same occupation class could draw substantial attention.

**What current evidence can show:** essentially nothing directly; this mechanism has not been tested against any comparative data source in this project.

**What evidence is still needed:** a comparative local-news-coverage-volume study across occupation classes and comparable-severity events — not attempted anywhere in this project to date, and dependent on a currently empty discourse corpus.

### Credentialing / licensing / certification

**What it means:** whether a required professional credential is tied to a distinct classification or pay premium, and whether that credential functions as a genuine labor-market scarcity signal or simply a hiring standard.

**Strongest groups:** DPW (commercial driving, hoisting, and water/wastewater licenses, richly confirmed with tiered stipend schedules in multiple cities) and custodial/facilities (a confirmed licensed-versus-unlicensed maintenance pay differential, a close parallel to DPW's finding).

**Counterpoints:** a national commercial-driver "shortage" narrative is directly contradicted by federal labor-market analysis; credentialing can confer a real classification consequence (as in DPW and custodial/facilities) without that meaning the underlying labor market is actually scarce.

**What current evidence can show:** that credential-to-pay linkage is a real, corpus-confirmed mechanism in at least two occupation groups; that a credential in a different occupation (library's MLS/MLIS) can exist without producing a comparable, consistent wage premium.

**What evidence is still needed:** vacancy-duration data comparing credentialed and non-credentialed positions within the same department.

### Classification / reclassification

**What it means:** a formal administrative process through which an individual title's pay grade can change outside ordinary across-the-board bargaining.

**Strongest groups:** clerical/administrative staff, via the most procedurally restrictive classification-appeal process found anywhere in this project's corpus in one city's contract, and DPW and library, both of which show comparable, if less restrictive, reclassification processes.

**Counterpoints:** classification systems of this kind are confirmed as a broadly national civil-service norm, not a distinctive feature of any one occupation or of Massachusetts specifically — the presence of a classification system is not itself occupation-specific evidence.

**What current evidence can show:** that at least one city's process is unusually narrow, explicitly excluding workload or technology-driven duty changes as valid reclassification grounds.

**What evidence is still needed:** reclassification-request outcome data (approval rates, dollar impact), which is internal administrative data not published in contract text; whether police or fire contracts contain a comparable classification-review process.

### Pay compression / internal equity

**What it means:** whether rising minimum wages or other external pressures compress the gap between entry-level and senior pay steps within an occupation's schedule.

**Strongest groups:** clerical/administrative staff, via a documented multi-state cost estimate that includes Massachusetts, though this project's own corpus does not yet span enough contract cycles per city to test a within-city compression trend directly.

**Counterpoints:** compression pressure could in principle be resolved by moving an entire pay schedule upward as a block, which would not represent wage restraint at all.

**What current evidence can show:** a general multi-state cost estimate; no city-specific compression trend has been demonstrated in this project's own corpus.

**What evidence is still needed:** multiple contract cycles per city, which the current single-cycle-per-city corpus does not support.

### Service deferral / buffering

**What it means:** whether staffing or budget pressure is absorbed through reduced or deferred service rather than through wage growth.

**Strongest groups:** teachers (documented reliance on long-term substitutes and emergency certification rather than base-wage growth), DPW (a documented, though not directly tested, service-deferral hypothesis around delayed infrastructure maintenance), and clerical/administrative staff (a documented, though CBA-text-invisible, service-backlog absorption channel).

**Counterpoints:** each occupation's buffering channel differs sharply in how closely it resembles a wage-adjacent cost — teacher buffering (substitute pay) is the least wage-adjacent, while DPW's overtime-based buffering is the most.

**What current evidence can show:** that distinct buffering channels exist and are documented, in different forms, across several non-safety groups.

**What evidence is still needed:** matched, same-city, same-period data linking a documented staffing-strain period to a specific buffering response — not supported by the current corpus for any group.

### Contractor substitution / outsourcing

**What it means:** whether a municipality substitutes private contractors for work that could otherwise be performed by its own employees, and under what conditions.

**Strongest groups:** DPW (confirmed across nearly every collected contract, ranging from an unconditional right to a heavily conditioned arrangement), sanitation (confirmed as the majority residential-collection arrangement across this project's own cities), and transit (confirmed as a state-law-mandated structure for regional transit authorities, not a discretionary choice).

**Counterpoints:** none of the three groups above show a comparable substitution clause in teacher, clerical/administrative, or library contracts — this appears to be a mechanism specific to operationally substitutable functions, not a universal municipal-contracting feature.

**What current evidence can show:** that contractor substitution operates along a spectrum from narrowly conditioned (most custodial/facilities and several DPW examples) to effectively unconditional (one city's general DPW contracting right) to structurally mandatory (transit's regional-authority model).

**What evidence is still needed:** custodial/facilities-specific outsourcing arrangements beyond the three cities already reviewed; confirmation of prevailing-wage-law coverage for specific contracted work types.

### Governance distance

**What it means:** whether the entity that actually sets an occupation's wages is the city government itself, or a state authority, regional authority, or private company operating under state-law requirements.

**Strongest groups:** transit, where every one of this project's nine cities' transit workforce sits under a state authority or a privately-contracted regional authority rather than any municipal government — the clearest and most complete instance of this mechanism identified in this project.

**Counterpoints:** sanitation shows a partial, city-by-city version of the same mechanism (a majority, not all, of this project's cities contract the function out), while dispatchers show a more limited version (a state grant and certification structure exists for regional dispatch centers, but the underlying dispatch employees appear to remain municipal in the cities examined).

**What current evidence can show:** that governance distance is not a single phenomenon — it ranges from a city-level contracting choice (sanitation) to a state-law-mandated structure applying uniformly across an entire service category (transit).

**What evidence is still needed:** whether any city outside this project's current set directly operates its own municipal transit service; whether any of this project's cities' dispatch functions are organized through a regional emergency-communications center rather than a purely municipal one.

### Budget / fiscal constraint

**What it means:** whether an occupation's wage-setting process is constrained by a budget mechanism specific to that occupation, beyond ordinary municipal fiscal capacity.

**Strongest groups:** teachers, via a confirmed, occupation-specific two-sided constraint (a state-mandated spending floor combined with the same general municipal levy-limit ceiling other departments face), and library, via a weaker, waivable analog specific to library appropriations.

**Counterpoints:** general municipal fiscal capacity is a candidate confound for every occupation's wage outcome, not specific to any one group — a wealthier city could in principle pay more across every occupation simultaneously.

**What current evidence can show:** that teachers face a documented, occupation-specific budget floor that no other group examined shares in the same form.

**What evidence is still needed:** a cross-occupation, within-city fiscal-capacity correlation test, not yet built for any group in this project.

### Source-document production effects

**What it means:** different document types systematically preserve different kinds of wage-setting reasoning, independent of how important that reasoning actually is to the underlying wage outcome — a caution that applies to every other mechanism in this section, not a mechanism in its own right.

**Strongest groups:** confirmed as a corpus-wide pattern, independently replicated across DPW's and clerical/administrative staff's full document reviews — both found an identical absence of recruitment-difficulty and peer-comparability language in every contract reviewed.

**Counterpoints:** none — this is itself the caution that qualifies every other finding in this report.

**What current evidence can show:** that arbitration and award documents preserve explicit wage-setting reasoning in a way ordinary contracts do not, because a statute directs the arbitrator to record that reasoning; this is a property of the document type, not evidence that the underlying reasoning is absent from non-safety bargaining altogether.

**What evidence is still needed:** budget documents, human-resources reports, and other document types not yet represented in this project's causal corpus, which would be expected to preserve recruitment, retention, and workload reasoning that contract text does not.

## 6. Massachusetts and National Nuance

Massachusetts institutional facts, confirmed directly in this project's own corpus and through targeted institutional review, should not be read as general national findings. This project has now identified three distinct Massachusetts wage-impasse regimes:

- **Police and fire:** JLMC, under M.G.L. c. 1078 (established by Chapter 589 of the Acts of 1987), orders compulsory, binding interest arbitration.
- **Most other municipal occupations — teachers, DPW, clerical/administrative staff, library workers, custodial/facilities staff, and, by direct confirmation this session, dispatchers:** the ordinary Chapter 150E Section 9 process, involving mediation and advisory (non-binding) fact-finding, with a voluntary arbitration route that exists on paper but is rarely invoked.
- **Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority employees specifically:** a separate compulsory-arbitration statute (M.G.L. c. 161A), applying to the state authority's own workforce rather than to any city government.

Regional transit authorities (governing transit service in five of this project's nine cities) are required by state law (M.G.L. c. 161B) to contract their operations to a private management company, meaning the workforce that actually operates transit service in those cities is a private-sector employer's employees, not a public employer's at all. Sanitation service, by contrast, shows no single governing statute of this kind — its contracted-versus-municipal status varies city by city, based on each municipality's own procurement choice rather than a state-law requirement.

National context developed alongside this project's Massachusetts-specific findings should be read carefully for scope. A citation-audited review confirms that compulsory police/fire interest arbitration recurs as a policy pattern in several other states, but this is not a majority-of-states finding, and the specific vehicle (a named commission, a specific statute) differs by state. At least one state (Iowa) is confirmed, via primary statutory text, to extend compulsory arbitration to teachers as well — a documented exception to the general pattern that non-safety occupations lack this backstop. Two additional national findings carry explicit sourcing caveats: one state's public-sector bargaining-rights statute for non-safety-versus-safety employees is currently under active legal challenge and should be treated as unsettled, and a bargaining prohibition in at least one other state rests on secondary-source consensus rather than as clean a primary citation as a comparable prohibition in a different state.

None of the Massachusetts-specific institutions described in this report — JLMC, Chapter 70's school-finance formula, Proposition 2½'s levy-limit mechanics, or the transit-governance statutes described above — should be treated as describing how municipal wage-setting works nationally. Where this report states a national pattern, it is because a specific, citable source supports that pattern; where no such source exists, the report states that the finding is Massachusetts-specific or limited to the cities examined.

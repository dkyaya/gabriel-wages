# Texas/Ohio Multi-City Pre-Ingestion Scan

**Date:** 2026-07-08
**Type:** final pre-ingestion multi-city comparison scan across Texas and Ohio. Planning/audit only — no ingestion, no `data/contracts.csv`/`data/city_coverage.csv` edits, no corpus/inbox changes, no causal claim.

## 1. Purpose and scope

This memo responds to the PI's original request (Texas and Ohio suggested as non-Massachusetts comparison states, to test whether this project's safety/non-safety wage-mechanism findings depend on Massachusetts's JLMC arbitration statute specifically) and follows directly from the prior two Texas/Ohio sessions. Those sessions converged on Houston and Columbus as first-choice acquisition targets — but Houston's most distinctive feature (a population-triggered compulsory-arbitration exception for fire, §174.1535, and a population-triggered non-safety bargaining statute, Chapter 146) is explicitly gated to municipalities above 1.9 million and 1.5 million residents respectively, thresholds only Houston meets among Texas cities. **This memo broadens the scan across five additional Texas cities and five additional Ohio cities to check whether the prior sessions' Houston/Columbus recommendation is representative of a genuine institutional pattern or an artifact of picking the single largest, most population-exceptional city in each state.** It does not ingest any document, does not edit `data/contracts.csv` or `data/city_coverage.csv`, and does not make any causal claim about Texas or Ohio wage outcomes.

## 2. Why a multi-city scan is needed

- **Houston is unusually large and appears to trigger Texas provisions that do not apply to most cities.** The prior session's follow-up memo confirmed that both §174.1535 (mandatory fire arbitration) and Chapter 146 (non-safety meet-and-confer) apply only to municipalities of 1.9 million and 1.5 million residents or more, respectively — thresholds Houston alone meets among Texas cities. An ingestion plan built only on Houston risks reporting a genuinely Houston-specific institutional exception as if it were a general Texas finding.
- **Columbus is promising, but Ohio's Chapter 4117/SERB framework should be checked across multiple cities before treating any single city as sufficient.** Unlike Texas, Ohio's statute contains no population gate — but that does not by itself guarantee that source availability, bargaining-unit structure, or impasse-history documentation is equally rich in every Ohio city. This needs to be checked directly, not assumed from the statute's uniform text alone.
- **This project's own Massachusetts comparison spans nine cities and towns of varying size** (Boston at one end, Georgetown and Seekonk at the other), not one city — Boston alone would not have been treated as sufficient evidence for this project's Massachusetts findings. The same discipline should apply before treating Houston or Columbus alone as sufficient for a Texas/Ohio comparison; a fair non-Massachusetts comparison needs to check institutional fit across a size range comparable to what this project already does for Massachusetts.

## 3. Texas institutional variation by city

### Houston (population ≈ 2.39 million — above both the 1.9M and 1.5M thresholds)

- Chapter 174 full collective bargaining: adopted for fire in 2003 (HPFFA/IAFF Local 341); §174.1535 makes binding arbitration compulsory for Houston Fire specifically, since Houston is the only Texas city above the 1.9-million threshold.
- Chapter 142 meet-and-confer: governs police (HPOU); non-compulsory.
- Chapter 146 non-safety meet-and-confer: applies (Houston is the only Texas city above the 1.5-million threshold); HOPE/AFSCME Local 123 has held this role since 2008.
- Police/fire agreements publicly findable: yes, official `houstontx.gov` copies located for all three tiers (fire, police, non-safety), though none extracted as readable text through this session's tooling (see the prior follow-up memo, Section 8).
- Non-safety agreements/pay plans publicly findable: yes (HOPE agreement plus a separate civil-service classification/pay-grade system).
- City budget/pay plans publicly findable: yes (`houstontx.gov/hr/compensation`).
- Expected usefulness: highest of any city scanned in either state — three distinct institutional tiers in one city — but **not representative of sub-threshold Texas cities**, precisely because two of its three tiers (fire's compulsory arbitration, non-safety's statutory bargaining channel) exist only because of Houston's population.

### San Antonio (population ≈ 1.4 million — below both thresholds)

- Chapter 174 collective bargaining: adopted for police (SAPOA) and, per the same city portal, fire (SAFD) — full bargaining, non-compulsory arbitration (no population-triggered exception applies, since San Antonio is below 1.9 million).
- Chapter 142 meet-and-confer: not applicable (San Antonio uses full Chapter 174, not the lighter meet-and-confer track).
- Chapter 146 non-safety meet-and-confer: not available (San Antonio is well below the 1.5-million threshold).
- Police/fire agreements publicly findable: yes, a centralized, official `sanantonio.gov` Collective Bargaining portal hosts both a 2022-2026 police CBA and a fire CBA.
- Non-safety agreements/pay plans publicly findable: not identified in this or the prior session — this remains San Antonio's clearest gap.
- City budget/pay plans publicly findable: not directly checked this session.
- Expected usefulness: high for the police/fire pair specifically — San Antonio is the clearest example of full, adopted Chapter 174 bargaining operating *without* any population-triggered compulsory-arbitration exception, a useful contrast to Houston Fire's compulsion. A 2021 ballot measure (Proposition B) to repeal Chapter 174 for police is a documented public-salience event.

### Dallas (population ≈ 1.3 million — below both thresholds)

- Chapter 174 collective bargaining: not adopted, per this session's research — Dallas instead uses a joint "Meet and Confer Team" agreement covering **both** police and fire together in a single agreement, a structurally distinct pattern from Houston/Austin/Fort Worth's fire-vs-police split (see Section 4).
- Chapter 142 meet-and-confer: yes, for the combined police/fire team; full-text copies located via both a labor-relations aggregator (`lris.com`) and Ballotpedia's document archive, with references to official `dallascityhall.com` budget memoranda discussing the agreement.
- Chapter 146 non-safety meet-and-confer: not available (below the 1.5-million threshold).
- Police/fire agreements publicly findable: yes, though the cleanest full-text copies located this session are hosted by a labor-relations aggregator and Ballotpedia rather than an official `dallascityhall.com` PDF directly — an official city copy should be confirmed before acquisition.
- Non-safety agreements/pay plans publicly findable: no confirmed statutory bargaining channel. An employee association (NTAPE Local 9487, USW) and CWA Local 6215 exist, but neither was confirmed this session to have a state-recognized meet-and-confer or bargaining relationship comparable to Houston's HOPE — Dallas's non-safety wage-setting appears to run through a civil-service classification/pay system (reported over 1,100 classifications and 8 salary schedules) rather than a negotiated agreement.
- City budget/pay plans publicly findable: yes, via `dallascityhall.com`'s Compensation and Classification page.
- Expected usefulness: useful as a clean example of the Chapter 617 default-prohibition pattern operating largely unmodified for non-safety employees, alongside a joint (not split) police/fire meet-and-confer structure.

### Austin (population ≈ 980,000 — below both thresholds, the smallest of this section's five core cities)

- Chapter 174 collective bargaining: adopted for fire (Austin Firefighters Association Local 975 — a full "Fire Collective Bargaining Agreement," distinct in name and legal basis from the police agreement below), confirmed in the prior scoping session.
- Chapter 142 meet-and-confer: governs police (Austin Police Association, 2024-2029 agreement).
- Chapter 146 non-safety meet-and-confer: not available (below the 1.5-million threshold).
- Police/fire agreements publicly findable: yes, on a dedicated, well-organized official `austintexas.gov` labor-relations portal — the best-organized single-city Texas portal found across all sessions.
- Non-safety agreements/pay plans publicly findable: **a genuinely new and important finding this session.** AFSCME Local 1624 represents roughly 4,900 City of Austin and Travis County employees; as of February 2026, Austin became, per AFSCME's own reporting, "the first city in Texas to codify" a formal **consultation agreement** between city management and AFSCME Local 1624 — a locally-created channel, not a Chapter 146 meet-and-confer relationship (Austin does not meet Chapter 146's population threshold), establishing regular structured meetings between union representatives and city executives. This is a recent, dated, non-statutory institutional innovation, not yet a full bargaining or meet-and-confer agreement in the Chapter 142/146 sense.
- City budget/pay plans publicly findable: not directly re-checked this session (civil-service/classification pages were noted in the prior session).
- Expected usefulness: **the richest sub-threshold Texas city found in this scan.** Austin shows the same fire-vs-police institutional split as Houston (full Chapter 174 bargaining for fire, lighter Chapter 142 meet-and-confer for police) without needing any population-triggered exception, plus a genuinely novel, very recent non-safety consultation channel that is not a statutory bargaining relationship but is also not nothing — a useful middle case between Houston's full Chapter 146 channel and Dallas's/Fort Worth's apparent absence of any non-safety channel.

### Fort Worth (population ≈ 980,000 — below both thresholds)

- Chapter 174 collective bargaining: **now confirmed for fire** — official city language describes "the Fire Collective Bargaining Contract... between the City of Fort Worth and the Fort Worth Professional Firefighters Association," language consistent with full Chapter 174 bargaining rather than meet-and-confer. This is the third of five core cities (with Houston and Austin) where fire specifically has moved to full Chapter 174 bargaining.
- Chapter 142 meet-and-confer: governs police (Fort Worth Police Officers Association) — official city language distinguishes the "Police Meet and Confer Agreement" from the fire department's "Collective Bargaining Contract," confirming the same fire/police institutional split found in Houston and Austin. Fort Worth's police meet-and-confer relationship was itself established by a November 2006 city-wide referendum authorizing the city to recognize the police association as sole bargaining agent.
- Chapter 146 non-safety meet-and-confer: not available (below the 1.5-million threshold).
- Police/fire agreements publicly findable: yes — a `fortworthtexas.gov` City Secretary's office maintains dedicated "Collective Bargaining" event pages (with a 2024 fire-specific page), and prior-cycle full agreement PDFs (e.g., a 2017-2020 police meet-and-confer agreement with salary-schedule attachment) are hosted on the city's council-packet system.
- Non-safety agreements/pay plans publicly findable: no union contract found for non-safety employees this session; non-civil-service employees are reported to receive pay adjustments through a "Pay for Performance Program" rather than a negotiated agreement, alongside published citywide salary schedules (FY2024, FY2025, FY2026 versions all located as official PDFs).
- City budget/pay plans publicly findable: yes, official citywide salary schedules for multiple fiscal years.
- Expected usefulness: high for confirming the fire/police institutional-split pattern is not a Houston-only or population-triggered phenomenon; the non-safety gap mirrors Dallas and San Antonio.

### El Paso (population ≈ 679,000 — added as a sixth Texas city; below both thresholds)

- Chapter 174 collective bargaining: reported (via a secondary Texas Senate bill-analysis document) to be one of "over 30 cities throughout the state" that have implemented Chapter 174, alongside Fort Worth, Houston, and San Antonio — not independently re-confirmed via an official El Paso city source this session.
- A full police CBA (El Paso Municipal Police Officers' Association, 2023-2027) was located, though hosted by CLEAT (a statewide police-union umbrella organization), not an official `elpasotexas.gov` page — an official copy should be located before treating this as an acquisition-ready source.
- Non-safety and fire-specific sources were not explored this session for El Paso.
- Expected usefulness: lower confidence than the five core cities; useful mainly as a data point corroborating that Chapter 174 adoption is common across many mid-size-to-large Texas cities, not merely a Houston/San Antonio/Austin/Fort Worth phenomenon. Not recommended for near-term acquisition without further work.

## 4. Texas threshold implications

- **How Houston differs from sub-1.9M/sub-1.5M cities:** Houston is the only Texas city, among all six scanned, where a population threshold converts an otherwise-ordinary institutional feature into a compulsory or uniquely-available one. Every other scanned city's institutional arrangement (Chapter 174 adoption, Chapter 142 meet-and-confer, or the absence of any non-safety channel) reflects a local choice or a Chapter 617 default, not a population-driven legal switch.
- **What §174.1535 likely means for Houston Fire compared with other Texas fire departments:** the underlying bargaining relationship (Chapter 174 adoption for fire) is not unique to Houston — Austin and Fort Worth have adopted the same chapter for their fire departments, and secondary sourcing suggests 30+ Texas cities have done so in some form. **What is unique to Houston is that its Chapter 174 fire bargaining converts to true compulsory arbitration on impasse, while Austin's and Fort Worth's fire bargaining remains subject to the general Chapter 174 rule that arbitration requires both parties' mutual, written consent (§174.163).** In other words: the *bargaining relationship* generalizes across at least three of five core Texas cities; the *compulsion* does not generalize beyond Houston.
- **Whether Chapter 146 creates a Houston-specific non-safety channel:** yes, confirmed and reinforced by this session's broader scan — none of the five additional Texas cities examined has a Chapter 146 relationship (all are below the 1.5-million threshold), and none was found to have an equivalent *statutory* non-safety bargaining channel. What varies instead is how each sub-threshold city has filled that gap informally: Austin's brand-new (February 2026) AFSCME consultation agreement is the closest analogue found; Dallas's and Fort Worth's non-safety employees appear to rely on civil-service classification/pay systems with no confirmed negotiated channel at all.
- **Whether sub-threshold Texas cities still have public-safety wage channels:** yes, clearly — every one of the five sub-threshold cities examined has *some* police and/or fire institutional channel (Chapter 174 adoption for fire in Austin and Fort Worth; Chapter 174 adoption for both police and fire in San Antonio; joint Chapter 142 meet-and-confer for both in Dallas; a mix of Chapter 174 fire/Chapter 142 police in Fort Worth). What is genuinely rare below the population thresholds is a *non-safety* statutory channel, and a genuinely compulsory safety-side arbitration mechanism.
- **What this means for comparing Texas to Massachusetts:** the comparison is cleanest for the police/fire side, where multiple Texas cities across a wide population range (from ~680,000 to ~2.4 million) show a real, if generally non-compulsory, bargaining relationship — comparable in kind, though generally weaker in compulsion, to Massachusetts's JLMC-backed police/fire bargaining. The comparison is harder for the non-safety side: Massachusetts's Chapter 150E applies uniformly to non-safety municipal employees regardless of city size, while in Texas, a genuine statutory non-safety bargaining channel is essentially Houston-only, and most sub-threshold Texas cities' "matched non-safety" comparison unit would need to be a civil-service classification/pay-plan document rather than a negotiated CBA — a real design constraint for source acquisition, not a minor detail.

## 5. Ohio institutional variation by city

### Columbus (previously confirmed top target)

- Police (FOP Capital City Lodge No. 9, 2023-2026), fire (IAFF Local 67, 2023-2026), and two non-safety unions (AFSCME Local 1632, 2024-2027; CWA, 2023-2026) are all officially hosted on one `columbus.gov` labor-relations portal, cross-checkable against a SERB-filed IAFF Local 67 fact-finding report. A Health Administrative Compensation Plan (HACP) provides non-bargaining-unit pay-plan context. See the prior follow-up memo for full detail.

### Cleveland

- Police: two distinct bargaining units confirmed — the Cleveland Police Patrolmen's Association (CPPA, non-supervisory patrol officers and trainees, 2025-2028 and a prior 2022-2025 CBA) and a separate Fraternal Order of Police Lodge 8 CBA (2022-2025, likely covering supervisory ranks, paralleling this project's own Franklin Police Sergeants precedent for a rank-vs-supervisor split) — both officially hosted at `clevelandohio.gov`.
- Fire: IAFF Local 93, with both a current 2025-2028 CBA and a prior 2019-2021 CBA officially hosted at `clevelandohio.gov`.
- Non-safety: AFSCME Ohio Council 8 Local 100, with both a 2022-2025 CBA and a prior 2019-2022 CBA officially hosted at `clevelandohio.gov`.
- SERB archive availability: Ohio's statewide archive applies uniformly; a Cleveland-specific SERB filing was not individually pulled this session but is expected to exist given the pattern confirmed for Columbus.
- Factfinding/conciliation/award availability: not individually confirmed this session, though reported news coverage (a 2025 tentative deal announcement between Cleveland's mayor and the CPPA) suggests an active, documented bargaining history.
- City budget/pay plan availability: not directly checked this session.
- Expected usefulness: **the most complete single-portal Ohio city found in this entire scan** — all four institutional tiers (patrol police, supervisory police, fire, non-safety), each with both a current and at least one prior CBA, all on one official page.

### Cincinnati

- Police: two distinct bargaining units — FOP Queen City Lodge No. 69 for non-supervisors (2024-2027) and a separate supervisors' agreement (also FOP Queen City Lodge No. 69, 2024-2027, covering sergeants through lieutenant colonel/assistant chief) — both officially hosted at `cincinnati-oh.gov`.
- Fire: IAFF Local 48, 2023-2026 CBA, officially hosted at `cincinnati-oh.gov`.
- Non-safety: CODE (Cincinnati Organized and Dedicated Employees) and Teamsters, both 2025-2028, previously confirmed and officially hosted at `cincinnati-oh.gov`/`cincyweb.cincinnati-oh.gov`.
- SERB archive availability: confirmed in the prior session for a related Cincinnati docket.
- Factfinding/conciliation/award availability: not individually re-confirmed this session for Cincinnati specifically.
- City budget/pay plan availability: not directly checked this session.
- Expected usefulness: essentially as complete as Columbus — all four tiers confirmed on one official portal, with a genuine rank-vs-supervisor police split as an added comparison dimension.

### Toledo

- Police: two distinct units — the Toledo Police Patrolman's Association and the Toledo Police Command Officers' Association (TPCOA, 2021-2024) — both with agreements hosted on an official `cdn.toledo.oh.gov` Human Resources document repository.
- Fire: Toledo Firefighters Local 92, plus a separately named Toledo Fire Chiefs Association, both referenced via the same official repository.
- Non-safety: **the richest non-safety union structure found in either state** — AFSCME Locals 7, 2058, and 3411 (AFSCME Local 7 alone reported at 799 members, the city's largest bargaining unit; a 2025-2027 AFSCME Local 3411 agreement also located), plus Teamsters Local 20 and UAW Local 12, all reportedly on file with the city and multiple confirmed as officially hosted PDFs.
- SERB archive availability: applies statewide; not individually pulled for Toledo this session, though the city's own repository appears to be a sufficient primary source in its own right.
- Factfinding/conciliation/award availability: news coverage (Toledo Blade, July 2025) confirms an active, recently-concluded bargaining round for both an AFSCME unit and, separately, police/fire units, indicating a documented, current impasse/negotiation history even without a specific SERB filing pulled this session.
- City budget/pay plan availability: not directly checked this session.
- Expected usefulness: high — Toledo shows the same rank-vs-command police split as Cincinnati and the same multi-union non-safety structure question this project already handles for Massachusetts's mixed AFSCME/Teamsters units (e.g., Worcester, Franklin), making it a strong structural analogue.

### Akron

- Police: FOP is reported to be the "deemed-certified exclusive representative" of Akron's sworn police officers, but no specific, current, officially-hosted CBA PDF was located this session.
- Fire: the Akron Fire Fighters Association (IAFF-affiliated) is confirmed to exist, and a 2025 news report (Signal Akron) describes an active contract impasse — the union rejected a mediator's report during negotiations — a genuinely interesting live example of Ohio's fact-finding process in action, but the underlying fact-finding report itself was not located at a specific URL this session.
- Non-safety: only a historical AFSCME agreement (1991-1993) was located; current AFSCME status in Akron was not confirmed this session.
- SERB/factfinding/conciliation availability: the Akron impasse described above should have a corresponding SERB fact-finding or conciliation filing, but a specific document was not located this session.
- City budget/pay plan availability: not checked this session.
- Expected usefulness: **the weakest-documented Ohio city in this scan.** Akron may still be a viable future target — its active fire-department impasse is exactly the kind of "impasse in progress" evidence this project has not yet captured for any city, safety or non-safety, Massachusetts or otherwise — but it needs a dedicated follow-up search before being treated as acquisition-ready.

### Dayton

- Police: FOP Captain John C. Post Lodge No. 44 is confirmed as the bargaining agent for patrol officers, but no specific, current CBA PDF was located at an official `daytonohio.gov` URL this session.
- Fire: IAFF Local 136 CBA, hosted via Ohio's SERB archive (a specific docket PDF located).
- Non-safety: **a genuinely positive correction to this project's own prior assessment.** The Dayton Public Service Union (DPSU), an AFSCME-affiliated unit representing roughly 600 "Operations Employees," has a 2020-2023 CBA officially hosted on `daytonohio.gov`'s own Document Center — this reverses the prior session's characterization of Dayton as lacking a "comparably centralized public labor-relations portal."
- SERB archive availability: confirmed useful for locating Dayton's fire CBA specifically.
- Factfinding/conciliation/award availability: two 2023 SERB fact-finding reports referencing Dayton-area dockets were located, though the specific employer/bargaining-unit identity of each was not confirmed in depth this session.
- City budget/pay plan availability: not checked this session.
- Expected usefulness: moderate, and higher than the prior session's assessment — Dayton has an official city document center (a positive correction) and a confirmed non-safety CBA, but its police-specific source and the exact identity of its SERB fact-finding filings need a follow-up before this city is acquisition-ready.

## 6. Ohio statewide framework implications

- **Chapter 4117/SERB provides materially more consistent cross-city comparability than Texas.** Every one of the six Ohio cities scanned operates under the identical statutory framework regardless of size — there is no Ohio analogue to Texas's population-triggered §174.1535 or Chapter 146. What varies across Ohio cities is *documentation quality and portal organization*, not the underlying institutional design: Columbus, Cleveland, Cincinnati, and Toledo (Ohio's four largest cities scanned) all show essentially the same four-tier structure (one or two police units, one fire unit, one or more non-safety units), each independently confirmed via an official city-hosted portal. Akron and Dayton show that smaller/mid-size Ohio cities can have thinner or less centrally organized documentation, but nothing in this session's research suggests their underlying legal framework differs — the gap is one of source-acquisition convenience, not institutional structure.
- **How safety-force/no-strike impasse pathways compare to non-safety workers:** unchanged from the prior sessions' finding and confirmed to apply uniformly across every city scanned — ORC §4117.14(D)(1)'s no-strike list (naming police, firefighters, and several other categories) routes to compulsory final-offer conciliation if fact-finding fails, while other public employees (including this project's non-safety comparison groups) retain a conditional statutory strike right instead. Akron's currently-unresolved fire impasse is a live illustration of this pathway in progress, though its specific documentation was not located this session.
- **Is Columbus representative enough as a first Ohio target, or is a second Ohio city needed?** Columbus remains a fully sound first target, but this session's findings show **Cleveland and Cincinnati are equally complete, not merely "second-choice" backups** — both have all four institutional tiers confirmed on one official portal, and Cleveland in particular shows an even richer rank-vs-supervisor police split than Columbus's single police CBA. Given Ohio's much greater cross-city institutional consistency (Section 6, first bullet), adding a second Ohio city is a low-cost, low-risk way to demonstrate the Ohio comparison is not overfit to Columbus specifically — a materially easier correction to make for Ohio than for Texas, where a second city (e.g., Austin or Fort Worth) is needed to show the underlying institutional split generalizes at all, not merely to add documentation depth.

## 8. Recommended ingestion design

- **Do not ingest Houston + Columbus only.** Doing so would risk exactly the overfitting concern this scan was commissioned to check — Houston's most distinctive features are population-gated, and while Columbus is a sound anchor, treating it alone as "Ohio" would understate how consistent Ohio's institutional framework is across its largest cities.
- **Add one sub-threshold Texas city: Austin.** Austin shows the same fire/police institutional split as Houston (Chapter 174 full bargaining for fire, Chapter 142 meet-and-confer for police) without needing any population-triggered exception, plus a genuinely new non-safety consultation channel — making it the clearest available demonstration that Houston's *general* institutional pattern (not its *compulsory-arbitration specific* feature) generalizes to a city one-third Houston's size. Fort Worth is a strong backup that would further corroborate the same split pattern; San Antonio is the strongest backup specifically for a full-Chapter-174-without-compulsion contrast.
- **Add one additional Ohio city: Cleveland.** Cleveland's four-plus-tier institutional structure (patrol police, supervisory police, fire, non-safety) is, if anything, more complete than Columbus's own, and is entirely officially documented on one portal — this is a low-cost, low-risk addition that directly demonstrates Ohio's cross-city consistency rather than merely adding a second data point for its own sake. Cincinnati is an equally strong backup if a different rank-structure comparison (non-supervisor/supervisor rather than patrol/supervisor-lodge) is preferred.
- **Ingest legal/institutional context sources as context only**, not as causal-corpus rows — this applies to every state statute (Texas Chapters 174/142/146/617; Ohio Chapter 4117), every SERB or state-agency guidance page, and every secondary/news source used for source discovery in this and the prior two sessions.
- **Prioritize agreements/CBAs first, budgets/pay plans second, impasse awards third**, for the recommended first batch (Houston, Austin, Columbus, Cleveland) — CBAs are this project's primary causal-corpus document type and the most directly comparable to this project's existing Massachusetts rows; budget/pay-plan documents are the necessary substitute artifact wherever a non-safety statutory bargaining channel does not exist (most Texas sub-threshold cities); impasse/fact-finding/conciliation awards (most readily available via Ohio's SERB archive) are a valuable but secondary enrichment, best pursued once the core matched-CBA rows for the first batch are confirmed acquisition-ready.

## 9. Bottom-line recommendation

**Recommended first ingestion batch (pending separate authorization to begin acquisition): Houston and Austin for Texas; Columbus and Cleveland for Ohio.** This batch pairs one population-exceptional city with one representative-size city in each state, directly addressing the overfitting concern this scan was commissioned to check, while still prioritizing the two cities (Houston, Columbus) that the prior sessions found to have the richest, most officially-documented source availability. **Backup choices, in priority order:** Texas — Fort Worth (confirms the fire/police split independently of Austin), then San Antonio (a full-Chapter-174-without-compulsion contrast); Ohio — Cincinnati (an equally complete alternative to Cleveland), then Toledo (richest non-safety union variety, at the cost of more complex occupation-class mapping). **Deferred, not rejected:** Dallas (pending an official-city-hosted source confirmation), El Paso (pending independent verification beyond secondary sourcing), Akron and Dayton (pending dedicated follow-up searches for their police-specific and, for Akron, non-safety-specific sources). No city in either state is recommended for outright rejection at this stage — the "defer" cities may still become useful once the first batch's acquisition work clarifies how much additional depth this project's design actually needs.

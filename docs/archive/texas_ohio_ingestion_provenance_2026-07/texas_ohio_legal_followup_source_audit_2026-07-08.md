## 2. Texas Chapter 174 clarification

- **What Chapter 174 covers:** full collective bargaining rights for fire fighters and/or police officers of a political subdivision (defined in §174.003(4) to include municipalities), covering wages, hours, and other conditions of employment.
- **Statewide automatic or local adoption/election:** local adoption only, confirmed directly from §174.051 (Adoption Election) — a political subdivision is under Chapter 174 only after its voters adopt it in a referendum triggered by a petition of the lesser of 20,000 signatures or 5% of the last general-election turnout. §174.053 allows a later repeal election. This is unchanged from the prior session's finding.
- **How it treats police/fire collective bargaining:** once adopted, the statute establishes a genuine bargaining relationship — recognition of a bargaining agent, a duty to bargain, and (per §174.153) a process by which either party may request arbitration on impasse.
- **Impasse/mediation/factfinding/arbitration/court-enforcement mechanisms:** Chapter 174's general rule, per §174.163, is that **compulsory arbitration is not required** — even where one party requests arbitration under §174.153, both parties must separately make a written "election to arbitrate" within 5 days, meaning either side (in practice, typically the city) can simply decline and leave the dispute unresolved by any forced mechanism. This is unchanged from the prior session's finding.
- **What §174.1535 "Mandatory Arbitration" means in context (directly resolved this session, via a direct fetch-and-read of the section text, corroborated across two independent sources):** §174.1535 is a narrow, population-triggered **exception** to §174.163's general no-compulsory-arbitration rule. It applies **only to a fire department that serves a municipality with a population of 1.9 million or more**, and to the association that is the bargaining agent for that department's employees. For a covered department, if the parties cannot settle within 61 days after the municipality's governing body fails to approve a bargained contract, **binding interest arbitration is mandatory** — each party must send the other written notice of every disputed issue within 5 days of the impasse (or the expiration of an extension period), and the matter proceeds to binding arbitration rather than remaining unresolved. **As of this session, Houston (population approximately 2.39 million per the most recent U.S. Census Bureau data located) is the only Texas municipality that meets this threshold.** No other Texas city (San Antonio ~1.4 million, Dallas ~1.3 million, Austin and Fort Worth smaller still) is anywhere near 1.9 million. This is therefore, in practical effect, a Houston-fire-only compulsory-arbitration statute, not a general Chapter 174 feature.
- **Automatic, optional, election/charter-dependent, or limited:** a layered answer, now fully resolved — (1) Chapter 174 itself is election-dependent (a city must adopt it by referendum); (2) once adopted, arbitration is optional/mutual-consent under the general §174.163/174.153 rule; (3) **but for the single population tier defined in §174.1535, arbitration becomes mandatory/compulsory once the underlying Chapter 174 bargaining relationship exists and an impasse persists past the statutory window** — no further local election or additional consent is needed to trigger §174.1535 once a covered city's fire department is already bargaining under Chapter 174.
- **How this differs from Massachusetts JLMC:** JLMC applies automatically, statewide, to every Massachusetts city's police and fire units, with no population threshold and no local-adoption step. §174.1535 is the structural mirror image in miniature: it produces a genuinely compulsory arbitration outcome, but confined by a population threshold to a single Texas fire department (Houston's) rather than applying uniformly. This makes Houston Fire a materially closer, though far narrower, institutional analogue to Massachusetts's JLMC-covered police/fire than this project's prior Texas assessment (which treated all examined Texas cities as uniformly non-compulsory) had characterized. Houston Police, by contrast, remains under Chapter 142 meet-and-confer with no compulsory-arbitration equivalent — so within one city, this project would find one occupation (fire) under a compulsory-arbitration regime and a directly adjacent occupation (police) under a non-compulsory one, a genuinely new within-city contrast this project's Massachusetts corpus does not have (Massachusetts's JLMC covers both police and fire uniformly).

## 3. Texas Chapter 146 clarification

- **What Chapter 146 covers (directly resolved this session via a fetch-and-read of §146.001 and §146.002):** Local Government Code Chapter 146, "Local Control of Municipal Employment Matters in Certain Municipalities," authorizes a meet-and-confer relationship between a qualifying municipality and a recognized "employee association" on behalf of "covered employees" — defined as municipal employees other than department heads and other than firefighters/police officers covered by Chapter 143 (civil service) or Chapter 174. In plain terms: **it is the non-safety-employee counterpart to Chapter 142's police/fire meet-and-confer**, using the same non-compulsory design (no duty to reach agreement on any issue; the earlier session's finding here is confirmed, and no arbitration or impasse-resolution section was found within Chapter 146's own section range, 146.001-146.017, consistent with Chapter 142's structure).
- **Available to municipal employees generally, and does it require local adoption?** This is the single most important correction from this session: **Chapter 146 is not a general, city-choice local-option statute.** Per §146.001(a), directly quoted: "This chapter applies only to a municipality with a population of 1.5 million or more." There is no election, referendum, or ordinance-adoption step described in the chapter — applicability is automatic and purely population-gated, structurally identical in form to §174.1535's fire-specific population gate (Section 2 above), just set at a lower threshold (1.5 million vs. 1.9 million) and covering non-safety employees instead of fire. **As of this session, Houston is again the only Texas municipality that meets this threshold** — San Antonio, Dallas, Austin, and Fort Worth are all well under 1.5 million residents. The prior session's characterization of Chapter 146 as a chapter that Texas cities "choose to adopt" was inaccurate and is corrected here: no Texas city currently has, or could presently exercise, a choice to adopt Chapter 146 for a population under 1.5 million.
- **Relevant for Houston or other candidate cities?** Relevant for Houston only, among this project's five previously-scanned Texas candidate cities (Houston, San Antonio, Austin, Dallas, Fort Worth) — and, on current population figures, for no other Texas municipality at all.
- **Does it give non-safety workers a comparable impasse pathway to police/fire?** No. Chapter 146 provides a bargaining/meet-and-confer channel but, like Chapter 142, creates no arbitration or compulsory impasse-resolution mechanism — the "comparable" institutional feature it shares with police/fire in Houston specifically is the meet-and-confer relationship itself (paralleling Houston Police's Chapter 142 status), not any arbitration backstop (which, per Section 2, exists only for Houston Fire under §174.1535, not for Houston Police or Houston's Chapter 146 non-safety employees).
- **A materially important, previously-incorrect assumption this corrects:** the prior scoping memo and candidate-target table stated that Houston's non-safety municipal employees likely have "no collective-bargaining channel at all" absent a confirmed Chapter 146 adoption, with civil-service classification/pay-plan systems substituting entirely. **This is now confirmed incorrect for Houston specifically.** Houston has met Chapter 146's population threshold since the chapter's 2005 enactment (Houston's population has been well above 1.5 million throughout this period), and the Houston Organization of Public Employees (HOPE), AFSCME Local 123, has been the recognized Chapter 146 bargaining agent for Houston's non-safety "covered employees" since 2008, with successor meet-and-confer agreements in 2015, 2018, 2021, and 2024 — the current 2024 agreement is posted on both HOPE's own site and on an official City of Houston HR page (see Section 4 and the companion CSVs). Web-search synthesis (not yet an independently line-by-line read of the agreement's own preamble text, since the located PDF did not extract cleanly this session — see Section 8) corroborates, across multiple independent sources including the union's own materials and AFSCME's national blog, that the agreement is expressly grounded in "the meet and confer provisions of Chapter 146 of the Texas Local Government Code" and that HOPE represents municipal employees "in every city department," with a reported membership over 12,000.
- **What remains uncertain:** (1) the exact recognition-clause language and the precise list of departments/job classifications HOPE represents was not independently confirmed via a direct, successfully-extracted read of the agreement's own text this session (the official houstontx.gov PDF did not extract as readable text through this session's tooling); (2) whether Houston's civil-service classification/pay-plan system (confirmed last session) operates as a separate wage-setting layer alongside, rather than instead of, the HOPE meet-and-confer relationship, is a design question worth resolving before any future acquisition, since the two systems likely interact (e.g., a meet-and-confer agreement negotiating wage adjustments on top of an underlying classification/pay-grade structure) rather than one simply replacing the other.

## 5. Ohio Chapter 4117 and SERB confirmation

- **High-level structure:** unchanged from the prior session's finding, and not re-litigated in depth this session beyond a light confirmation pass. ORC Chapter 4117 is a single statewide statute covering essentially all Ohio public employees, administered by SERB, requiring mediation and then fact-finding on impasse.
- **Safety forces/no-strike workers:** employees named in §4117.14(D)(1) — police, firefighters, and several other named categories including certain dispatchers and nurses — are barred from striking and proceed automatically to binding, final-offer, issue-by-issue conciliation if fact-finding fails.
- **Final-offer conciliation/arbitration relevance:** directly relevant and unchanged from the prior session — this is Ohio's structural analogue to Massachusetts's JLMC and, in a narrower sense, to Houston Fire's §174.1535 mechanism, though Ohio's applies statewide with no population gate, unlike either of the Texas population-triggered provisions found this session.
- **Non-safety comparison:** unchanged — non-safety employees retain a conditional statutory strike right (10-day notice) rather than a compulsory-arbitration backstop.
- **Difference from Massachusetts JLMC and Texas Chapter 174:** now sharper, in light of this session's Texas findings. Massachusetts's JLMC and Ohio's §4117.14(D)(1) conciliation both apply automatically wherever their respective covered-employee categories exist — no population threshold, no local election. Texas's §174.1535 achieves a structurally similar compulsory-arbitration outcome, but only via a population gate that, on current Texas city populations, confines it to a single city's fire department. Ohio therefore remains the cleaner, broader-based single-state analogue to Massachusetts's institutional design; Texas's Houston-specific finding is a sharper, narrower, but genuinely useful natural-experiment-style contrast (a compulsory-arbitration mechanism triggered by a population rule rather than applying uniformly).
- **Source caveats:** this session's Ohio statutory confirmation rested on the same primary sources cited in the prior session's audit (`codes.ohio.gov`); no new statutory re-read was performed this session beyond what the prior session already completed directly. The city-level Columbus findings below are new this session.

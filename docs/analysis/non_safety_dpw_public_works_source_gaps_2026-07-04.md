# DPW / Public Works Source Gap List

**Date:** 2026-07-04
**Scope:** desk-research planning only. Lists the highest-value evidence gaps surfaced by `non_safety_dpw_public_works_wage_mechanism_refinement_2026-07-04.md`. Several items below already have a partial public source identified during this session's bounded web search; those are noted. This is a punch list for future sessions, not a completed data build.

For each gap: why it matters, likely source families, a feasibility tag (`desk_research_feasible`, `data_build_feasible`, `likely_hard`), and which dimension it speaks to (wage levels, wage growth, staffing supply, classification, licensing, outsourcing, overtime, or budget constraints).

## 1. DPW applicant counts over time

**Why it matters:** the same volume-vs-qualification distinction already established for police and teacher recruitment (are there few applicants, or many applicants who lack a required CDL/hoisting/water-operator credential) needs DPW-specific evidence rather than an assumption carried over from national CDL/water-workforce reporting.

**Likely source families:** individual Massachusetts municipal HR/civil-service application records; DPW annual reports; Massachusetts Municipal Association surveys if any exist.

**Feasibility:** `likely_hard` at a systematic level — unlike NCES's national teacher-vacancy snapshot, there is no equivalent centralized national or Massachusetts-specific DPW applicant-tracking survey identified this session; `data_build_feasible` only if narrowed to a small number of this project's specific cities and their own HR records.

**Dimension:** staffing supply.

## 2. DPW vacancy rates

**Why it matters:** the most direct staffing-strain measure, and the DPW-side analog to the police/teacher vacancy data already used elsewhere in the project's mechanism map.

**Likely source families:** municipal budget books (personnel/vacancy line items); DPW annual town reports; APWA workforce-development materials (national framing, not Massachusetts-specific vacancy data).

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` for this project's specific cities if their annual town reports or budget books include DPW staffing/vacancy detail, which Massachusetts municipal annual reports often do; `likely_hard` for a systematic multi-city Massachusetts series, since no centralized DPW vacancy database was identified this session.

**Dimension:** staffing supply.

## 3. CDL/equipment operator vacancy evidence

**Why it matters:** needed to test whether the national CDL "shortage vs. retention problem" debate (Section 4 of the refinement memo) actually shows up as extended vacancy duration for Massachusetts municipal CDL/equipment-operator roles specifically, as opposed to a general industry narrative.

**Likely source families:** individual DPW job postings and re-posting history (illustrative, not systematic); municipal HR records; local news coverage of specific hiring difficulties.

**Feasibility:** `likely_hard` for a systematic series; `desk_research_feasible` only as illustrative spot-checks of individual job postings, consistent with the project's caution against treating local news or individual postings as broad proof.

**Dimension:** staffing supply / licensing.

## 4. Water/sewer/wastewater operator shortage evidence, Massachusetts-specific

**Why it matters:** this session found strong national and New England-regional (NEIWPCC) evidence of an aging water/wastewater workforce, but no Massachusetts-city-specific confirmation; this is the strongest candidate DPW-side scarce-credential mechanism and deserves direct verification for this project's cities.

**Likely source families:** NEIWPCC (already identified this session as a regional source); Massachusetts Department of Environmental Protection (MassDEP) operator certification program data if publicly available; individual Massachusetts water/sewer enterprise-fund budget documents and CBAs.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` for further NEIWPCC/MassDEP program-level context; `data_build_feasible` for confirming whether any of this project's current cities' water/sewer departments report certified-operator vacancy or retirement data specifically.

**Dimension:** staffing supply / licensing.

## 5. Overtime/callback spending

**Why it matters:** central to the emergency-work comparison in Section 6 of the refinement memo — whether DPW emergency response (especially snow/storm) shows up as overtime spending rather than base-wage growth, and whether any Massachusetts DPW department shows the "chronic overtime" pattern that municipal risk-management guidance flags as a planning problem rather than legitimate emergency cost.

**Likely source families:** municipal budget books (overtime line items by department); finance committee reports; DPW annual reports.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` — this project already reviews municipal budget documents for other purposes, and DPW overtime is often a visible, itemized budget line in Massachusetts town reports; a same-city comparison of DPW overtime against police/fire overtime (already partially scoped in the prior workforce refinement memo's non-safety-comparison gap item 6) is a realistic near-term build.

**Dimension:** overtime / wage growth.

## 6. Snow/storm response staffing

**Why it matters:** needed to confirm whether Massachusetts DPW departments actually use pre-arranged seasonal on-call rosters and contractor retainer agreements the way general municipal risk-management guidance describes, or whether some departments instead rely on ad hoc, higher-cost emergency contracting.

**Likely source families:** DPW winter operations plans (often public documents in Massachusetts towns); municipal risk-management/insurance guidance (already identified this session as general, not Massachusetts-specific); local news coverage of specific storm responses.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` for individual Massachusetts town winter-operations plans, several of which are publicly posted; `likely_hard` for a systematic cross-city comparison without a much larger review effort.

**Dimension:** overtime / staffing supply.

## 7. Contractor/substitution use

**Why it matters:** needed to test the `dpw_contractor_substitution` hypothesis directly — how much DPW-type work in this project's cities is actually contracted out, and whether that contracted work is covered by Massachusetts prevailing wage law (which would blunt any wage-suppression effect of contracting) or falls outside prevailing-wage coverage (which would not).

**Likely source families:** municipal procurement/contract records; Massachusetts Department of Labor Standards (DLS) prevailing-wage determinations and coverage guidance (already identified this session at a general level); DPW annual reports describing contracted services.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` for identifying which of this project's cities contract out snow removal, paving, or specialized water/sewer work, if procurement records are public; `likely_hard` for a systematic determination of prevailing-wage coverage status for each specific contract type without direct DLS guidance review per contract.

**Dimension:** outsourcing / wage levels.

## 8. Delayed maintenance or service backlog evidence

**Why it matters:** needed to test the `dpw_service_deferral` hypothesis — whether documented DPW staffing strain in a city actually precedes a measurable increase in capital spending or emergency capital appropriations, as the refinement memo hypothesizes, or whether deferred maintenance simply persists without a clear budget-response lag.

**Likely source families:** municipal capital-improvement plans; DPW annual reports describing infrastructure condition (e.g., pavement condition indices, water-main break logs where published); local news coverage of specific infrastructure failures.

**Feasibility:** `likely_hard` — this requires linking a staffing-strain period to a later capital-spending response with some confidence about causal timing, which is a more demanding evidentiary task than most other gaps on this list; best treated as a longer-term research question rather than a near-term desk-research closure.

**Dimension:** budget constraints.

## 9. Classification and pay-grade structures

**Why it matters:** the single most direct way to test whether DPW credentials (hoisting license, water/wastewater certification) actually correspond to a higher pay grade in practice, as hypothesized in Section 7 of the refinement memo, rather than assumed from the general logic of classification systems.

**Likely source families:** DPW CBAs and published salary/classification schedules (often appended to union contracts); this project's own already-collected `public_works` occupation-class contracts, which may already contain this information without new ingestion.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` — this is likely the single easiest gap on this list to close, since it only requires reviewing documents this project may have already collected, not new source acquisition.

**Dimension:** classification / wage levels.

## 10. Private-sector wage competition

**Why it matters:** needed to test whether DPW wage-schedule changes track local private-sector construction/trucking/trades wage indices, as hypothesized in Section 4 of the refinement memo, which would support a "DPW wages respond to an observable external market" reading distinct from the institutional/political mechanisms hypothesized for police/fire.

**Likely source families:** BLS OEWS construction/trucking/trades occupational wage data (source context only, not to be processed or built into a panel per this session's hard boundaries); Massachusetts-specific trade/construction wage surveys if available.

**Feasibility:** `likely_hard` within this project's current scope, since any real test would require the OEWS-adjacent descriptive baseline work that is explicitly out of bounds for desk-research sessions; flagged here as a future, not immediate, task.

**Dimension:** wage levels / wage growth.

## 11. Union bargaining language

**Why it matters:** needed to confirm, rather than assume, whether Massachusetts DPW bargaining documents actually invoke recruitment/retention, hazard, comparability, or ability-to-pay language, and in what proportion relative to the police/fire and teacher documents already reviewed in prior sessions.

**Likely source families:** this project's own already-collected `public_works` CBAs; Massachusetts municipal union (e.g., AFSCME, Teamsters, or laborers' union locals commonly representing DPW workers) public statements if available.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` — same logic as gap 9, this project may already hold relevant documents without new ingestion.

**Dimension:** wage levels / classification.

## 12. Peer-community comparisons

**Why it matters:** tests whether DPW bargaining invokes "surrounding" or "comparable" community wage comparisons the way teacher bargaining does (per the teacher peer-district comparability hypothesis), and whether that comparator language is backed by any institutional enforcement mechanism or functions as persuasive rhetoric only, mirroring the question already resolved for teachers via the JLMC-vs-Chapter-150E-Section-9 distinction.

**Likely source families:** DPW CBAs and bargaining materials; Massachusetts DLR impasse records if any DPW bargaining unit has gone to fact-finding.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` if this project's already-collected `public_works` CBAs contain comparator language; `desk_research_feasible` for confirming whether DPW bargaining follows the same general Chapter 150E Section 9 process as teachers (this is also listed as an open item in the roadmap memo update from this session, since it was not directly re-verified for public works specifically).

**Dimension:** wage levels / classification.

## 13. Massachusetts examples vs. national context

**Why it matters:** this session relied heavily on national CDL/trucking and national/regional (NEIWPCC) water-workforce sources, with comparatively thin Massachusetts-city-specific confirmation beyond the general hoisting-license and prevailing-wage institutional facts. Future work should be explicit about which claims are Massachusetts-verified versus nationally-plausible-but-unverified for Massachusetts specifically, consistent with the discipline already applied in this memo.

**Likely source families:** individual Massachusetts city/town DPW annual reports, budget books, and CBAs (illustrative, not comprehensive, per this session's scope instruction); Massachusetts Municipal Association materials.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` for a small number of illustrative Massachusetts examples; explicitly not `desk_research_feasible` at a comprehensive statewide level, consistent with this session's scope boundary against comprehensive statewide coverage.

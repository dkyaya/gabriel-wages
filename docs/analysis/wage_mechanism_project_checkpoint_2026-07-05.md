# Wage Mechanism Project Checkpoint — 2026-07-05

**Type:** dated strategic synthesis memo. Evaluates the project after the mechanism-expansion phase (teachers, DPW, clerical/admin), the national municipal workforce scan, the public-sector impasse/arbitration state-law citation audit, and the two-session metadata cleanup (audit + application + Somerville follow-up). Recommends next inspection priorities.

## 1. Purpose and scope

This memo takes stock of what this project has learned about wage-setting mechanisms across police, fire, teachers, DPW/public works, and clerical/admin — nationally and in Massachusetts — and recommends which non-safety occupation groups to inspect next at the same depth (mechanism refinement → institutional/impasse verification → full corpus scan) already applied to the first three non-safety groups. It synthesizes roughly a dozen prior memos rather than introducing new research.

**This memo does not make a causal claim about why police/fire wages rise faster than other municipal occupations.** Every finding below is a mechanism hypothesis, an institutional fact, or a description of what evidence this project currently has or lacks — not a demonstrated causal effect. It also does not authorize any GABRIEL run, model/API call, OEWS/BLS build, new ingestion, or PRR; those decisions remain separate and are explicitly deferred to §11.

## 2. Current project architecture

The design holds city and calendar time fixed and lets occupation vary, comparing wage-setting across five occupation groups this project has now mapped in some depth, plus a set of cross-cutting institutional mechanisms that can apply to more than one group:

- **Police** — the first safety-side group; recruitment/retention, overtime/staffing, comparator-ratchet, and arbitration/impasse-backstop mechanisms developed across three memos (`police_fire_wage_mechanism_context_2026-07-02.md`, `police_fire_workforce_context_refinement_2026-07-03.md`, plus the national scan and citation audit).
- **Fire** — developed alongside police throughout, with its own distinct mechanisms (career/volunteer split, EMS credential growth) layered on top of the shared police/fire institutional backstop (JLMC in Massachusetts).
- **Teachers/school employees** — the first non-safety comparison group (`non_safety_teacher_wage_mechanism_refinement_2026-07-04.md` and its institutional-verification companion), chosen because teachers share the most features with the safety side (credentialing, unionization, political salience, public esteem), making muted wage translation for teachers a hard, informative test.
- **DPW/public works** — the second non-safety group (`non_safety_dpw_public_works_wage_mechanism_refinement_2026-07-04.md` and its corpus-scan/institutional companions), chosen as the strongest *operational* (physical risk, licensure, emergency response) comparison, isolating operational similarity from safety's institutional/political advantages.
- **Clerical/admin** — the third non-safety group (`non_safety_clerical_admin_wage_mechanism_refinement_2026-07-05.md` and its corpus-scan/institutional companions), chosen as the "plainest" comparison — lacking teachers' credentialing/salience cover and DPW's operational/licensing cover — to isolate what pure wage-setting institutions (classification review, internal equity, budget competition) can restrain on their own.
- **Cross-cutting institutional mechanisms** — arbitration/impasse-backstop type (compulsory vs. voluntary), comparator/peer-wage logic, overtime/staffing spiral, classification/reclassification systems, service deferral/buffering, contractor substitution, budget-capacity/fiscal-constraint effects, public salience, and source-document production effects — all now tracked centrally in `wage_mechanism_evidence_checklist.md` §8.

**The two-sided comparison, restated precisely:** the police/fire-vs-non-safety wage gap this project studies is a difference of two rates of change, and it can widen for at least three non-exclusive reasons — (a) safety wages accelerate through mechanisms like recruitment scarcity, overtime spirals, comparator ratchets, and the JLMC compulsory-arbitration backstop; (b) non-safety wages are restrained through weaker or alternate translation channels — classification systems that absorb pressure administratively, shortage-buffering that substitutes cheaper labor or degraded service rather than wages, budget structures specific to each occupation, and the shared absence of a JLMC-equivalent backstop; or (c) both happen at once. Every mechanism memo before `non_safety_teacher_wage_mechanism_refinement_2026-07-04.md` addressed only term (a); the three non-safety mechanism memos since then have been this project's first systematic attempt at term (b).

## 3. What we now think, by occupation group

### Police

- **Main upward mechanisms:** recruitment/retention strain (real, per MissionSquare/SLGE, but not the single hardest local-government occupation — mental health, nursing, and corrections all rank harder to fill); minimum-staffing/overtime spirals; academy/training bottlenecks; peer-community comparator ratchets, statutorily mandated for JLMC arbitrators to weigh; the JLMC compulsory-interest-arbitration backstop itself.
- **Main restraint/alternate mechanisms:** none specific to police — police sit on the "upward pressure" side of this project's two-sided framing, though a within-department comparison (e.g., patrol vs. command staff) has not been developed.
- **Current corpus evidence:** confirmed in current corpus for JLMC citation/award text (Somerville SPSOA/SPEA awards, Wayland-adjacent police rows); comparator language confirmed in the Somerville awards specifically, now correctly captured after this session's metadata cleanup.
- **External/national evidence:** MissionSquare/SLGE 2024/2025 staffing surveys; compulsory police/fire arbitration confirmed (citation-audited) in NJ, NY, MI, RI, PA, OK, IL, and fire-only in WY.
- **Massachusetts-specific caveats:** JLMC itself (Chapter 589 of the Acts of 1987) is Massachusetts's own implementation of a broader multi-state policy pattern, not the pattern itself; Massachusetts paid-detail income is a distinct compensation channel from base wage and overtime, specific to Massachusetts/Northeast practice.
- **Unresolved gaps:** a direct (non-secondary) PERF/IACP multi-year applicant-trend citation; minimum-staffing clause text has not been directly reviewed in this project's own police CBAs; the post-2020 compound-shock channels (legitimacy/scrutiny vs. COVID disruption vs. retirement timing vs. Great Resignation) remain analytically separated but not empirically disentangled with this project's own data.

### Fire

- **Main upward mechanisms:** EMS/paramedic call-volume growth (confirmed in current corpus — Arlington's EMT stipend); volunteer-to-career transition pressure, now nationally quantified (NFPA: 897,750 volunteer firefighters in 1984 to 676,900 in 2020, a 25% decline against ~40% U.S. population growth); the same JLMC backstop as police.
- **Main restraint/alternate mechanisms:** the career/volunteer structural split itself restrains any single national "firefighter wage" narrative, since volunteer departments have no negotiated base wage to measure; combination-department conversion absorbs pressure before it reaches a career CBA base wage.
- **Current corpus evidence:** confirmed for JLMC (every fire row's `binding_arbitration_statute` cites MA G.L. c. 1078); confirmed for EMS stipend (Arlington); Wayland's JLMC stipulated award is a full document in corpus but its comparator-specific language has not yet been extracted in detail (still `weak evidence` per the checklist).
- **External/national evidence:** NFPA's volunteer-decline data is now the best-quantified national mechanism found anywhere in this scan; IAFF documents the all-career-conversion adaptation directly.
- **Massachusetts-specific caveats:** same JLMC caveat as police; most identified multi-state compulsory-arbitration statutes cover police and fire jointly, but Wyoming's applies to fire only, with no comparable police statute found.
- **Unresolved gaps:** EMS/paramedic credential scarcity beyond the Arlington example; minimum-staffing clause text not directly reviewed; whether firefighter applicant-to-seat ratios are genuinely higher than police's (a candidate explanation for the fire/police median-pay gap) remains unverified.

### Teachers/school employees

- **Main upward mechanisms:** documented national vacancy/hard-to-fill patterns (NCES: 74% of schools reported difficulty filling ≥1 vacancy for 2024-25); shortage concentration in special ed/STEM/bilingual-ESL; some states (Iowa, confirmed via primary source) extend compulsory arbitration to teachers, unlike Massachusetts.
- **Main restraint/alternate mechanisms:** step-and-lane salary-schedule rigidity (does not differentiate pay by subject/grade absent a deliberate stipend add-on, though ~3 in 4 sampled districts per NCTQ do add one); shortage buffering via long-term substitutes, emergency certification, larger classes, or program cuts — structurally *not* wage-adjacent, unlike police/fire overtime; the compulsory-vs-voluntary arbitration distinction (JLMC orders arbitration; Chapter 150E §9 gives teachers only mediation + advisory fact-finding + potential unilateral last-best-offer implementation, with a voluntary arbitration route that exists on paper but is rare in practice); a two-sided budget constraint (a state-mandated net-school-spending floor plus the same Proposition 2½ levy-limit ceiling other departments face) most other municipal departments, including police/fire, do not have on the floor side.
- **Current corpus evidence:** thin — only one `teacher` row (Seekonk), confirmed to keep teacher aides explicitly outside the bargaining unit (no TA/teacher merge risk); no comparator or arbitration language reviewed in depth from this row yet.
- **External/national evidence:** the strongest external-sources base of any occupation group in this project — NCES/IES, Learning Policy Institute, NCTQ, DESE, Mass.gov DLR all directly verified.
- **Massachusetts-specific caveats:** Chapter 70's specific 82.5% required-local-contribution cap and exact net-school-spending definition are Massachusetts-specific parameters within a nationally dominant foundation-formula architecture (confirmed comparable in NJ, WI); the voluntary-only Massachusetts arbitration finding does not generalize — Iowa's Code §20.22 is a confirmed, primary-source-verified, general (not teacher-specific) compulsory-arbitration statute that also carries its own 2017-reform safety-favoring asymmetry via bargaining-scope/arbitration-criteria restriction rather than arbitration-availability restriction.
- **Unresolved gaps:** city-specific override/net-school-spending-compliance history for this project's own cities; grade/subject composition data for this project's cities (needed before any raw average-wage comparison to police/fire); whether Seekonk's own teacher CBA contains peer-district comparator language (not yet reviewed for this specific document).

### DPW/public works

- **Main upward mechanisms:** classification-tied credential pay, richly corpus-confirmed (Arlington's CDL-class-tied MEO grades; Franklin's tiered biweekly stipend schedule for CDL/hoisting/water-wastewater/pesticide/ASE certifications); water/wastewater operator certification as the strongest genuine scarcity case (a documented national and New England-regional "silver tsunami" retirement wave, 30-50% of the workforce eligible to retire within 5-10 years); storm/emergency response, contractually confirmed across every fully-readable `public_works` row.
- **Main restraint/alternate mechanisms:** contractor substitution (confirmed corpus-wide, ranging from Arlington's unconditional management right to Franklin's two heavily-conditioned side letters), with a Massachusetts-specific prevailing-wage-law wrinkle (contracted public-construction wages tied to locally bargained rates, so contracting out does not simply undercut the substituted work's wage floor); classification fragmentation (DPW spans laborers, several equipment-operator tiers, licensed water/wastewater operators, mechanics, and foremen — a wider skill/licensing spread than teachers' grade/subject spread); the ordinary Chapter 150E §9 impasse route, confirmed both externally and by this project's own corpus (every `public_works` row's `binding_arbitration_statute` cites MA G.L. c. 150E; every arbitration clause found is grievance/discipline-scoped, most explicitly in Seekonk's "Final binding arbitration will prevail on grievances only").
- **Current corpus evidence:** the richest non-safety corpus evidence of the three groups — seven fully-reviewed `public_works` rows across five cities, with detailed credential-to-pay, overtime/storm, and contractor-substitution language confirmed, but a genuine, confirmed *absence* of peer-community wage-comparability and explicit recruitment/retention framing language in every document reviewed.
- **External/national evidence:** the CDL/truck-driver "shortage" is **contradicted** at the national labor-economics level (BLS's own 2019 Monthly Labor Review found no evidence of a secular shortage, with real driver wages up only ~1.1% since 2010) — a genuine counterexample worth foregrounding, not a confirmation; water/wastewater retirement-wave data is confirmed national plus New England-regional (NEIWPCC).
- **Massachusetts-specific caveats:** the prevailing-wage-law contractor-substitution wrinkle is Massachusetts-specific and has not been verified for other states; DPW's "no JLMC access" finding presumes bargaining rights exist at all, which is false in NC and SC and was false in VA pre-2020.
- **Unresolved gaps:** actual vacancy/applicant data for any DPW classification in this project's cities; whether DPW overtime spending in any specific city shows the "chronic overtime as planning problem" pattern municipal risk-management guidance warns against; private-sector wage-index tracking for DPW trades (explicitly deferred, since it would require the OEWS-adjacent build this project has not authorized).

### Clerical/admin

- **Main upward mechanisms:** general labor-market/cost-of-living pressure shared with all occupations; reclassification pressure as a real, corpus-confirmed channel (Boston's Compensation Grade Appeals process); minimum-wage-driven pay compression, with a directly quantified, Massachusetts-inclusive multi-state cost estimate (Century Foundation: ~0.6% weighted-average annual payroll cost across six jurisdictions including Massachusetts).
- **Main restraint/alternate mechanisms:** the most procedurally restrictive reclassification/dispute-resolution mechanism found anywhere in this project's entire corpus — Boston's Compensation Grade Appeals process limits any arbitrator to an "arbitrary or capricious" standard (not a full merits review), explicitly excludes workload/technology-driven duty changes as valid reclassification grounds (stated twice, Articles 30 and 33), and provides no retroactive remedy; classification-and-internal-equity systems that can span the entire non-safety municipal workforce at once; four distinct headcount-reduction (not wage-reduction) channels — part-time staffing, automation/digitization, reorganization/consolidation, and Massachusetts's own documented Efficiency and Regionalization shared-services grant program; the same Chapter 150E §9 route as teachers and DPW, now the most directly confirmed of the three (the MMA Select Board Handbook explicitly names "clerical and administrative employees" as JLMC-ineligible, rather than requiring inference from a general rule); a newly-discovered "confidential employee" exclusion (administrative staff closest to Select Board/town-manager leadership are excluded from bargaining entirely, with pay set unilaterally) — a wage-setting channel not documented for any other occupation group in this project.
- **Current corpus evidence:** three rows fully reviewed (Worcester, Boston, Seekonk); Boston is by far the richest single document in this project's entire corpus for classification/reclassification mechanism detail; Seekonk is confirmed to be a school-based unit, not general-municipal; peer-community comparability and pay-compression language are confirmed *absent* from all three documents.
- **External/national evidence:** the general civil-service classification-and-appeal architecture is confirmed as a national norm (NY, NJ, federal OPM, Massachusetts's own state Civil Service Commission all share it) — Boston's *specific* narrow "arbitrary or capricious" standard has not been benchmarked against another city's equivalent, remaining an open comparison; gendered occupational valuation ("comparable worth") is now academically well-anchored (Paula England, IWPR, a CRS report noting comparable-worth policy "has made the most headway in state and local governments") but explicitly remains an unevidenced hypothesis for this project's own cities, per repeated, deliberate task instruction not to upgrade its status.
- **Massachusetts-specific caveats:** the "confidential employee" exclusion category is documented via the MMA Handbook but not tested against other states; Boston's specific arbitration-standard narrowness is a city-specific parameter, not a Massachusetts-wide or national one.
- **Unresolved gaps:** actual reclassification-request outcome data (approval rates, dollar impact) is `likely_hard` — internal HR data not published in CBA text; workload/backlog service-level data for any specific Massachusetts municipality; whether Worcester's and Seekonk's shorter contracts have any comparable-but-less-formal reclassification apparatus.

## 4. New or refined hypotheses since the original comparability focus

The hypothesis matrix has grown from an original comparability/arbitration-centered map (the `comparability_emphasis` v9 and `arbitration_or_impasse_backstop` v10 GABRIEL attribute candidates) to 27 rows spanning both sides of the two-term gap framing:

- **Recruitment/retention/labor scarcity (H1):** sharpened from a police-specific claim into a calibrated, cross-occupation finding — police staffing difficulty is real (68% hard-to-fill, MissionSquare 2024) but not the single hardest local-government occupation (mental health 83%, nursing 77%, corrections 74% all rank higher).
- **Post-2020 police compound shock (H2):** explicitly disaggregated into five separable channels (legitimacy/scrutiny, COVID-era disruption, retirement-eligibility timing, Great Resignation, alternative-opportunity growth) that require distinct evidence rather than being treated as one bundled "post-2020" story.
- **Firefighter career/volunteer/EMS mechanisms (H11, FD03, FD04):** the volunteer-to-career transition is now a three-channel model (combination-department conversion, overtime/minimum-staffing substitution, full professionalization) with a national quantitative anchor (NFPA); EMS/paramedic credential growth is corpus-confirmed for at least one city (Arlington's stipend).
- **Teacher supply pressure and shortage buffering (H13, H14):** added as a genuinely new hypothesis pair, establishing the project's first systematic non-safety-side mechanism — shortage exists (NCES/LPI) but is structurally more likely to be absorbed via substitutes/emergency certification/class size than converted into base-wage growth, unlike police/fire overtime buffering.
- **Teacher composition and salary schedule rigidity (H15, H16):** a measurement-discipline pair guarding against naive average-wage comparisons (grade/subject/experience mix) and documenting the step-and-lane structure's default inability to differentiate pay by subject-specific shortage absent a deliberate add-on (~3 in 4 districts per NCTQ do add one).
- **DPW operational essentiality and license/classification/overtime mechanisms (H18-H22):** five new rows establishing DPW as the strongest *operational* non-safety comparison, with the CDL-scarcity claim explicitly flagged as contested/contradicted (BLS) while the water/wastewater-operator claim is treated as the stronger genuine case.
- **Clerical/admin classification, reclassification, compression, and backlog absorption (H23-H27):** five new rows establishing clerical/admin as the "plainest" comparison, isolating pure wage-setting-institution effects from any credentialing/salience/operational cover.
- **Public-safety institutional privilege/arbitration/impasse backstop (H6, XC09):** transformed from a Massachusetts-only observation into the single most thoroughly cross-verified finding in the project — independently confirmed for teachers, DPW, and clerical/admin (all three: Chapter 150E §9, no JLMC access), citation-audited for 8 other states' police/fire statutes, and now carrying two documented complications (Iowa's general-not-teacher-specific arbitration statute with its own scope/criteria-based safety asymmetry; Wisconsin's active equal-protection legal challenge to the safety/non-safety bargaining distinction itself).
- **Source-document production effects (XC14, national scan §10):** elevated from a single caution in the original v9 memo into a corpus-wide, twice-independently-replicated finding — both the DPW and clerical/admin corpus scans found the identical absence (no recruitment/retention framing, no peer-comparability language) in every document reviewed, suggesting this is a general property of CBA text as a source type, not a Massachusetts or occupation-specific gap.
- **Non-safety wage restraint (H17):** the umbrella hypothesis unifying all three non-safety mechanism memos, now supported by three independent occupation-specific institutional verifications rather than a single teacher-only finding, plus Iowa's citation-audited illustration of a *different* wage-restraint lever (bargaining-scope/arbitration-criteria restriction rather than arbitration-availability restriction) than anything in this project's own Massachusetts corpus.

## 5. National-level nuance

**Appears broadly national:**
- Police/fire staffing pressure is real but not uniquely severe among local-government occupations (MissionSquare/SLGE, confirmed multi-year).
- Public-safety arbitration/impasse privilege is a recurring *pattern* — primary-source-confirmed in NJ, NY, MI, RI, PA, OK, IL, and fire-only in WY — but is genuinely state-varying, not a majority-of-states finding, and its specific vehicles (JLMC, PERC programs, Act 312, etc.) are each state-specific.
- Teacher shortages and certification/substitute buffering are confirmed national patterns (NCES, LPI, NCTQ); the foundation-formula school-finance architecture behind Chapter 70 is the dominant national model (Urban Institute, with NJ and WI as confirmed structural parallels).
- DPW water/wastewater workforce concerns are a confirmed national-plus-New-England-regional (NEIWPCC) retirement-wave pattern; the CDL "shortage" narrative is contradicted at the national labor-economics level (BLS 2019).
- Clerical/admin classification/reclassification logic (a formal classification office plus an appeal process) is a confirmed national civil-service norm (NY, NJ, federal OPM, Massachusetts's own state Civil Service Commission).
- Local-government staffing and compensation constraints (Proposition-2½-style levy limits, general local-government recruitment/retention pressure) are structurally common nationally, though exact parameters (levy caps, minimum-wage-compression cost percentages) are state-specific.

**Should not be generalized too broadly:** JLMC itself, Chapter 70's 82.5% cap and net-school-spending definition, Proposition 2½'s specific 2.5% mechanics, the MMA Select Board Handbook, and Boston's specific "OHR Classification and Compensation Unit" are all named Massachusetts (or Boston-specific) institutions whose *pattern* may generalize but whose *exact parameters and vehicle names* do not. The national scan's own design guidance (§12 of `national_municipal_workforce_mechanism_scan_2026-07-05.md`) is explicit on this: code the institutional pattern as the primary variable, the named institution as a secondary, jurisdiction-identifying field — never the reverse.

## 6. State-level nuance

- **Massachusetts JLMC is specific, but public-safety arbitration variants recur elsewhere** — primary-source-confirmed in NJ (N.J.S.A. 34:13A-14 to -21), NY (Civil Service Law §209.4), MI (Act 312 of 1969), RI (Gen. Laws Ch. 28-9.1/28-9.2/28-9.5), PA (Act 111 of 1968), OK (Title 11 §51-101), IL (5 ILCS 315/14), and WY (fire only, W.S. §§27-10-101 to -109).
- **Chapter 70/Proposition 2½ are Massachusetts-specific school-finance institutions, though school-finance constraints are national** — the foundation-formula architecture is the dominant U.S. model; the specific 82.5%/2.5% parameters are not.
- **Iowa complicates the non-safety arbitration story** — Iowa Code §20.22 is a confirmed, primary-source-verified, general (not teacher-specific) compulsory arbitration statute available to essentially all covered public employees since the 1970s, and its 2017 reform (House File 291) added a *different* safety-favoring asymmetry (bargaining-scope and arbitration-criteria restriction for units under 30% public-safety composition) than anything in this project's own Massachusetts corpus. Iowa both confirms and complicates the "non-safety lacks compulsory arbitration" finding.
- **North Carolina/South Carolina-style bargaining restrictions complicate national comparisons** — NC's total public-sector bargaining prohibition (G.S. 95-98, 1959) is primary-confirmed with high confidence; SC's is only secondary-source-confirmed (Ballotpedia, CEPR, a multi-state legal tracker) and remains the weakest-sourced entry in the citation audit. Virginia's 2020/2021 local-option reform (Va. Code §40.1-57.2) shows bargaining-rights status itself can change materially by state legislative action.
- **Wisconsin/Act 10 public-safety asymmetry is legally/politically unstable and needs caution** — a December 2024 Dane County ruling struck down Act 10's police/fire bargaining exemption as an equal-protection violation under the Wisconsin Constitution; this project's Massachusetts JLMC finding faces no comparable legal challenge identified in this project's research, but Wisconsin is a live reminder that the public-safety/non-safety institutional distinction is not treated as self-evidently constitutional everywhere it appears.
- **Wyoming is fire-specific in the current audit unless police evidence is later found** — the confirmed statute (W.S. §§27-10-101 to -109) is titled "Collective Bargaining for Fire Fighters"; no comparable Wyoming police statute was found in either research session, which should be read as "not found," not "confirmed absent."
- **Collective bargaining institutions vary substantially across states** — from Rhode Island's unusually granular three-chapter structure (separate for municipal police, state police, and fire) to Illinois's broader-than-usual covered category ("security employees" plus fire-department paramedics) to states where the underlying right to bargain at all does not exist. Any future multi-state extension of this project's work must check each target state's basic bargaining-rights status before assuming a CBA-based mechanism map is even viable there.

## 8. What the current corpus can and cannot show

- **CBAs are strong for** formal wage schedules, classifications, credential-tied premiums, overtime/callback rules, grievance procedures, and management-rights language — this project's 32-row corpus demonstrates this richly across all five occupation groups, especially DPW (contractor/credential language) and clerical/admin (Boston's classification apparatus).
- **Arbitration/JLMC/factfinding documents are stronger for explicit wage-setting reasoning** — the two Somerville JLMC awards are the only documents in this corpus that state statutory ability-to-pay and peer-wage-comparator criteria explicitly, precisely because the enabling statute directs the arbitrator to weigh and write about them. This is a *document-production* effect, not evidence that comparator reasoning is absent from non-safety bargaining in practice — it may simply not be preserved in text absent a statutory requirement to do so.
- **School committee/budget/HR documents may be needed for teacher supply and budget mechanisms** — this project's single teacher CBA (Seekonk) is confirmed to contain very little of the shortage/comparator/budget reasoning that the strongest teacher-side evidence (NCES, LPI, DESE Chapter 70 pages) actually comes from external sources, not the CBA itself.
- **DPW CBAs show contract mechanics but not necessarily recruitment narratives** — confirmed twice over (this project's own corpus scan and the national scan's independent observation): every DPW document reviewed has rich classification/credential/overtime/contractor language but zero recruitment/retention framing or peer-comparability language.
- **Clerical/admin CBAs show classification/reclassification channels but not necessarily workload/backlog evidence** — Boston's CGA process is extensively documented in its own contract text, but no document in this project's corpus contains backlog, reduced-hours, or cross-training language; the closest adjacent finding is that Boston's CGA process explicitly excludes workload increases as valid reclassification grounds, which is evidence about how workload does *not* convert to pay, not evidence about how workload shortfalls are operationally absorbed.
- **Current discourse corpus is empty** — `data/discourse.csv` has 0 rows. Every mechanism finding to date rests entirely on the causal corpus (CBAs/awards) or external desk research; no news, budget-narrative, or academic-commentary text keyed to this project's own cities has been collected. This is a genuine, structural gap for any mechanism (political salience, public framing of a specific staffing crisis) that would primarily live in discourse rather than causal text.
- **The existing matched-pair design remains useful but does not solve source-type production effects** — the coverage audit shows 12 healthy matched pairs (9 exact-cycle, 3 overlap-cycle) and 3 unmatched safety units, which is sufficient scaffolding for the city×time-fixed comparison this project's design requires, but matching cities and cycles does not by itself correct for the fact that police/fire documents (arbitration awards) are a structurally richer source type for wage-setting reasoning than any non-safety document type collected so far.

## 11. Decisions needed from user/PI

1. **Whether to include sanitation as its own comparison group or fold it conceptually into DPW.** The schema already has both as separate controlled-vocabulary values, but sanitation is often organizationally bundled with DPW in practice (this project's own Wayland "other" row bundles DPW-adjacent functions already); a decision here affects whether sanitation gets its own mechanism memo or is treated as a DPW sub-case.
2. **Whether to include transit given governance differences.** Transit in Massachusetts is often organized regionally (MBTA, regional transit authorities) rather than by a single municipality, which may not fit this project's one-city-one-bargaining-unit design cleanly — needs a decision on whether a regionally-bargained unit can be treated as "one city's" observation for this project's purposes, or whether transit should be deferred indefinitely.
3. **Whether to treat public health/nurses as separate from municipal general government.** Given the population-mismatch risk flagged in §9 (most nursing labor-market evidence is hospital-based, not municipal), a decision is needed on whether this project should pursue municipal/public-health nursing specifically (a narrower, possibly harder-to-find comparison) or deprioritize this group indefinitely.
4. **Whether to prioritize source acquisition or a descriptive wage baseline next.** The roadmap and this memo both lean toward continuing mechanism development (library, then sanitation) before any OEWS/ASPEP descriptive build, but this is a resource-allocation choice for the user/PI to confirm, not a decision this memo can make unilaterally.
5. **Whether/when to restart GABRIEL attribute work.** Every mechanism memo to date, including this one, has recommended deferring GABRIEL/model-scoring work until the source base is more stable across occupation groups; a decision is needed on whether that recommendation should now be revisited (e.g., piloting an attribute on the police/fire side, where the corpus and mechanism map are most mature) or held further pending the library/sanitation work above.
6. **Whether to add new controlled-vocabulary occupation_class values (custodial/facilities) or continue coding such units as `other`.** Two rows already exist coded as `other` that are, in substance, custodial units — a decision is needed on whether this is an acceptable permanent home for them or whether a schema change is warranted.
7. **How to treat dispatchers analytically.** This project's own corpus already shows dispatchers embedded, unlabeled, inside a `public_works`-classified bargaining unit (Arlington). A decision is needed on whether dispatchers should ever become their own occupation class, remain invisibly embedded within whatever class happens to bundle them, or be explicitly flagged wherever they appear as a composition caveat — this is a genuine boundary case for the safety/non-safety framing this project's entire design depends on.

## 12. Bottom line

This project has moved, in roughly two weeks of desk-research sessions, from a single-sided police/fire mechanism map anchored in one Massachusetts institution (JLMC) to a five-occupation-group, two-sided comparison framework with independently verified institutional findings for teachers, DPW, and clerical/admin, a citation-audited multi-state legal comparison for the police/fire arbitration backstop, and a now-clean, actively-audited metadata layer underneath all of it. The single most robust finding to date is not about any one occupation's wage level — it is structural: Massachusetts's police/fire compulsory-arbitration backstop (JLMC) is confirmed, three separate times, to have no non-safety equivalent in this project's own city set, and this pattern is now known (via the citation audit and national scan) to recur, in varying forms, in a genuine but bounded set of other states, while remaining genuinely complicated by at least two documented counterexamples (Iowa's general arbitration statute; Wisconsin's currently-contested safety/non-safety distinction). The clearest near-term opportunity is low-cost, not speculative: the `library` occupation class already has three collected, coverage-matched corpus rows sitting unexamined at the same depth already applied to teachers, DPW, and clerical/admin, and completing that pass is the single cheapest way to extend the two-sided comparison to a fourth non-safety group before any new source acquisition, descriptive-baseline build, or GABRIEL work is even considered.

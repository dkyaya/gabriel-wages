# Teacher/Non-Safety Source Gap List

**Date:** 2026-07-04
**Scope:** desk-research planning only. Lists the highest-value evidence gaps surfaced by `non_safety_teacher_wage_mechanism_refinement_2026-07-04.md`. Several items below already have a partial public source identified during this session's bounded web search; those are noted. This is a punch list for future sessions, not a completed literature review or data build.

For each gap: why it matters, likely source families, a feasibility tag (`desk_research_feasible`, `data_build_feasible`, `likely_hard`), and which dimension it speaks to (wage levels, wage growth, staffing supply, composition, salary schedules, or budget constraints).

## 1. Teacher applicant counts over time

**Why it matters:** distinguishes an applicant-volume shortage from an applicant-qualification/certification shortage (NCES's own 2024-25 data already separates "too few candidates" at 62% from "lack of qualified candidates" at 64%, but a multi-year applicant-count series by district or state is still needed to see the trend, not just a snapshot).

**Likely source families:** NCES/IES School Pulse Panel (snapshot only, not a long time series); state teacher-licensure/certification boards; individual district HR application-tracking data; Learning Policy Institute state-level teacher-supply analyses.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` for the existing national snapshot (already partially closed this session — see refinement memo Section 3); `data_build_feasible` for a Massachusetts-specific multi-year applicant series if DESE or individual districts publish one; `likely_hard` for a clean nationally comparable time series, since applicant counts are not centrally or consistently reported the way vacancy counts are.

**Dimension:** staffing supply.

## 2. Teacher vacancy rates

**Why it matters:** the most direct, already-partially-available measure of teacher staffing strain, and the natural teacher-side analog to police/fire vacancy data used elsewhere in this project's mechanism map.

**Likely source families:** NCES/IES School Pulse Panel (national, snapshot-style); Massachusetts DESE staffing reports; individual district staffing/HR reports.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` — national-level vacancy-difficulty data already identified this session (74% of schools reporting difficulty filling at least one vacancy for 2024-25, per IES); `data_build_feasible` for assembling a Massachusetts-specific, multi-year, district-level vacancy series if DESE publishes one in a comparable format year over year.

**Dimension:** staffing supply.

## 3. Under-certified/emergency-certified teacher shares

**Why it matters:** this is the clearest quantitative signature of shortage buffering discussed in Section 6 of the refinement memo — a rising share of positions filled by not-fully-certified teachers is direct evidence that a shortage is being absorbed through a credentialing workaround rather than (or in addition to) a wage response.

**Likely source families:** NCES/IES School Pulse Panel (already provides a national snapshot: 79% of reported vacancies were filled with fully certified teachers for 2024-25); state teacher-licensure boards' emergency/provisional/waiver-certification issuance data; Massachusetts DESE educator credentialing data if available at the district level.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` for the national snapshot (already partially closed this session); `data_build_feasible` for a Massachusetts district-level, multi-year series if DESE publishes emergency/provisional licensure counts by district and year.

**Dimension:** staffing supply / composition.

## 4. Long-term substitute use

**Why it matters:** the second clearest quantitative signature of shortage buffering — sustained long-term substitute placements (as opposed to short-term absence coverage) indicate a position is effectively unfilled by a certified teacher of record for an extended period, without necessarily showing up as a formal "vacancy."

**Likely source families:** district budget line items for substitute pay; school committee staffing reports; state education agency data where collected; local news coverage of specific district staffing gaps.

**Feasibility:** `likely_hard` at a systematic level — long-term substitute usage is not consistently or centrally reported the way vacancy counts are, and would likely require district-by-district budget or HR document review; `data_build_feasible` only if this project narrows to a small number of its existing cities and reviews their budget documents directly.

**Dimension:** staffing supply.

## 5. Grade-level composition

**Why it matters:** directly needed to test the composition-effect hypothesis in Section 4 of the refinement memo — whether a district's elementary/middle/secondary mix affects its reported average teacher salary independent of the underlying salary schedule.

**Likely source families:** Massachusetts DESE staffing/FTE reports (the DESE Directory of Datasets and Reports and School and District Profiles pages list FTE-by-category data); district HR/personnel records; NCES Common Core of Data staffing files at the national level.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` — Massachusetts DESE's public profiles pages already appear to carry FTE-level staffing detail; assembling grade-band composition for this project's current cities is a realistic near-term build, smaller in scope than the full OEWS/ASPEP baseline.

**Dimension:** composition.

## 6. Subject-area composition

**Why it matters:** same logic as grade-level composition, but for subject mix (e.g., a district with a larger special-education or bilingual/ESL caseload relative to general-education classroom teachers), which also bears directly on the shortage-area stipend/differentiated-pay hypothesis in Section 5 of the refinement memo.

**Likely source families:** Massachusetts DESE educator/staffing profiles; district HR data; state licensure-endorsement records where subject-area credentials are tracked.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` for Massachusetts DESE data if subject-area detail is available at the district level; `likely_hard` for a nationally comparable subject-composition series.

**Dimension:** composition.

## 7. Special education, STEM, and bilingual/ESL shortages specifically

**Why it matters:** these are the three shortage areas most consistently flagged by both NCES/IES and the Learning Policy Institute (special ed in 45 states, science in 41, math in 40 per LPI's most recent scan; special ed and ESL/bilingual specifically flagged as hardest-to-fill by grade band per the 2024-25 NCES School Pulse Panel), so they are the highest-priority subject areas for any future teacher-side mechanism attribute.

**Likely source families:** Learning Policy Institute state-by-state shortage area lists; NCES/IES School Pulse Panel; Massachusetts DESE if it publishes a comparable state-specific shortage-area list (Massachusetts's own shortage-area designations, if any, would need direct verification — not confirmed this session).

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` — largely already closed at the national level this session (see refinement memo Section 3); a Massachusetts-specific shortage-area list, if DESE publishes one, would need direct verification in a future session.

**Dimension:** staffing supply / composition.

## 8. Salary schedule structure

**Why it matters:** needed to actually test whether a given district's schedule is a pure step-and-lane structure or includes subject/grade differentiation, hard-to-fill stipends, or extra-step credit for shortage subjects — the core evidence question in Section 5 of the refinement memo.

**Likely source families:** individual district CBAs and published salary schedules (often appended to teacher union contracts and publicly posted by districts or unions); NCTQ's compensation-strategy database and reports, which already provide some cross-district aggregation.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` — this project already collects CBAs for its current cities under the causal-corpus rules in `CLAUDE.md`; teacher salary schedules, where the teacher unit is already in the corpus, may already be present as exhibits/appendices and could be reviewed without new ingestion.

**Dimension:** salary schedules.

## 9. Hard-to-fill stipends or differentiated pay

**Why it matters:** the direct evidence needed to distinguish "uniform schedule with no shortage response" from "uniform schedule plus a targeted add-on," per the rigidity-vs-differentiation question in Section 5 of the refinement memo. National base rates (roughly three in four sampled districts per NCTQ) are already identified this session; district-specific evidence for this project's current cities is not.

**Likely source families:** NCTQ compensation-strategy reports and state policy summaries (already identified this session); individual district CBAs and side-letter/MOU documents; school committee budget line items for stipend programs.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` for the national base-rate context (already closed this session); `data_build_feasible` for this project's specific cities if their teacher CBAs are reviewed for stipend/MOU language.

**Dimension:** salary schedules.

## 10. Teacher assistant/paraprofessional separation

**Why it matters:** ensures any future non-safety wage-trend or mechanism work does not accidentally blend a distinct, lower-paid, non-licensure-tracked occupation into "teacher" figures, per the explicit boundary in Section 4 of the refinement memo and the existing project instruction not to merge these roles.

**Likely source families:** BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook (teacher assistants are already confirmed this session as a distinct SOC-classified occupation, separate from teacher categories); Massachusetts DESE staffing reports if they separately break out paraprofessional/aide FTE counts; district HR job classifications.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` — the occupational distinction itself is already confirmed; `data_build_feasible` for confirming that this project's specific city-level data sources (DESE profiles, district budgets) keep the two categories separate in practice, which was not verified this session.

**Dimension:** composition.

## 11. School budget constraints and state aid

**Why it matters:** needed to verify the specific mechanics of Massachusetts's Proposition 2½ levy-limit/override system as it applies to school budgets (partially closed this session) and, separately, the state education aid formula referenced but not verified in Section 7 of the refinement memo (commonly called Chapter 70 aid) — an important gap because the memo explicitly flagged this as unverified this session rather than asserting it as confirmed fact.

**Likely source families:** Mass.gov Department of Local Services publications on Proposition 2½ (already identified this session); Massachusetts Department of Elementary and Secondary Education finance/Chapter 70 program pages; municipal/school committee budget books for this project's current cities.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` for the Proposition 2½ mechanics generally (already closed this session); `desk_research_feasible` but not yet done for the Chapter 70/state-aid formula specifics; `data_build_feasible` for tying levy-limit/override history to this project's specific cities' school budget decisions.

**Dimension:** budget constraints.

## 12. District-level peer salary comparisons

**Why it matters:** needed to test the `teacher_peer_district_comparability` hypothesis directly — whether Massachusetts teacher bargaining documents actually invoke "surrounding" or "comparable" district salary comparisons in the way the mechanism memo hypothesizes, and whether those comparisons correlate with settlement outcomes the way comparator language may correlate with police/fire arbitration awards.

**Likely source families:** teacher union bargaining fact sheets and public statements (treat as advocacy sources, not neutral evidence, per Section 7 of the refinement memo); school committee bargaining update materials; local news coverage of teacher contract negotiations; Massachusetts DESE Educator Contracts Statewide Report as a possible cross-district comparison baseline.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` for this project's current cities if their teacher bargaining materials are reviewed for comparator language; `likely_hard` for a systematic, cross-district test of whether comparator claims predict settlement outcomes, since that would require matched bargaining-history and outcome data beyond what a small corpus can support.

## 13. Massachusetts teacher bargaining impasse process (no-strike / no-arbitration verification)

**Why it matters:** the refinement memo (Section 7) raises, but explicitly does not confirm, the hypothesis that Massachusetts teachers are barred from striking under M.G.L. c. 150E without a compensating binding-arbitration backstop for wages, unlike police/fire under JLMC. This is a specific, checkable institutional claim central to the police/fire-vs-teacher comparison in Section 7 of the refinement memo, and it should not be treated as established until verified directly against Massachusetts labor-relations law or Department of Labor Relations guidance.

**Likely source families:** Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 150E; Massachusetts Department of Labor Relations (DLR) guidance or case decisions; labor-relations legal treatises or law-review articles on Massachusetts public-sector impasse procedures.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` — this is a specific statutory/institutional fact that a targeted search or legal-source review should be able to confirm directly in a future session; flagged here rather than closed this session because it was not directly verified during this session's bounded searches.

# Report Outline — Deeper Look Into Safety & Non-safety Wage Mechanisms

**Type:** planning document. This is a detailed outline and claims map for the PI-facing report draft (`report_draft_safety_non_safety_wage_mechanisms_2026-07-06.md`), not the report itself. It exists so the report's structure, evidence basis, and claim strength can be reviewed independently of the prose.

## 1. Proposed title block

- **Title:** Deeper Look Into Safety & Non-safety Wage Mechanisms
- **Subtitle:** Mechanisms, Counterpoints, and Source Needs Across Municipal Occupations
- **Short header/report label:** Safety & Non-safety Wage Mechanisms

## 2. Intended audience and purpose

This is a PI-facing internal research memo, not a publication draft and not a causal-evidence report. Its purpose is to synthesize the mechanism-development and cross-group audit work completed to date (police, fire, teachers, DPW, clerical/admin, library, custodial/facilities, dispatchers, nurse_health, sanitation, transit) into one reviewable document that a PI can use to decide what to prioritize next — further source acquisition, a descriptive wage baseline, or eventual GABRIEL attribute design. It is explicitly a working evidence map: every claim is graded by evidence basis (current corpus vs. external/national context vs. an identified source need), and no claim in the report should be read as an established causal finding. The report also carries forward the project's standing source-needs and next-step planning, so a PI reading it can see not only what is known but what would need to be true, or acquired, before a stronger version of any given claim could be made.

## 3. Section-by-section outline

### Executive Summary

- **Purpose:** orient a PI who may not have followed every session — state the central finding, the evidentiary status of the work, and the group-retention frame in under a page.
- **Key claims:** (1) police/fire combine real upward wage pressure with a distinctive institutional translation mechanism (JLMC-style compulsory arbitration); (2) essentially every non-safety group examined has at least one genuine upward-pressure mechanism of its own; (3) the sharp, evidence-supported contrast is not "hard job vs. easy job" but "strong vs. weak/absent pressure-to-wage conversion channels."
- **Evidence basis:** synthesis of ~25 prior session memos plus a dedicated cross-group audit and a must-have evidence-verification pass; drawn from a 32-row, Massachusetts-only causal corpus plus bounded external/national desk research.
- **Caveats:** this is a working evidence map, not a finished causal analysis; the corpus is small and geographically concentrated; several groups (transit, sanitation, nurse_health) have thin or structurally limited corpus footholds.
- **Callouts:** none required here beyond the group-retention preview (see Evidence Map).
- **What not to overclaim:** do not state or imply that any mechanism has been shown to *cause* the police/fire wage pattern; do not imply the group-retention list is final or comprehensive of all municipal occupations.

### Main Takeaways

- **Purpose:** a scannable, numbered list a PI can absorb in one read before deciding whether to read further.
- **Key claims:** the 7 items specified in the task brief (translation institutions; real non-safety pressures; CBA mechanics vs. wage reasoning; arbitration/award documents as the richer wage-logic source; contracted/regionalized services disappearing from municipal data; esteem/danger/credentialing not automatically wage power; source needs remain).
- **Evidence basis:** each takeaway should carry an implicit pointer to the section that substantiates it (Mechanism Synthesis, Evidence Map, or MA/National Nuance).
- **Caveats:** keep each takeaway to 1-2 sentences; do not let any single takeaway smuggle in an unqualified causal claim.
- **Callouts:** could bold the mechanism name in each takeaway for scanability.
- **What not to overclaim:** do not rank the takeaways as if they were a ranked list of "true findings" — they are working syntheses.

### Evidence Map

- **Purpose:** the single table a PI is most likely to return to — one row per group, answering "where does this group stand and what's still needed."
- **Key claims:** status assignments (central / strong comparison / public-safety-adjacent / secondary / source-design case / deferred) as specified in the group-retention frame.
- **Evidence basis:** each row's `strongest_current_evidence` cell should name a specific, real, already-documented finding (e.g., "JLMC comparator criteria in Somerville awards," "Georgetown's licensed-vs-unlicensed maintenance pay differential") rather than a general assertion.
- **Caveats:** a `key_caveat` column per row keeps the table honest without requiring prose hedging elsewhere.
- **Callouts:** this table is a strong candidate to also appear, in expanded form, in the appendix.
- **What not to overclaim:** do not let "strong comparison" or "central" read as "settled" — these are relative-priority labels within this project's own sequencing, not external validity judgments.

### Group-by-Group Findings

- **Purpose:** give each of the 11 groups a compact, consistent treatment so a PI can compare across groups without re-deriving context.
- **Key claims:** per-group upward-pressure and restraint/buffering mechanisms, stated as findings-to-date, not conclusions.
- **Evidence basis:** explicitly separate "current corpus shows" from "external/national context suggests" in every group's writeup, per the task's tone requirement.
- **Caveats:** every group needs at least one caveat sentence; groups with a single-city or single-document evidentiary base (nurse_health, dispatchers, several sanitation/transit findings) need that stated plainly.
- **Callouts:** short snippets are acceptable (already-quoted spans from the underlying memos) but should not become full block quotes; a bold one-line "status" tag at the top of each group's entry aids scanning.
- **What not to overclaim:** resist the temptation to let this section re-argue the Executive Summary's central claim group-by-group; keep it descriptive, save the interpretive synthesis for the Mechanism-by-Mechanism section.

### Mechanism-by-Mechanism Synthesis

- **Purpose:** this is the section that actually carries the report's analytical weight — organizing by mechanism (not occupation) is what lets the "translation institutions, not difficulty" argument be made cleanly, since the same mechanism can be shown operating differently across groups side by side.
- **Key claims:** the 13 mechanisms listed in the task brief, each with a strongest-groups / counterpoint-groups structure.
- **Evidence basis:** this section should carry the heaviest cross-referencing to specific corpus findings (e.g., naming which cities' documents show which pattern) since it is where the report's central argument lives.
- **Caveats:** the "source-document production effects" mechanism is itself a caveat about every other mechanism in this section and should be positioned so a reader encounters it before or alongside the others it qualifies, not buried last.
- **Callouts:** a short "counterpoint" or "what would change this" line per mechanism keeps the section from reading as a list of confirmed facts.
- **What not to overclaim:** for mechanisms where the current evidence is genuinely one national data point (e.g., the MBTA's 2024 wage-then-hiring-surge finding) or one city's finding, say so explicitly rather than letting the mechanism description imply a broader pattern.

### Massachusetts and National Nuance

- **Purpose:** protect the report from the single biggest external-validity risk this project has flagged repeatedly across sessions — treating a Massachusetts-specific institution (JLMC, Chapter 70, M.G.L. c. 161A/161B) as if it were a general national pattern.
- **Key claims:** the specific institutional map per group (as specified in the task brief), plus the general finding that public-safety impasse privilege recurs nationally as a *pattern* while its *vehicles* are state-specific.
- **Evidence basis:** the citation-audited state-law table is the strongest source for any national claim in this report and should be the primary citation for this section.
- **Caveats:** South Carolina's bargaining prohibition and Wisconsin's Act 10 status are both flagged elsewhere as weaker-sourced or legally unsettled and should carry that same caveat here if mentioned.
- **Callouts:** the Massachusetts institutional comparison table (see Section 5 below) belongs here or immediately preceding this section.
- **What not to overclaim:** do not state that any non-Massachusetts state's institutional pattern has been independently verified by this project — the citation audit verified statutory text, not this project's own corpus, for every non-Massachusetts state.

## 4. Report-level claim hierarchy

**Central claims** (load-bearing, stated directly in the Executive Summary and Main Takeaways):
- Police and fire combine multiple real upward wage-pressure mechanisms with a distinctive, confirmed institutional translation mechanism (JLMC/M.G.L. c. 1078 compulsory interest arbitration) that no non-safety group in this project's corpus has.
- Every non-safety group examined has at least one genuine, evidenced upward-pressure mechanism of its own (credentialing, hazard, coverage obligations, staffing shortages) — the absence of a comparable wage outcome is not evidence of an absence of pressure.
- Non-safety pressures translate through identifiably different channels: classification/reclassification systems, service buffering, contractor/outsourcing substitution, governance distance, budget-structure constraints, or a weaker (voluntary, advisory) impasse pathway (Chapter 150E §9) rather than a compulsory one.

**Supporting claims** (real findings that sharpen but do not carry the central argument alone):
- DPW's classification-tied credential-to-pay linkage (CDL, hoisting, water/wastewater licenses) is richly corpus-confirmed.
- Clerical/admin's Boston Compensation Grade Appeals process is the most procedurally restrictive dispute-resolution mechanism found anywhere in this project's corpus.
- Library's professional credentialing (MLS/MLIS) is real but uneven and does not function as a guaranteed wage floor.
- Custodial/facilities now has two fully-evidenced salary schedules with genuine credential-tied classification tiers.
- Dispatchers show real 24/7 minimum-staffing pressure and civilian-status institutional placement, evidenced directly in two cities' corpus text.

**Caveats** (findings that qualify or bound other claims, not claims in their own right):
- Source-document production effects: CBA text systematically under-represents recruitment/retention and peer-comparability reasoning regardless of occupation; absence in CBA text is not proof of absence in practice.
- The current corpus is small (32 rows) and entirely Massachusetts-based; national generalizability has been separately, only partially, tested.
- Public esteem, danger, credentialing, and essentiality have not been shown, anywhere in this project's evidence, to automatically produce wage power.

**Source-design findings** (findings about the research design itself, not about any occupation's wages):
- Sanitation is contracted out for a majority (5 of 9) of this project's own cities, structurally limiting municipal-CBA observability.
- Transit sits entirely outside any of this project's nine cities' own municipal government (MBTA state authority or privately-contracted regional authority) — a design-fit finding, not merely a data-availability gap.
- The discourse corpus remains empty (0 rows), meaning no political-salience or public-framing evidence exists outside bounded desk research.

**Deferred claims** (explicitly not asserted, flagged for future work):
- Any claim about transit as a direct city-level occupation comparison.
- Any claim that nurse_health's Wayland finding generalizes to another city.
- Any quantitative wage-tier comparison between dispatchers and sworn police/fire (the underlying wage table is not in this project's corpus for the one city where a direct comparison was attempted).
- Any claim about which mechanism is causally dominant in producing the police/fire wage pattern.

## 6. Open decisions before formatting

- **Whether to include custodial/facilities as a named group or fold it into `other`/DPW discussion:** recommend naming it explicitly in the main text, now that it has two fully-evidenced salary schedules — folding it into DPW would understate a now-confirmed, distinct finding (the licensed-vs-unlicensed maintenance credential differential), and it remains its own row in every tracking table this project maintains. The unresolved `occupation_class` schema decision is a `data/contracts.csv` question, not a reason to omit the group from a report about mechanisms.
- **Whether to foreground dispatchers as public-safety-adjacent:** recommend giving dispatchers their own clearly-labeled subsection (not folded into DPW or into police/fire) — this is the group whose institutional ambiguity is itself analytically interesting (civilian status, Chapter 150E confirmed, but operational profile closest to police/fire of any group examined), and burying it inside another group's discussion would lose that.
- **How much to include about transit:** recommend a compact treatment — the governance-mismatch finding (no city-operated cases, MBTA/RTA governance for all 9 cities) is genuinely important context for why the project's design can't currently include transit, but transit has zero corpus rows and is explicitly deferred; it should not receive the same depth as the central/strong-comparison groups. A short Group-by-Group entry plus a mention in the MA/National Nuance section (the newly-found MBTA c.161A arbitration regime) is sufficient.
- **Whether to mention nurse_health in main text or appendix:** recommend a short main-text entry (Group-by-Group section) rather than appendix-only, since the Wayland finding is genuinely substantive (dollar-figure pay grades, a credential stipend, a documented wage-restraint finding) — but the entry should be short and carry the single-city/fragmented-unit caveat prominently, consistent with its "keep_secondary" status.
- **How large appendix tables should be:** recommend the appendix hold the *full* versions of the group-retention table (all columns from the audit, not just the 6 main-text columns), the full 20-mechanism disposition table, the must-have-plus-useful source-needs rows (not the full 44-row CSV — that stays referenced, not reproduced), and a condensed hypothesis-disposition summary (grouped by recommendation category, not all 43 rows individually) — full unabridged reproduction of every underlying CSV would make the appendix unreadable and duplicate files already in the repo.

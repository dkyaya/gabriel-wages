# PI progress memo: Gabriel Wages source discovery

Date: July 22, 2026

We have completed the first major build-out of the project’s national source-discovery infrastructure. The project now has an authoritative universe of 35,589 municipal and township governments, a reproducible scout pipeline, a transparent priority-tier system, and a static dashboard for coverage, queue, runtime, yield, and readiness reporting.

Across the work completed to date, 794 municipalities have a successful parseable scout outcome. Of these, 612 produced at least one candidate source lead and 182 produced a valid empty result. A separate 20 municipalities had transport, timeout, or empty-response failures and remain outside successful coverage for a later bounded retry. The national candidate queue now contains 1,602 URL-bearing rows.

A **candidate row** is one possible source document or webpage identified by the scout and retained for later review. A municipality can produce multiple rows—for example, possible police, fire, general employee, salary schedule, ordinance, or HR records. These rows are unverified. They are not yet confirmed official documents, matched bargaining units, ingested contracts, or observations that can support wage estimates.

The four coordinated 150-municipality waves show substantial operational progress. Wave 1 returned 149 parseable results and 246 candidate rows in 115 minutes and 37 seconds. Wave 2 returned 148 parseable results and 223 candidate rows in 102 minutes and 30 seconds. Tier 1 Wave 1 returned 142 parseable results and 268 candidate rows in 112 minutes and 3 seconds. The latest Tier 1 Wave 2 returned 148 parseable results, 122 candidate-positive municipalities, and 325 queue-eligible candidate rows in 95 minutes and 39 seconds. It was the fastest and highest-yield wave reviewed so far, at 94.1 attempted rows per hour and 2.209 parsed candidate records per parseable municipality.

Several engineering changes made the workflow more robust and auditable. Full runs now use one serialized coordinator lane, exact locked inputs, timing artifacts, safe resume support, and explicit stop conditions. A stronger preflight gate checks both baseline and hosted-search routes before a large run. Compact prompts preserve employer, unit, source, duplicate, and stage controls while reducing repeated prose. Deterministic query hints make the search instructions reproducible. Adaptive sleep responds to transport instability rather than relying only on a fixed delay. State-yield reports and priority tiers provide a transparent learning loop instead of treating all municipalities as equally valuable.

The dashboard data have been refreshed to the post–Tier 1 Wave 2 state. The configured project URL is `https://dkyaya.github.io/gabriel-wages/`, subject to GitHub Pages being enabled and deployed. The dashboard reports national and state discovery coverage, candidate-queue volume, the discovery funnel, operational yield, runtime trends, and priority tiers. Its metrics describe workflow status. They should not be interpreted as verified source quality or wage evidence.

The refreshed priority layer scores all 35,589 governments using a documented operational heuristic. It leaves 34,789 future-scout-eligible municipalities, including 1,227 in Tier 1 and 3,478 in Tier 2. The score is designed to schedule work using employer scale, government type, smoothed state yield, research relevance, and geographic value. It is not a factual claim about unionization, source availability, or wage effects.

What can we conclude now? The project can systematically identify possible municipal labor and salary records at national scale, preserve exact employer identities, and learn which target groups yield more leads. Priority targeting appears to improve discovery efficiency, and the latest wave achieved the strongest operational yield.

What can we not conclude? We do not yet have verified wage gaps, safety/non-safety wage differentials, bargaining-mechanism effects, causal estimates, or final state comparisons. We have not yet confirmed the provenance, completeness, dates, unit identity, wage extractability, duplicate status, or matched-cycle value of the 1,602 queued leads. No scout candidate has been promoted by this report into verified, ingested, codified, or claim-supporting evidence.

I recommend that the next phase begin with a 50–100-row verification pilot. The sample should combine high-yield and geographically diverse states, large municipalities, and candidate groups that appear likely to contain both public-safety and ordinary municipal records. For each row, we would confirm the exact employer and unit, source owner, document type, dates, completeness, access status, duplicate status, wage-field extractability, and whether the record contributes to a matched city-cycle comparison. The outputs would be a verified-source ledger, unit/source classification, and ingestion-readiness decision—not wage extraction or modeling yet.

The main decision for the PI is the preferred balance between breadth and verification. If national coverage remains the immediate priority, the refreshed Tier 1 pool supports further discovery waves. My recommendation is to pause long enough to establish the verification protocol and measure how many candidate leads convert into analysis-relevant sources, then decide how aggressively to continue broad scouting.

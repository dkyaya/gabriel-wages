# Non-Safety Wage Mechanism Refinement: Clerical / Administrative Municipal Workers

**Date:** 2026-07-05
**Scope:** context-building and hypothesis-refinement only. No GABRIEL run, no model/API calls, no OEWS/BLS data build, no ingestion, no causal claim. Focuses on Massachusetts municipal clerical/administrative workers where public sources are readily available, with national context for general mechanisms; this memo does not attempt comprehensive statewide coverage and is mechanism discovery, not corpus building.

## 1. Purpose and scope

This memo develops the third non-safety comparison group in the project's sequenced roadmap (`non_safety_comparison_roadmap_2026-07-04.md`): clerical and administrative municipal workers. It follows the teacher mechanism work (`non_safety_teacher_wage_mechanism_refinement_2026-07-04.md`, `ma_teacher_bargaining_school_finance_institutional_context_2026-07-04.md`) and the DPW/public-works mechanism work (`non_safety_dpw_public_works_wage_mechanism_refinement_2026-07-04.md`, `non_safety_dpw_existing_corpus_scan_2026-07-04.md`, `ma_dpw_bargaining_impasse_context_2026-07-04.md`), and applies the same discipline: every mechanism gets a counterargument, and every mechanism needs a stated way to distinguish it from alternatives.

The central question is whether clerical/administrative municipal workers face wage-restraint mechanisms that help explain why police/fire wages may rise faster than some non-safety wages. This memo focuses on Massachusetts municipal clerical/administrative workers where public sources are readily available, using national context only to identify general mechanisms (recruitment/retention trends, minimum-wage compression, automation, shared-services arrangements) that are not Massachusetts-specific. It does not attempt comprehensive statewide coverage of Massachusetts clerical/admin units, and it does not draw a causal conclusion about whether or how these mechanisms actually move clerical/admin wages relative to police/fire.

## 2. Why clerical/admin matters for the project

Clerical/administrative municipal work is the project's strongest available **wage-restraint / internal-equity** comparison group, occupying a different analytical position than teachers or DPW. Teachers offer the strongest *credentialing/salience* comparison (public-facing, credentialed, unionized, publicly esteemed) and DPW offers the strongest *operational* comparison (physical risk, licensure gating, emergency response). Clerical/admin work, by contrast, generally lacks the obvious emergency-response argument DPW can make, the state licensure/certification argument that gates entry to teaching or many DPW trades, and the acute, single-incident political visibility a police or fire failure can generate. This absence of ready-made justifications is exactly what makes clerical/admin useful: it is closer to a "plain" municipal labor-market observation, unclouded by an emergency-services or credentialing story, and so it helps identify what the **denominator** of the police/fire wage comparison actually looks like when none of the usual safety-side or teacher-side or DPW-side arguments are available. If clerical/admin wages grow more slowly than police/fire wages even though clerical/admin workers may face real recruitment, cost-of-living, and workload pressures of their own, that is informative about how much of the police/fire wage story is occupation-specific institutional advantage versus a general municipal labor-market pattern that clerical/admin, absent its own institutional advantages, cannot access.

## 3. Clerical/admin occupation and classification map

Clerical/administrative municipal work spans a wide range of titles, skill levels, and departmental placements, and this memo follows the project's instruction not to collapse it into a single homogeneous category:

- **Clerk / senior clerk / principal clerk** — a common Massachusetts municipal title progression (this project's own corpus shows a comparable pattern in DPW classification systems, e.g., Arlington's MC-grade laborer-to-craftsman progression; clerical titles often follow an analogous clerk-to-senior-clerk-to-principal-clerk ladder tied to years of service, added duties, or department size).
- **Administrative assistant / executive assistant** — often attached to a specific department head or elected official, with duties and pay that can vary considerably depending on the office's complexity and public-facing load.
- **Office manager** — a supervisory or lead clerical role within a department, sometimes the promotional ceiling within a clerical bargaining unit before crossing into non-union management, directly analogous to a DPW foreman's position in the classification hierarchy.
- **Payroll/accounting staff** — clerical work requiring specialized municipal-finance knowledge (payroll tax withholding rules, municipal accounting standards, benefits administration) that is not interchangeable with general clerical duties.
- **Assessor/collector/treasurer office staff** — clerical roles requiring familiarity with property-tax assessment, tax-title/collections law, and municipal debt/investment recordkeeping; often subject to state-mandated procedures and deadlines (e.g., tax-recapitulation filings) that create externally-imposed workload timing.
- **Town clerk / city clerk staff** — a distinctive municipal function combining vital-records administration, elections administration, licensing, and public-meeting/open-records compliance, frequently with statutory deadlines and legal-liability exposure (e.g., election law compliance) not shared by general clerical roles.
- **School administrative assistants** — clerical staff embedded in a school department's administrative structure rather than general municipal government; this project's own corpus includes an example of this distinction (Section 8 below).
- **Department-specific clerical roles** — for example, DPW clerks (already documented in this project's `public_works`-classified Worcester row, per the prior DPW corpus scan) or clerical staff embedded in police or fire departments — a reminder that "clerical/admin" as an occupation class can appear administratively nested inside a safety or operational department rather than only in a standalone city-hall office.
- **Supervisors** — lead or supervisory clerical titles managing other clerical staff, typically still inside the bargaining unit but at its top grade.

**Why this matters for wage-mechanism analysis:** a shortage or wage-pressure signal concentrated in one of these roles (say, payroll staff, who require specialized municipal-finance knowledge) should not be read as evidence about "clerical/admin" generally, mirroring the composition-effect discipline already established for teachers (grade/subject mix) and DPW (equipment operator/water-operator/mechanic mix). A single "clerical/admin wage" figure would blend general clerks, specialized finance staff, statutorily-exposed town clerk functions, and department-nested clerical roles — a composition spread at least as wide as the DPW classification spread already documented in this project's corpus.

## 4. Upward wage-pressure mechanisms

**Recruitment/retention.** National reporting from MissionSquare Research Institute (a public-sector-focused research organization) found that state and local government recruitment challenges eased somewhat through 2024, with occupations such as information technology, dispatch, and policing seeing declines of at least ten percentage points since 2022 in the share of governments reporting them as hard to fill — but retirements remain a looming structural concern, and BLS's own 2024 Job Openings and Labor Turnover Survey data cited in the same reporting shows public-sector quits continuing to rise (an increase of 32,000 in a single month, April 2024). Exit-interview data cited in that reporting found non-competitive compensation was the most commonly cited reason for leaving (53 percent), ahead of retirement (33 percent) and better advancement opportunities elsewhere in the public sector (32 percent) ([MissionSquare, Recruitment Challenges Easing for State and Local Governments, 2024-07-22](https://www.missionsq.org/about-us/news-and-updates/media-inquiries/news-20240722-recruitmentchallengeseasing.html)). This is national, cross-occupation local-government data, not clerical/admin-specific or Massachusetts-specific, but it establishes that compensation-driven turnover is a documented general local-government phenomenon that clerical/admin staff would plausibly share. **Counterargument:** the same reporting shows recruitment difficulty easing rather than worsening for many occupations, and clerical/admin work generally competes in a much larger, more liquid private-sector office/administrative labor market than a licensed water operator or a certified teacher does, which could mean clerical/admin recruitment pressure, where it exists, resolves faster than in more specialized occupations rather than building into sustained wage pressure.

**Private-sector office/admin competition.** Unlike teaching (which requires state licensure) or many DPW roles (which require CDL/hoisting/water-operator credentials), general clerical/administrative skills are directly transferable to, and in competition with, a large private-sector office and administrative labor market. This is structurally similar to the DPW mechanism memo's point about CDL/mechanic labor competing with the broader trucking/trades economy, but the comparison pool for clerical/admin work is arguably even larger and more liquid, spanning nearly every private-sector industry that employs office staff.

**Cost of living.** As a general labor-market pressure, cost-of-living increases raise the wage a municipality must offer to remain competitive for any occupation, clerical/admin included; this is not occupation-specific but is a background pressure this memo notes for completeness rather than treats as a distinguishing mechanism.

**Workload growth from digital systems, compliance, permitting, finance, benefits, and records.** Even as automation reduces some routine tasks (Section 5), the shift toward digital permitting, records, and benefits systems can simultaneously increase the compliance and system-management burden on clerical staff (data entry accuracy, cybersecurity/records-retention compliance, public-records-request administration, benefits-enrollment system management) — a workload-composition shift rather than a simple net reduction. Industry vendor material describing municipal records/permitting software explicitly frames the goal as allowing staff "to focus more on hard-to-solve issues or projects that help the community grow" rather than eliminating clerical roles outright, implying a shift in task composition, not a straightforward headcount reduction ([GovPilot, Transforming Municipal Clerk Operations](https://www.govpilot.com/blog/-municipaclerk-operations-how-digital-forms-and-automated-workflows-deliver-results); this and similar vendor sources should be read as industry marketing material illustrating a plausible mechanism, not as neutral, independently-verified evidence).

**Specialized municipal knowledge.** Payroll, benefits administration, municipal finance, permitting, assessing, and town/city clerk functions all require specific procedural and legal knowledge (municipal accounting rules, tax-title law, election law, records-retention schedules) that is not general clerical knowledge and is not fully substitutable with a generic administrative hire, even though it does not require a state-issued professional license the way teaching or several DPW trades do.

**Pay compression, including minimum-wage-driven compression.** Pay compression occurs when the wage gap between an entry-level position and its supervisor (or between an entry step and a more senior step) narrows to the point of limited differentiation, sometimes described as "salary inversion" when a subordinate's pay actually exceeds a supervisor's. A documented, quantifiable version of this mechanism specific to state/local minimum-wage policy: a Century Foundation analysis estimated the annual cost of raising all local-government workers above a $15 minimum wage threshold across California, Massachusetts, New York, New Jersey, Illinois, and the District of Columbia, finding costs ranging from about 0.2 percent of relevant payroll per year (District of Columbia) to about 1.0 percent per year (Illinois), with a weighted average of about 0.6 percent per year — and **Massachusetts is explicitly one of the states covered by that estimate** ([The Century Foundation, The Impact of Increased Minimum Wages on Local Governments](https://tcf.org/content/report/impact-increased-minimum-wages-local-governments/)). This is the clearest, most directly quantified upward pressure mechanism specific to lower-graded clerical positions found in this session's research: as a state minimum wage rises, municipalities must raise entry-level clerical (and other low-grade) step rates to comply, which can compress the pay differential between entry-level and senior clerical steps unless the whole schedule is adjusted upward in tandem.

**Reclassification pressure.** Because clerical/admin classification systems are typically grade-and-step structures similar in kind to (though procedurally distinct from) the DPW classification systems already documented in this project's corpus, wage pressure in a specific role can surface as a request to reclassify that position to a higher grade rather than as a general wage increase. This project's own corpus provides a direct, concrete example: Boston's SENA Local 9158 clerical/administrative CBA describes a formal institutional process for this — a reclassification application is forwarded by the Office of Labor Relations (OLR) to the city's "OHR Classification and Compensation Unit," which reviews the application and returns a determination to the union. This is a real, source-grounded example of reclassification functioning as a distinct, administratively-formalized wage-pressure channel, separate from across-the-board bargaining (Section 6 below).

## 5. Wage-restraint or weaker wage-translation mechanisms

**Lower public salience than police/fire, teachers, and DPW.** A clerical/admin staffing shortfall rarely produces a visible, attributable public failure the way an unfilled patrol shift, an unfilled classroom, or a missed snowplow route can. A permit taking longer to process, a records request answered a week later than usual, or reduced counter hours are diffuse inconveniences rather than acute incidents. **Counterargument:** some clerical/admin functions carry real, occasionally acute public and legal salience — town/city clerk election administration failures, for example, can generate serious legal and political consequences, and treasurer/collector office errors can affect residents' tax bills or municipal bond ratings. Salience should be treated as function-dependent, not uniformly low across all clerical/admin roles, mirroring the same caution already applied to DPW's "lower public salience" hypothesis.

**Classification and internal-equity systems.** Municipal classification and compensation systems, as documented in Boston's clerical CBA, typically operate through a formal administrative review process (an "OHR Classification and Compensation Unit" or equivalent) rather than through open-ended bargaining leverage, and municipalities generally try to maintain internal pay-parity norms across many job titles and departments at once. This can restrain any single clerical classification's wage growth, because a municipality generally cannot raise one title's pay without either a formal reclassification finding or accepting internal-equity pressure from comparably-graded titles elsewhere in the municipal workforce — directly analogous to the classification-fragmentation mechanism already documented for DPW, but potentially more binding for clerical/admin because clerical classification systems often span the entire non-safety municipal workforce (clerks, administrative assistants, and department-specific clerical roles are frequently graded on the same unified classification-and-compensation schedule, unlike DPW's more department-specific grading).

**Local labor-market anchoring.** Because general clerical/administrative pay in most municipalities is benchmarked, explicitly or implicitly, against the broad local private-sector office/administrative labor market (Section 4) rather than against a specialized, less-substitutable comparator, clerical/admin wages may track a lower, more broadly competitive local wage rate rather than the higher, occupation-specific comparator wages (peer police departments, peer fire departments) that JLMC criteria direct arbitrators to weigh for public safety.

**Part-time staffing, automation/digitization, reorganization, and shared services.** These four mechanisms are grouped here because they share a common structural logic: each is a way to reduce the *number* of clerical/admin positions or hours needed, rather than a way to raise the *wage* of the positions that remain.

- *Part-time staffing* reduces benefit and often base-wage cost per position without necessarily reducing total task coverage claimed.
- *Automation/digitization* (Section 4) can plausibly reduce routine task volume even while shifting remaining staff toward more compliance-oriented work; vendor materials frame this as a productivity gain rather than a wage-suppression tool, but a productivity gain that reduces required headcount is, from a total-clerical-wage-bill perspective, a form of restraint even if it does not lower any individual employee's wage.
- *Reorganization/consolidation* can combine clerical functions across departments (e.g., a single shared clerical pool serving multiple small departments) without necessarily changing the classification-and-pay structure for the remaining positions.
- *Shared-service arrangements between municipalities* are a documented, real Massachusetts practice: Mass.gov's Efficiency and Regionalization grant program (building on the earlier Community Innovation Challenge grant program created in 2011) has funded shared administrative and clerical-adjacent positions across small Massachusetts towns, with concrete examples including a shared Regional Sustainability Coordinator (Westford and Carlisle) and a shared Town Administrator (Berkshire Regional Planning Council and Savoy); municipal finance and information technology are explicitly named as common areas for this kind of shared-service arrangement ([Mass.gov, Ask DLS: Intermunicipal Agreements](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/ask-dls-intermunicipal-agreements); [Mass.gov, Efficiency and Regionalization grant recipients](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/efficiency-and-regionalization-grant-recipients)). A shared administrative position, by construction, spreads one wage-setting decision across two or more municipalities' budgets rather than generating a wage increase within any single municipality's own bargaining unit.

**Counterargument common to all four:** these mechanisms reduce headcount or hours, not necessarily the wage paid to remaining employees; a municipality could simultaneously shrink its clerical headcount through automation or shared services while still raising the remaining positions' wages in response to genuine recruitment or retention pressure. These mechanisms restrain the *total clerical wage bill* more directly than they restrain any *individual clerical wage rate*, which is an important distinction for interpreting any future wage-trend evidence.

**Limited overtime/callback translation.** Unlike police, fire, or DPW, clerical/admin work rarely involves mandatory overtime tied to an operational emergency; where overtime exists (this project's corpus shows both Boston's and Seekonk's clerical/admin contracts have standard overtime provisions), it is more likely tied to routine peak-workload periods (e.g., tax-billing season, town-meeting preparation) than to an unpredictable emergency callback, meaning the overtime-as-buffering channel already documented for police/fire and DPW is present in a much more muted, predictable form for clerical/admin.

**Vacancy absorption through backlogs, slower service, cross-coverage, or redistributed work.** This is the clerical/admin analog to DPW's service-deferral and teachers' substitute/class-size buffering: a vacant clerical position can be absorbed by redistributing duties among remaining staff, cross-training staff to cover multiple functions, or simply allowing service response times to lengthen (permit processing, records requests, phone/counter wait times), none of which show up as a wage response and none of which are as publicly visible as an unfilled patrol shift or an unplowed street.

**Budget constraints.** Clerical/admin positions compete with police, fire, teachers, and DPW for the same Proposition 2½-limited municipal levy capacity already discussed in the teacher and DPW mechanism memos, with no clerical/admin-specific budget floor or ceiling mechanism analogous to schools' net-school-spending requirement or water/sewer's enterprise-fund structure — clerical/admin is, in this sense, the "plainest" case of ordinary general-fund municipal budget competition among this project's non-safety comparison groups.

**Gendered occupational valuation — flagged as a hypothesis requiring evidence, not asserted as fact.** Clerical and administrative work has historically been, and in many workplaces remains, a female-dominated occupational category, and a body of labor economics and sociology research on "comparable worth" and occupational segregation has argued that female-dominated occupations can be systematically undervalued in wage-setting relative to their skill and complexity, independent of productivity differences. This is a well-documented general phenomenon in the labor economics literature, not an invented claim, but this memo does not assert that it explains any specific wage pattern in this project's Massachusetts cities, and it is not evidenced by anything found in this session's desk research or in this project's existing corpus. It is listed here, per the task's explicit instruction, as a hypothesis that would require dedicated, direct evidence (occupational gender composition data matched to wage data, ideally with a comparable-worth-style skill/complexity comparison) before being treated as an explanatory mechanism for this project's specific findings.

## 6. Classification, reclassification, and compression

Clerical/admin wage pressure, per this session's corpus review, is more likely to surface through the classification system itself than through visible across-the-board bargaining demands, for a structural reason: general wage increases in Massachusetts municipal CBAs are typically negotiated municipal-wide or bargaining-unit-wide (this project's corpus shows the Worcester clerical unit and the Worcester DPW clerks unit receiving the identical 2 percent annual pattern in the same contract cycle, consistent with a citywide wage-pattern practice rather than an occupation-specific negotiation), while a *specific* clerical title's pay problem (a role that has taken on more duties, or that is now hard to fill at its current grade) is more likely addressed through a formal reclassification request than through a renegotiation of the whole unit's wage schedule.

Boston's clerical/admin CBA provides the clearest documented example: a reclassification application moves from the Office of Labor Relations to a dedicated "OHR Classification and Compensation Unit," which reviews it against job-duties criteria and returns a determination — a bureaucratic, administratively-formalized process distinct from ordinary interest bargaining, and one that the same contract's management-rights language explicitly reserves as a management prerogative ("subject to management rights to reorganize or reclassify"). This is a plausible explanation for why reclassification-driven wage change could be **less visible** than a police/fire comparator argument or a DPW licensing stipend: it happens title-by-title, through an internal administrative review process, rather than through a citywide bargaining round or a published salary-schedule change, and may not surface in aggregate wage-trend data as a distinct, labeled event the way a JLMC award or a scheduled market adjustment (as seen in Arlington's DPW contract) would.

**Counterargument:** reclassification processes exist across many municipal occupations, including this project's own DPW documents (Franklin's "working out of classification" pay, Wayland's detailed reclassification procedures), so reclassification per se is not clerical/admin-specific — the distinguishing question is whether clerical/admin reclassification requests are processed more slowly, approved less often, or valued less (in dollar terms) than comparable requests in other occupation classes, which this session's evidence cannot establish.

## 7. Service deferral and workload absorption comparison

| occupation | how a staffing shortfall is typically absorbed | wage-adjacent or not |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Police/fire | Minimum staffing rules, mandatory overtime/callback, response-time pressure; a JLMC-eligible interest-arbitration backstop exists if bargaining reaches impasse | Directly wage-adjacent: overtime raises realized compensation; arbitration can raise base wages |
| Teachers | Long-term substitutes, larger class sizes, emergency/provisional certification, program cuts | Mostly not wage-adjacent: substitute pay is typically below a certified teacher's wage; class-size increases cost nothing extra per student absent a contractual overage stipend |
| DPW | Overtime/callback (especially storm response), contractor/subcontractor substitution, seasonal staffing, deferred maintenance | Partly wage-adjacent: overtime and (per this project's Franklin corpus finding) contractor-mobilization-triggered overtime premiums raise realized compensation; deferred maintenance and contractor substitution are not wage-adjacent |
| Clerical/admin | Slower permit/records/payroll/benefits processing, reduced office hours, cross-training and redistributed duties among remaining staff, part-time/shared-service staffing | Least wage-adjacent of the four: none of these absorption channels routinely convert into overtime, stipends, or a comparable premium in the documents reviewed for this project so far |

**Key question restated with the available evidence:** clerical/admin shortages appear, based on this session's research and this project's own corpus, more likely to be **absorbed internally** than to create visible public pressure. Unlike a missed patrol shift, an unfilled classroom, or an unplowed road, a slower permit turnaround or a longer records-request wait time is diffuse, individually low-stakes, and rarely attracts sustained local news or political attention — this is a hypothesis consistent with the available evidence, not a demonstrated finding, since this session did not review actual service-time or backlog data for any specific Massachusetts municipality.

## 8. Massachusetts context with national context

**Massachusetts-specific evidence found this session:** this project's own already-collected corpus provides three illustrative clerical/admin examples, reviewed lightly for this memo (a full corpus scan, mirroring the dedicated DPW corpus-scan session, is recommended as a future task in Section 12, not completed here). Worcester's Local 490 (NAGE/SEIU) clerical contract is a short successor Memorandum of Agreement providing a plain 2 percent annual base-wage increase for three years plus a new top step 2 percent above the prior maximum — the same wage pattern this project's corpus shows for Worcester's DPW clerks unit in the identical contract cycle, consistent with a citywide bargaining-pattern practice. Boston's SENA Local 9158 clerical/administrative CBA is the richest document found, with detailed classification, reclassification (the OHR Classification and Compensation Unit process described in Section 6), vacancy-posting, and civil-service-linked bumping/layoff provisions. Seekonk's Administrative Secretaries unit (AFSCME Local 1701) is specifically a **school-based** clerical unit (its contract references the School Committee and Superintendent), illustrating directly the task's instruction to distinguish school administrative assistants from general municipal clerical staff — this unit's institutional home is the school department, not city/town hall, even though it is classified under the same `clerical_admin` occupation class in this project's schema. Separately, Massachusetts's Efficiency and Regionalization grant program (and its predecessor, the 2011 Community Innovation Challenge) provides concrete, real examples of shared administrative positions across small Massachusetts towns (a shared Regional Sustainability Coordinator across Westford and Carlisle; a shared Town Administrator across the Berkshire Regional Planning Council and Savoy).

**National context used as background, not Massachusetts-specific evidence:** MissionSquare Research Institute's 2024 local-government recruitment/retention reporting; The Century Foundation's minimum-wage-compression cost estimate (which does explicitly include Massachusetts as one of six jurisdictions analyzed, so this item sits partway between pure national background and Massachusetts-specific evidence); general municipal-software-vendor material on permitting/records automation, which describes a national industry trend rather than any specific Massachusetts municipality's practice; and general HR-compensation literature on pay compression and comparable-worth/gendered-occupational-valuation research, none of which was verified against this project's specific Massachusetts cities this session.

**What this session did not do:** conduct a full document-by-document corpus scan of all three `clerical_admin` rows to the same depth as the dedicated DPW corpus-scan session (only a light, illustrative read was performed for this memo); review any specific Massachusetts city or town's clerical/admin vacancy, applicant, turnover, or service-backlog data; or verify whether Massachusetts clerical/admin bargaining follows the same general Chapter 150E Section 9 impasse process already confirmed for teachers and DPW (this is highly likely by the same institutional logic already established twice, since clerical/admin has no plausible JLMC eligibility argument, but it was not directly re-verified for clerical/admin specifically this session and is listed as a gap in the accompanying source-gap memo).

## 10. New or refined hypotheses

Five hypotheses are added as new rows to the hypothesis matrix, consistent with the instruction to prefer a small number of high-value additions; one additional hypothesis discussed above (`clerical_admin_specialized_municipal_knowledge`) and one (`clerical_admin_automation_reorganization`) are documented in this memo but folded into existing rows rather than given separate CSV rows, per Section 12 below and the CSV edit itself.

1. **`clerical_admin_classification_restraint`** — Formal classification-and-compensation review systems restrain any single clerical title's wage growth absent a reclassification finding or internal-equity adjustment, corpus-documented in Boston's OHR Classification and Compensation Unit process.
2. **`clerical_admin_reclassification_pressure`** — Clerical/admin wage pressure for a specific title is more likely to surface as a reclassification request than as a general wage increase, given the citywide/unit-wide pattern-bargaining practice observed in this project's Worcester corpus.
3. **`clerical_admin_pay_compression`** — Rising minimum wages and other floor-level pressures compress the gap between entry-level and senior clerical steps, a mechanism with a directly quantified national/Massachusetts-inclusive cost estimate (The Century Foundation, ~0.6% weighted average annual cost across six jurisdictions including Massachusetts).
4. **`clerical_admin_service_backlog_absorption`** — Clerical/admin staffing shortfalls are absorbed through slower service, cross-coverage, or redistributed work, the clerical/admin analog to DPW's service deferral (H21) and teachers' shortage buffering (H14), but with less wage-adjacent cost than either.
5. **`clerical_admin_lower_public_salience`** — Clerical/admin staffing problems generally draw less sustained public attention than police, fire, teacher, or DPW problems, except for statutorily-exposed functions (elections, tax billing) where salience is function-dependent rather than uniformly low.

Not given a separate row, per the preference for small, high-value additions:

- **`clerical_admin_specialized_municipal_knowledge`** — folded into the counterpoint discussion of `clerical_admin_classification_restraint`, since specialized knowledge (payroll, assessing, town clerk functions) is the substantive justification classification systems are meant to evaluate, not a separate wage-translation channel.
- **`clerical_admin_automation_reorganization`** — folded into the counterpoint discussion of `clerical_admin_service_backlog_absorption`, since automation/reorganization/shared-services all restrain the total clerical wage bill through headcount/hours reduction rather than through a distinct wage-suppression channel of their own.
- **`non_safety_wage_restraint`** (H17, already in the matrix) — extended by this memo's evidence, not re-added; see the CSV edit for the specific refinement.

## 11. Implications for future source extraction

None of the following should be built into a live GABRIEL attribute in this session; they are recorded so future clerical/admin-side mechanism extraction, if prototyped, uses signals disciplined against the classification-fragmentation and headcount-vs-wage confounds developed above.

Recommended textual signals: `clerk`, `senior clerk`, `principal clerk`, `administrative assistant`, `executive assistant`, `office manager`, `payroll`, `benefits`, `accounts payable`, `assessor`, `collector`, `treasurer`, `town clerk`, `city clerk`, `reclassification`, `classification`, `pay grade`, `step schedule`, `compression`, `minimum wage`, `internal equity`, `workload`, `backlog`, `permit`, `records`, `reduced hours`, `cross-training`, `shared services`, `automation`, `digitization`, `retention`, `vacancy`, `hard to fill`, `cost of living`, `comparable communities`.

Extraction discipline notes, consistent with the verbatim-capture and two-corpus rules already established in `CLAUDE.md`:

- Capture which specific clerical/admin title (clerk, administrative assistant, payroll staff, assessor/collector/treasurer staff, town/city clerk staff, school administrative assistant, department-nested clerical staff) a shortage/reclassification/workload mention refers to, rather than scoring generic "clerical/admin" language as evidence about the whole occupation class.
- Capture reclassification-application and classification-review-unit language as a distinct span from ordinary wage-schedule/percentage-increase language, mirroring the distinction already applied to DPW's out-of-classification pay and Arlington's scheduled market adjustment.
- Capture minimum-wage-compliance or schedule-compression language as a distinct span from ordinary annual percentage increases, so a floor-driven entry-step adjustment is not conflated with a general market-responsive raise.
- Capture shared-service/regionalization and automation/digitization language as headcount- or hours-relevant signals, distinct from wage-rate language, given the headcount-vs-wage distinction established in Section 5.
- Capture backlog/delay/reduced-hours language as a distinct span from wage-schedule language, and separately note whether the surrounding text ties any resulting service problem to a statutory-deadline or legal-compliance context (elections, tax billing) as a salience signal.
- Do not treat any gendered-occupational-valuation framing as established; if such language appears in a source (e.g., a pay-equity study or comparable-worth advocacy document), capture it as a distinct, explicitly-flagged span rather than as confirmed evidence of the mechanism.

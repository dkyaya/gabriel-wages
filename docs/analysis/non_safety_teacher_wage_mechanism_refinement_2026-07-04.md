# Non-Safety Wage Mechanism Refinement: Teachers First

**Date:** 2026-07-04
**Scope:** context-building and hypothesis-refinement only. No GABRIEL run, no model/API calls, no OEWS/BLS data build, no ingestion, no causal claim.

## 1. Purpose and scope

Every prior mechanism memo in this project (`police_fire_wage_mechanism_context_2026-07-02.md`, `police_fire_wage_mechanism_synthesis_for_pi_2026-07-03.md`, `police_fire_workforce_context_refinement_2026-07-03.md`) has developed the police/fire side of the comparison: why might safety wages rise, through what channels, and how might police and fire differ from each other. This memo develops the other half of the same comparison, which has so far been treated mostly as a residual category rather than as its own object of study.

The project's empirical claim is that police/fire wages rise faster than other municipal occupations. That gap is a difference of two rates of change, and it can grow for at least three reasons that are not mutually exclusive: safety wages accelerate, non-safety wages are restrained, or both happen at once. Every mechanism developed so far addresses only the first term. This memo starts filling in the second term by asking, concretely, why a non-safety occupation's wages might grow more slowly even when it faces staffing or labor-market pressure that looks superficially similar to what police/fire face.

This memo starts with teachers rather than treating "non-safety" as one undifferentiated bucket, for a specific reason: teachers are the strongest available non-safety comparison group, not the weakest. They are public-facing, credentialed, unionized, and (like firefighters on the safety side) generally socially valued. If a non-safety occupation with that profile still shows muted wage translation relative to police/fire under comparable staffing strain, that is a much sharper test of a genuine institutional or structural difference than starting with a less salient, less unionized comparison group. Public works/DPW and clerical/admin are set up briefly in Section 8 as the next two comparison groups, in that order, for reasons explained there, but this memo does not develop them in depth.

## 2. Current project baseline

### v9 and v10, restated in plain English

`comparability_emphasis` (v9) asks whether a document explicitly justifies wages by referring to peer communities, comparator jurisdictions, parity relationships, or other external wage benchmarks. `arbitration_or_impasse_backstop` (v10) asks whether wage-setting is shaped by formal impasse institutions such as interest arbitration, JLMC proceedings, mediation, factfinding, or bargaining in the shadow of those procedures. Both are described in more detail in the existing mechanism memos; this memo does not change that description.

### Why v9/v10 cannot explain the gap by themselves

Even a strong finding on v9 or v10 for police/fire documents would only show that comparator language and impasse-backed bargaining are more visible in safety-side documents. It would not show that non-safety wages are restrained, and it would not show why. A complete account of the police/fire vs. non-safety wage gap needs an explicit theory of the non-safety side: what institutions, budget constraints, and shortage-buffering channels might cap or slow non-safety wage growth even when non-safety staffing pressure exists. That is the gap this memo starts to fill.

### Why teachers specifically

Teachers share several features with firefighters that make them a disciplined comparison rather than a strawman: both are credentialed occupations requiring specific training and licensure; both are heavily unionized in most of this project's geography; both are politically salient, in the sense that visible failures (an unfilled classroom, a slow fire response) attract public and political attention; and both enjoy generally positive public regard. The existing mechanism memo already makes the parallel point for firefighters that public esteem is not evidence of abundant labor supply at the credentialed margin. This memo applies the identical caution to teachers, and treats "teachers are well-liked" and "teachers are easy to hire" as two separate, separately testable claims.

## 3. Teacher supply context

Public reporting supports the existence of real, if unevenly distributed, teacher staffing difficulty, while also cautioning against treating it as a uniform national crisis.

The U.S. Department of Education's Institute of Education Sciences (IES), through the NCES School Pulse Panel, reported that 74 percent of public schools had difficulty filling at least one teaching vacancy entering the 2024-25 school year, a decline from 79 percent the year before; schools reported an average of six teaching vacancies each and filled 79 percent of those vacancies with fully certified teachers, meaning roughly one in five reported vacancies was filled by a not-fully-certified hire or left open ([IES press release, 2024-10-17](https://ies.ed.gov/learn/press-release/most-u-s-public-elementary-and-secondary-schools-faced-hiring-challenges-start-2024-25-academic-year); [K-12 Dive summary](https://www.k12dive.com/news/schools-challenges-teacher-vacancies-decrease-2024-25-school-pulse-panel-education-department-nces/730163/)). The two most commonly cited hiring challenges were a lack of qualified candidates (64 percent of schools) and too few candidates applying at all (62 percent) — a useful distinction between an applicant-volume problem and an applicant-quality/certification problem, echoing the same volume-vs-qualification distinction raised for police recruitment in the prior workforce refinement memo. As of October 2024, half of public school leaders reported feeling their school was understaffed.

The Learning Policy Institute's most recent national scan states that special education (45 states), science (41 states), and math (40 states) are the most commonly reported statewide shortage subject areas, that roughly one in eight teaching positions nationally is either unfilled or filled by a teacher not fully certified for the role (affecting more than six million students), and that special education vacancies are especially acute: about half of all schools and 90 percent of high-poverty schools report difficulty finding qualified special education teachers ([LPI, An Overview of Teacher Shortages: 2025](https://learningpolicyinstitute.org/product/overview-teacher-shortages-2025-factsheet); [LPI, A Coming Crisis in Teaching?](https://learningpolicyinstitute.org/product/coming-crisis-teaching-brief)). LPI also frames shortages as likely to grow, driven by declining teacher-preparation-program enrollment, rising student enrollment in some areas, class-size-reduction policies, and high attrition — and makes an important point directly relevant to the wage-mechanism question: LPI's modeling suggests that if teacher attrition were cut roughly in half, to a rate comparable to some high-performing education systems internationally, most of the projected shortage would disappear. That is a retention/attrition problem more than a raw-applicant-volume problem, at least in LPI's framing, and retention and raw supply may call for different wage-relevant responses (LPI also separately studies turnover directly: [Teacher Turnover in the United States](https://learningpolicyinstitute.org/product/teacher-turnover-united-states-report)).

**Public sympathy is not the same claim as labor supply.** Consistent with the caution already applied to firefighters in the prior workforce memo, general public support for teachers as a profession says nothing about the size of the pool willing to complete a licensure pathway, accept a specific subject or grade assignment, and remain in the role for multiple years. The vacancy and shortage evidence above is about that narrower, credentialed-and-willing population, not about public opinion.

**Shortage existence is a separate claim from wage-translation mechanism.** None of the evidence above says anything about whether these shortages raise teacher wages, and by how much, in the specific districts affected. That is the question taken up in Sections 5 and 6. A shortage could show up as base-wage growth, as targeted shortage-area stipends, as lowered certification requirements, as larger class sizes, or as some mix of these — and distinguishing between them is exactly the kind of evidence question this project's discipline requires.

## 4. Teacher composition and observed wage levels

"Teacher wages," reported as a single average, is not a homogeneous quantity, and the project should not treat it as one. Public reporting on teacher pay — including Massachusetts's own state-level reporting — is generally an aggregate across a wide range of underlying job types and career stages:

- grade band (elementary, middle, secondary);
- special education, which the evidence above shows is disproportionately affected by shortages and disproportionately covered by non-fully-certified or provisionally certified staff in some districts;
- vocational/technical instruction, which often carries separate licensure and labor-market dynamics tied to trade/industry wages rather than to the general K-12 teacher labor market;
- bilingual/ESL instruction, another subject area flagged as high-shortage above;
- years of experience and step placement, since step-and-lane schedules (Section 5) mean a district's average pay is partly a function of how senior its teaching staff happens to be;
- education/lane placement (bachelor's vs. master's vs. doctoral, additional credit hours).

**Teacher assistants/paraprofessionals must not be merged into this comparison.** These roles (also called teacher aides, instructional aides, education assistants, or paraeducators) work under the supervision of a licensed teacher and are a distinct occupational category from teachers in the federal Standard Occupational Classification system used by BLS, with a separate labor market, separate (and generally lower) pay scale, and often no equivalent licensure requirement ([BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook, Teacher Assistants](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/education-training-and-library/teacher-assistants.htm)). Any composite "school employee wage" figure that blends teachers and paraprofessionals would badly understate teacher-specific wage levels and trends, and any GABRIEL attribute or wage-trend build touching schools needs to keep this boundary explicit, mirroring the existing occupation crosswalk's separate treatment of `teacher` from other categories.

**Why composition matters for comparing teachers to police/fire, stated as a concrete testable claim:** Massachusetts DESE's own public Teacher Salaries reporting gives salary total, FTE count, and average teacher salary at the district level ([DESE Teacher Salaries Report](https://profiles.doe.mass.edu/statereport/teachersalaries.aspx)), which is exactly the kind of statistic vulnerable to composition effects. If a district or region has a higher share of elementary teachers, or a younger/less senior teaching staff overall (for example, because of high turnover or recent expansion), its reported average teacher salary can be lower than a comparably-paying district with a more senior staff mix, even if the two districts' underlying step-and-lane salary schedules are identical or the first district's schedule is actually more generous. This means a naive "teacher wages grew more slowly than police/fire wages" comparison risks confusing a composition shift (e.g., a wave of teacher retirements followed by hiring at the bottom of the schedule) with genuine wage restraint. The project's existing occupation crosswalk already partially anticipates this by listing separate elementary and secondary teacher SOC categories and flagging that "school bargaining units may span multiple teacher titles" — this section makes that caution explicit as an analytical requirement rather than a footnote.

**Counterargument:** composition is not purely a measurement artifact to be corrected away. A district skewing younger because of high turnover, or skewing toward more special-education or bilingual positions because of enrollment shifts, may itself be a symptom of the same labor-market or budget conditions this project wants to measure. The evidence needed to tell these apart is a like-for-like comparison — average salary by grade band and experience band, or ideally the underlying schedule itself (Section 5) — rather than a single district-wide average.

## 5. Teacher salary schedules and market signals

The dominant compensation structure in U.S. public K-12 teaching is the step-and-lane salary schedule: pay is set primarily by years of experience (the step) and level of educational attainment or credit hours (the lane), laid out in a grid. Crucially, in its default form this schedule does **not** differentiate pay by grade level or subject area. A mathematics teacher, an elementary generalist, and a special education teacher with the same years of experience and the same lane placement are typically paid identically under a pure step-and-lane schedule, regardless of which of those positions is harder to fill.

This creates a specific, checkable hypothesis: uniform step-and-lane schedules may mute the wage signal that would otherwise respond to subject- or grade-specific shortages, because the schedule structure itself has no lever for it absent a deliberate policy add-on. Evidence on those add-ons:

- Roughly three in four surveyed districts (110 of a 148-district sample) report offering some form of differentiated compensation for hard-to-staff subjects — stipends, signing/retention bonuses, alternative schedules, loan-repayment assistance, or similar — with special education, math, science, and ESL/bilingual education the subjects most commonly targeted, consistent with the shortage-area evidence in Section 3 ([NCTQ, More districts are paying teachers strategically to meet critical needs](https://www.nctq.org/research-insights/more-districts-are-paying-teachers-strategically-to-meet-critical-needs-is-yours/); [NCTQ, The ins and outs of teacher salaries](https://www.nctq.org/research-insights/the-ins-and-outs-of-teacher-salaries/)).
- Concrete examples illustrate how varied these add-ons are in form: Detroit Public Schools Community District has offered a one-time $15,000 bonus for eligible teachers; West Virginia state policy grants math and special education teachers three additional years of "experience" credit when placed on the state salary schedule (effectively a lane/step manipulation rather than a separate pay track) and offers a loan-assistance program of up to $3,000 per year for up to five years ($15,000 total) for teachers in critical-need subjects or geographic areas ([NCTQ, More districts are paying teachers strategically](https://www.nctq.org/research-insights/more-districts-are-paying-teachers-strategically-to-meet-critical-needs-is-yours/)).

The design of these incentives matters for wage-trend interpretation: a one-time bonus (Detroit-style) sits outside the published base schedule and would not show up as base-wage growth in a schedule-based wage series; an experience-credit adjustment (West Virginia-style) permanently raises a specific group's position on the existing schedule; and a loan-assistance program is a recruitment subsidy rather than a wage change at all. This is directly analogous to the earlier caution about Massachusetts police paid-detail income sitting outside base wage and overtime — different compensation channels need to be captured as distinct verbatim spans, not conflated, per the two-corpus and verbatim-capture discipline already established in `CLAUDE.md`.

**Counterargument:** the NCTQ evidence also implies that roughly one in four surveyed districts reported no such differentiated pay mechanism, meaning uniform-schedule rigidity — and therefore muted subject-specific wage response — may still be the norm rather than the exception in a meaningful share of districts. Differentiated pay, where it exists, may also reflect local political and labor-relations bargaining outcomes (what a union was willing to accept, what a school committee was willing to offer) as much as a market-clearing response to measured scarcity, so its presence should not be read as proof that "the market cleared."

## 6. Shortage buffering vs. wage adjustment

The central asymmetry this memo wants to put on record is how a staffing shortfall gets absorbed differs structurally between teaching and public safety, and that difference plausibly matters for wage translation.

**Teacher-side buffering channels**, in likely rough order of frequency: assigning a long-term substitute rather than a certified teacher of record; hiring under an emergency or provisional certification pathway (raising the "not fully certified" share of filled positions documented in Section 3); increasing class size for the affected grade or subject; leaving a position vacant and redistributing students or course sections among remaining staff; or reducing or cutting a program, elective, or specialist role entirely (e.g., not offering an AP section, combining special-education caseloads, cutting a bilingual support position).

**Police/fire-side buffering channels**, per the prior workforce refinement memo: mandatory overtime, callback, and minimum-staffing rules that keep incumbent officers/firefighters working more hours at premium pay rather than leaving a shift uncovered.

The structural difference worth stating plainly: police/fire overtime buffering directly increases realized compensation paid to the existing workforce, even with no change to the negotiated base-wage schedule — it is a real, visible, and politically salient increase in personnel cost that itself becomes a source of budget and bargaining pressure over time. Most teacher-side buffering channels do the opposite: a long-term substitute is typically paid well below a certified teacher's rate; an emergency-certified hire may enter at the bottom of the schedule regardless of true market scarcity; a larger class size costs nothing extra per student in most standard schedules; and a cut program simply removes a cost rather than redistributing it to existing staff at a premium. In other words, teacher-side buffering is more likely to substitute toward cheaper labor or reduced service than to convert directly into more dollars paid to existing staff, which is a plausible structural reason non-safety wage growth could lag safety wage growth even under comparable measured staffing strain.

**Counterargument:** this asymmetry is not absolute. Some teacher CBAs include class-size-overage clauses that pay a stipend once a class exceeds a contractual cap, which would convert teacher-side buffering into a wage-adjacent cost much like overtime does for police/fire. Substitute staffing and recruiting/onboarding costs are also real budget line items even when they are not "teacher wage" costs, and repeated reliance on substitutes or emergency certification could itself become a bargaining grievance that eventually surfaces as a wage or staffing demand. The evidence needed to settle this is district-level tracking of whether staffing strain shows up primarily as (a) substitute/emergency-hire spending, (b) class-size-overage stipend spending, or (c) base-wage/schedule growth, compared against the equivalent police/fire overtime-vs-base-wage split in the same city and period.

## 7. Budget and institutional constraints

School spending in this project's Massachusetts-heavy corpus is constrained by the same general municipal finance environment as police and fire, but reaches the bargaining table through a different set of institutional channels.

Massachusetts's Proposition 2½ limits how much a city or town's property tax levy — the largest local revenue source for most municipalities — can grow each year: automatically, by 2.5 percent of the prior year's levy limit plus new growth from added construction/development, or beyond that only through a voter-approved override ([Mass.gov, Levy Limits: A Primer on Proposition 2½](https://www.mass.gov/doc/levy-limits-a-primer-on-proposition-2-12-0/download); [Mass.gov, Proposition 2½ overrides & exclusions](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/proposition-2-12-overrides-exclusions)). Because school budgets are typically the largest single line in a Massachusetts municipal budget, teacher compensation is directly exposed to this levy ceiling, and a school committee wanting to fund a larger raise, a shortage-area stipend program, or additional staffing may need either room under the existing levy limit, new growth revenue, an increase in state education aid, or a successful override vote. Massachusetts also layers a state education aid formula (commonly referred to as Chapter 70 aid) on top of local levy capacity — **background/analyst judgment on the specific formula mechanics, not independently re-verified via search this session**, though the general existence of a state foundation-aid formula for Massachusetts K-12 education is a long-standing, well-documented feature of state school finance and is flagged here for direct verification in a later session rather than left as an unstated assumption.

This is a structurally different path to the bargaining table than the police/fire path. Where JLMC gives Massachusetts police and fire units access to binding interest arbitration when bargaining reaches impasse (per the prior mechanism memo), Massachusetts public-sector bargaining generally, including teacher bargaining under M.G.L. c. 150E, does not extend that same binding-arbitration backstop for wages to non-safety units — **background/analyst judgment on the specific non-availability of binding wage arbitration for Massachusetts teacher units, not independently re-verified via search this session; flagged in the accompanying source-gap memo for direct verification.** If that is correct, it implies a possible institutional combination worth stating explicitly as a hypothesis rather than an assumption: non-safety units such as teachers may be barred from striking (as most Massachusetts public employees are) without holding the compensating access to a binding third-party wage-setting process, leaving impasse resolution comparatively weaker or more asymmetric than either a strike right or an arbitration backstop would provide on its own. This is the kind of institutional-comparison claim that should not be asserted without direct statutory verification, which is why it is listed here as a hypothesis and routed to the source-gap memo, not stated as settled fact.

Documentation channels differ accordingly: police/fire arbitration awards and JLMC materials often generate a formal written record of comparator reasoning, ability-to-pay findings, and other statutory criteria, as the existing mechanism memos discuss. Teacher compensation reasoning is more likely to surface in school committee budget books, override campaign materials, union bargaining fact sheets (which should be treated as advocacy sources, not neutral evidence — the Massachusetts Teachers Association's own "Fair Share" staffing report is one example of this kind of advocacy-framed source, useful as an illustration of union argument but not as a neutral wage-mechanism data source), and local news coverage of budget votes, rather than in a comparably formal award document.

## 9. New or refined hypotheses

The eight hypotheses below extend the project's existing hypothesis map. Five are added as new rows in `police_fire_wage_hypothesis_matrix_2026-07-02.csv` (`teacher_supply_pressure`, `teacher_shortage_buffering`, `teacher_composition_effect`, `teacher_salary_schedule_rigidity`, `non_safety_wage_restraint`); the remaining three are documented here and folded into light refinements of existing rows rather than added as new CSV rows, consistent with the project's stated preference to improve the existing map over proliferating new rows.

1. **`teacher_supply_pressure`** — Teacher shortages, concentrated in special education, STEM, and bilingual/ESL, create genuine hiring difficulty despite general public support for the profession, mirroring the esteem-vs-supply distinction already applied to firefighters. *(new CSV row)*
2. **`teacher_shortage_buffering`** — Teacher staffing strain is more often absorbed through substitutes, emergency certification, larger classes, or program cuts than through base-wage growth, structurally unlike police/fire overtime buffering, which directly raises realized pay to existing staff. *(new CSV row)*
3. **`teacher_composition_effect`** — Aggregate teacher salary figures can be pulled down by grade-level mix, subject mix, or staff seniority composition independent of the underlying salary schedule, so raw average-wage comparisons to police/fire risk a composition confound. *(new CSV row)*
4. **`teacher_salary_schedule_rigidity`** — Step-and-lane schedules do not differentiate pay by subject/grade by default, muting shortage-area wage response absent a deliberate stipend, bonus, or extra-step add-on. *(new CSV row)*
5. **`teacher_peer_district_comparability`** — Teacher bargaining commonly invokes "surrounding" or "comparable" district salary comparisons, but likely with weaker enforceability than the police/fire comparator ratchet because it lacks an arbitration backstop. *(folded into a refinement of H5, `comparator_ratchet`, rather than a new row)*
6. **`school_budget_constraint`** — Proposition 2½ levy limits, override votes, and state aid formulas are the concrete local channel through which fiscal capacity constrains teacher compensation in Massachusetts. *(folded into a refinement of H9, `fiscal_capacity_ability_to_pay`, rather than a new row)*
7. **`non_safety_wage_restraint`** — The police/fire vs. non-safety gap could grow because non-safety wage-setting institutions convert comparable labor-market strain into buffering or budget deferral rather than wage growth, not only because safety wages accelerate. *(new CSV row; the general, cross-occupation mirror of `teacher_shortage_buffering`)*
8. **`non_safety_service_deferral_or_substitution`** — Non-safety services can generally substitute cheaper labor or defer/reduce service more easily than public safety's harder immediate-coverage floor allows, though this needs testing against counterexamples (nursing, transit) already flagged in the prior workforce refinement memo. *(documented here as a general framing concept motivating #2 and the DPW/clerical roadmap in Section 11/roadmap memo; not yet given its own CSV row, pending DPW/clerical evidence to populate it meaningfully)*

## 10. Implications for future source extraction

None of the following should be built into a live GABRIEL attribute in this session; they are recorded so that when teacher-side mechanism extraction is eventually prototyped, it uses signals that are already disciplined against the composition and buffering confounds developed above.

Recommended textual signals to look for in future non-safety/teacher source extraction:

`teacher shortage`, `vacancy`, `long-term substitute`, `emergency certification`, `provisional license`, `under-certified`, `hard-to-fill`, `special education shortage`, `STEM shortage`, `bilingual/ESL shortage`, `retention`, `burnout`, `class size`, `step and lane`, `salary schedule`, `surrounding districts`, `peer districts`, `competitive salary`, `budget constraint`, `state aid`, `levy limit`, `override`, `paraprofessional shortage`, `teacher assistant`.

Extraction discipline notes, consistent with the verbatim-capture and two-corpus rules already established in `CLAUDE.md`:

- Capture whether shortage/vacancy language names a specific subject, grade band, or credential type, rather than scoring generic "teacher shortage" mentions as equivalent to a subject-specific shortage claim.
- Capture stipend/bonus/extra-step/loan-assistance language as a distinct verbatim span from the base step-and-lane schedule language, so one-time incentives are not conflated with permanent base-wage changes.
- Capture `teacher assistant` / `paraprofessional` mentions as a separate lane entirely from teacher-specific mechanism text; do not let a document's staffing narrative about aides get scored as teacher-specific evidence.
- Capture `levy limit` / `override` / `state aid` language only when tied to an explicit compensation or staffing decision, not generic municipal budget narrative, mirroring the existing `fiscal_capacity_ability_to_pay` discipline.
- Capture `surrounding districts` / `peer districts` / `competitive salary` language, and separately note whether the surrounding text indicates a binding process (e.g., an arbitration award) or a persuasive one (e.g., a budget hearing or override campaign document).

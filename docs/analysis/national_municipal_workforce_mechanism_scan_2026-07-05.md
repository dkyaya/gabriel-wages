# National Municipal Workforce Mechanism Scan

**Date:** 2026-07-05
**Scope:** bounded national qualitative scan across police, fire, teachers/school employees, DPW/public works, and clerical/administrative workers. No GABRIEL run, no model/API calls, no OEWS/BLS data build, no ingestion, no metadata edits, no causal claim.

## 1. Purpose and scope

This memo scans national context across the five occupation groups this project has developed mechanism maps for, with one specific purpose: to check which of the mechanisms and institutional findings already documented (largely from Massachusetts sources) generalize beyond Massachusetts, and which are Massachusetts-specific. It does not ingest documents, run GABRIEL, or build any dataset. It does not make a causal claim about why police/fire wages rise faster than other municipal occupations anywhere, in Massachusetts or nationally — it is mechanism discovery and generalizability checking only, consistent with every prior memo in this project.

## 2. Why the national scan matters

Every mechanism-development session so far in this project — the original police/fire memos, the teacher memos, the DPW memos, and the clerical/admin memos — has drawn its corpus evidence and much of its institutional verification from Massachusetts sources: this project's own city contracts, Massachusetts DESE, the Massachusetts Department of Labor Relations, the Joint Labor Management Committee (JLMC), and the Massachusetts Municipal Association. That concentration was a reasonable and disciplined way to build the mechanism map with real, verifiable, corpus-grounded evidence rather than speculation. But it creates a specific risk: a finding that is genuinely a Massachusetts institutional feature (a specific statute, a specific agency, a specific numeric threshold) could be mistaken for a general truth about how U.S. municipal wage-setting works, simply because it is the only version of the mechanism this project has directly verified. The clearest example already flagged in this project's own memos is JLMC: this project has correctly avoided calling JLMC itself a national institution, but it has been less explicit about whether the *underlying policy pattern* JLMC represents — a no-strike-for-compulsory-arbitration trade specific to police and fire — is common or rare among other states. This scan closes that gap directly. Distinguishing which hypotheses generalize (and how) from which are artifacts of this project's Massachusetts-heavy source base is a precondition for deciding whether any future GABRIEL attribute should be designed around a Massachusetts-specific institutional framing or a more general one.

## 4. Police national context

**Applicant supply and hiring pipeline.** The most directly comparable, credible national data point found this session is MissionSquare Research Institute's State and Local Government Workforce Survey, which tracks "hard to fill" ratings across occupations. The 2024 survey found policing rated hard-to-fill by 68 percent of responding state/local employers — real and substantial, but **not the single hardest local-government occupation to fill**: mental health professionals (83 percent), nursing (77 percent), and corrections/jail positions (74 percent) all ranked higher in the same survey ([MissionSquare, 2024 State and Local Government Workforce Survey Report](https://research.missionsq.org/content/media/document/2024/4/WorkforceSurveyReport2024.pdf)). This is an important calibration: police recruitment difficulty is a real, national, cross-jurisdiction phenomenon, but framing it as an unusually or uniquely severe staffing crisis relative to *other* local-government occupations is not supported by this comparison — several health- and corrections-adjacent occupations report even greater difficulty. The 2025 iteration of the same survey found hiring difficulty had eased across the board, with 19 tracked occupations showing a 10-percentage-point-or-more drop in "hard to fill" ratings since 2022 ([MissionSquare, 2025 survey results](https://www.missionsq.org/about-us/news-and-updates/media-inquiries/news-20250708-msqstateandlocalgovernmentemployersfacedfewerhiringchallengesinthepastyear.html)), consistent with the "recruitment challenges easing" finding already cited in the prior clerical/admin mechanism memo.

**Recruitment/retention/resignation/retirement.** No new national quantitative source beyond what prior memos already cited (PERF-adjacent reporting, BLS JOLTS-derived quit-rate context) was found this session; this remains an area where the prior memos' own flagged gap (a direct, non-secondary PERF/IACP release with a multi-year applicant trend) is still open nationally, not just for Massachusetts.

**Post-2020 compound shock.** This session did not find new national evidence beyond what the existing workforce refinement memo already established: the legitimacy/scrutiny channel, COVID-era hiring disruption, pension-vintage retirement timing, and the broad 2021-2023 labor-market churn remain analytically distinct, separately-testable channels nationally, not just in Massachusetts. Nothing found this session weakens or strengthens that existing disentangling framework; it is restated here as still the operative national framing, not overwritten.

**Overtime/minimum staffing/patrol coverage; academy/training bottlenecks; lateral hiring competition.** No new national-level quantitative source was located this session beyond what is already cited in the existing police/fire memos (BLS OOH's general injury-rate and shift-structure descriptions). These remain `partially searched` in the living checklist.

**Public-safety salience.** No new national source found; remains a hypothesis this project has not directly tested with comparative local-news-coverage data anywhere, Massachusetts included.

**Arbitration/collective bargaining variation across states — the most significant new finding this session.** Compulsory interest arbitration for police and fire bargaining impasses is **not unique to Massachusetts**. It exists, in varying statutory forms, in at least New Jersey (Public Employment Relations Commission's Police and Fire Interest Arbitration program, with arbitrators randomly selected from a Special Panel), New York (police and fire unions have had access to compulsory binding interest arbitration for "nearly 40 years," per commentary on New York's Taylor Law-era provisions), Michigan (Public Act 312, effective 1969, which itself is described as Michigan "joining" Rhode Island and Pennsylvania in permitting compulsory police/fire arbitration), Wyoming (compulsory arbitration specifically for fire department disputes), and Oklahoma (statutory public policy establishing arbitration as the alternative to a denied strike right for permanent police and fire employees) ([NJ PERC, Police and Fire Interest Arbitration](https://www.nj.gov/perc/conciliation/policeandfire/); [Michigan Public Act 312 of 1969](https://www.legislature.mi.gov/documents/mcl/pdf/mcl-Act-312-of-1969.pdf); [Oklahoma Statutes §11-51-101](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-11/section-11-51-101/)). The consistent policy rationale across these states, as stated in the Michigan-era legal commentary, is to compensate police and fire unions for the loss of the right to strike — the same no-strike-for-arbitration trade this project has already identified as JLMC's rationale in Massachusetts. **What generalizes:** the policy pattern itself — a state deciding that police/fire essential-service status justifies pairing a strike prohibition with a compulsory, binding, third-party wage-setting backstop — recurs across a meaningful set of states. **What is Massachusetts-specific:** the particular administrative vehicle (JLMC), its particular statutory criteria, and this project's own corpus evidence (Somerville and Wayland award text) describe Massachusetts's implementation of that pattern specifically, not the pattern's only instance. This is not a majority-of-states finding — most states were not confirmed to have a police/fire-specific compulsory-arbitration statute in this bounded scan — but it is enough states, spanning several regions, to treat "police/fire compulsory arbitration as a distinct essential-services policy choice" as a recurring national pattern rather than a Massachusetts idiosyncrasy.

## 5. Fire national context

**Career vs. volunteer distinction; volunteer recruitment/retention stress.** This is now the best-quantified national mechanism found across this entire scan. NFPA data shows volunteer firefighter numbers fell from 897,750 in 1984 to 676,900 in 2020 — a 25 percent decline over a period when the U.S. population grew roughly 40 percent — with the volunteer count reaching a further low in 2023 ([NFPA data, as reported via multiple fire-service outlets citing the same NFPA figures](https://firefighternow.com/volunteer-firefighter-statistics/); [IAFF, Communities shift to all-career fire departments as volunteer numbers decline](https://www.iaff.org/news/communities-shift-to-all-career-fire-departments-as-volunteer-numbers-decline/)). Contributing factors identified in this reporting include a more-than-tripling of call volume over 40 years (driven substantially by EMS calls), NFPA 1001 professional-qualification standards and state certification requirements that have substantially raised the time commitment required of a volunteer since the 1980s, and broader social/economic trends (dual-income households, longer commutes, less flexible work schedules) that reduce the pool of people able to commit to daytime volunteer response. The reported adaptation — communities converting to combination (volunteer-plus-career) departments — is exactly the transition-pressure channel this project's hypothesis matrix (H11) already hypothesizes, now with a national quantitative anchor rather than only a qualitative national/regional framing.

**EMS/paramedic credential scarcity.** No new national-level quantitative source specific to EMS/paramedic credential scarcity (as distinct from the broader call-volume-growth point above) was found this session; this remains `partially searched`.

**Minimum staffing/station coverage; overtime/callback; training burden.** No new national source found this session beyond what prior memos already cite; these remain areas for future targeted search.

**Public esteem vs. labor supply distinction.** No new national source found this session; the existing caution (public esteem is not evidence of labor supply at the credentialed, schedule-willing margin) is restated, not newly evidenced, by this scan.

**Municipal conversion or hybrid staffing pressure.** Directly supported by the IAFF/NFPA reporting above — this is a real, documented, national trend, not a hypothetical channel.

**Arbitration/collective bargaining variation across states.** The same states identified in Section 4 for police (New Jersey, New York, Michigan, Rhode Island, Pennsylvania, Wyoming, Oklahoma) generally extend the same compulsory-arbitration treatment to fire as to police, since most of these statutes are framed as covering "police and fire" jointly (Michigan's Act 312 title itself: "Compulsory Arbitration of Labor Disputes in Police and Fire Departments"). **What generalizes and what is Massachusetts-specific** mirrors the police finding in Section 4 exactly.

## 6. Teacher national context

**Vacancies, hiring difficulty, under-certification, hard-to-fill areas.** No new national source beyond what the prior teacher mechanism memo already cites (NCES/IES School Pulse Panel, Learning Policy Institute) was pursued further this session, since those findings were already well-sourced and directly national in scope; they are restated here as still current, not re-verified from scratch.

**Teacher composition effects.** No new national source found this session; the existing composition-effect framing (grade/subject/experience mix distorting district-average figures) remains a hypothesis grounded in general measurement logic and the specific Massachusetts DESE example already cited, not newly tested nationally.

**Step-and-lane salary schedules; shortage buffering.** No new national source found this session beyond the existing NCTQ citation (roughly three in four sampled districts nationally offer some shortage-area differentiated pay); this remains the operative national evidence.

**School finance variation across states — a significant generalizability finding.** State school funding formulas nationally fall into a small number of recognized types: flat grants, foundation formulas, guaranteed-tax-base (also called power-equalization) formulas, and combination formulas, with the foundation-formula model described as "the most popular" nationally — a state sets a minimum per-pupil spending target, calculates each district's local fiscal capacity, and fills the gap with state aid ([Urban Institute, How do school funding formulas work?](https://apps.urban.org/features/funding-formulas/)). This is structurally identical in architecture to Massachusetts's Chapter 70 system (foundation budget minus required local contribution equals state aid), and comparable formula structures were found in other states' own descriptions: New Jersey's aid formula is stated directly as "Adequacy Budget – Local Share = Equalization Aid" ([NJPSA/FEA, Understanding the Mechanics of School Funding](https://njpsa.org/understanding-the-mechanics-of-school-funding/)), and Wisconsin's is described as a cost-sharing formula where the state and district shares are set by the district's relative property wealth ([Wisconsin DPI, Equalization Aid Formula](https://dpi.wi.gov/sfs/aid/general/equalization/formula)). **What generalizes:** the foundation-formula architecture itself — a state-set minimum spending floor combined with a local-capacity-based required contribution — is the dominant national model, not a Massachusetts invention. **What is Massachusetts-specific:** the exact parameters (the 82.5 percent required-local-contribution cap, the specific "net school spending" definition, and Proposition 2½'s specific levy-limit mechanics) are Massachusetts's implementation choices within that general architecture, and should not be assumed to transfer numerically to another state even where the same general formula type applies.

**Teacher collective bargaining and impasse variation across states — a second significant generalizability finding, with an important internal-source discrepancy flagged.** Thirty-five states are reported to authorize collective bargaining for teachers with some combination of mediation, fact-finding, and/or arbitration procedures for impasse resolution, and roughly half of all states extend some strike right to teachers (with 27 states prohibiting teacher strikes) ([summarized from search results citing NCTQ's Collective Bargaining Map, an Education Commission of the States policy summary, and general secondary legal reference sources](https://teacherquality.nctq.org/contract-database/collectiveBargaining)). Critically, **at least one state (Iowa) is reported to allow its state labor board to order binding arbitration for teacher bargaining impasses at either party's request** — a materially different, more compulsory arrangement than Massachusetts's voluntary-only Chapter 150E Section 9 arbitration route. **This session also surfaced a direct discrepancy worth flagging rather than silently resolving:** one secondary source describes Massachusetts's own teacher impasse procedure as including "mediation and arbitration," with "strikes... permitted after impasse resolution procedures have been exhausted" — a characterization that conflicts with this project's own directly-verified finding (from `ma_teacher_bargaining_school_finance_institutional_context_2026-07-04.md`, sourced from Mass.gov's own Department of Labor Relations pages and M.G.L. c. 150E §9 directly) that Massachusetts teachers generally cannot strike and lack a compulsory arbitration route. This project's own direct, primary-source verification (Mass.gov DLR guidance stating non-police/fire bargaining has "no arbitration process," and the general Massachusetts public-sector no-strike rule under Chapter 150E) should be treated as authoritative for Massachusetts specifically; the secondary source's more general characterization is flagged here as a caution about relying on tertiary legal-reference summaries for state-specific accuracy, not as a correction to this project's own verified finding. **What generalizes:** the general architecture (mediation, then fact-finding, then possibly arbitration) is common across roughly three-fifths of states; strike prohibition is common but far from universal. **What is Massachusetts-specific:** the specific finding that Massachusetts's arbitration route is voluntary-only and rarely invoked is a Massachusetts fact, not a claim about all 35 states that "authorize" some impasse procedure — some of those states (Iowa being the clearest example found) provide a materially more compulsory mechanism than Massachusetts does.

## 7. DPW / public works national context

**Operational essentiality; snow/storm/emergency response.** No new national source found this session beyond what the prior DPW mechanism memo already established (municipal risk-management guidance on contractor supplementation during storms); this remains the operative national evidence.

**CDL/equipment operator scarcity.** Restated, not re-investigated this session: the existing finding stands — national industry reporting claims a large truck-driver deficit, but BLS's own 2019 Monthly Labor Review analysis found no evidence of a secular shortage, with real driver wages essentially flat since 2010. This is a national labor-economics finding, not Massachusetts-specific, and it continues to caution against treating "CDL shortage" language at face value anywhere.

**Water/sewer/wastewater operator certification and workforce pipelines.** No new national source pursued beyond what the DPW mechanism memo already cites (the national "silver tsunami" retirement-wave statistics and NEIWPCC's New England-regional documentation); this remains the strongest genuine DPW-side credential-scarcity case, and it is explicitly a national-plus-regional finding, not Massachusetts-specific — NEIWPCC covers multiple New England states, not Massachusetts alone.

**Mechanics/skilled trades/private-sector competition.** No new national source found this session; the underlying logic (DPW mechanics/operators compete in the general private-sector construction/trades labor market) is restated from prior work, not newly evidenced.

**Overtime/callback/standby; contractor substitution and service deferral; classification fragmentation.** No new national source found this session beyond the existing municipal risk-management guidance already cited; the Massachusetts-specific prevailing-wage-law wrinkle on contractor substitution remains flagged as Massachusetts-specific, since this session did not verify whether other states' prevailing-wage laws (many states do have some form of prevailing-wage statute) apply comparably to municipal contractor substitution for DPW-type work.

**Public salience relative to police/fire.** No new national source found this session; remains untested empirically anywhere in this project.

**Collective bargaining/impasse variation across states — the most consequential generalizability finding for DPW specifically.** DPW/public works workers' collective bargaining rights vary far more dramatically across states than any other mechanism reviewed in this scan, because the underlying *right to bargain at all* is not universal. Public-sector collective bargaining is reported to be banned outright in North Carolina and South Carolina, and Virginia banned all public-sector bargaining until a 2020 reform permitted local governments to grant it by local ordinance ([summarized from search results citing EPI's "States with Comprehensive Collective Bargaining Laws" map and general public-sector labor-law reference sources](https://files.epi.org/uploads/bwp-collective-bargaining-map.pdf)). **What generalizes:** DPW's general lack of a compulsory-arbitration backstop (already verified for Massachusetts specifically) is *directionally* consistent with the general pattern that non-safety municipal occupations nationally have weaker impasse-resolution rights than police/fire, where any bargaining exists at all. **What is Massachusetts-specific and should not be assumed to transfer:** the entire premise that a DPW mechanism map can be built from CBA text depends on DPW workers having a right to organize and bargain in the first place — a premise that is simply false in North Carolina, South Carolina, and (prior to 2020) Virginia. Any future national or multi-state extension of this project's DPW mechanism work must check each target state's basic public-sector bargaining-rights status before assuming a CBA-based approach is even viable there.

## 8. Clerical / administrative national context

**Classification systems and internal equity; reclassification pressure.** This session's most direct generalizability finding for clerical/admin: formal civil-service classification-and-reclassification-appeal systems, structurally similar to what Boston's clerical/admin CBA documents, are a **broadly national, not Massachusetts-specific, tradition**. New York State's Comptroller maintains a formal reclassification process; the federal Office of Personnel Management maintains classification/job-grading appeal procedures with published FAQs; New Jersey has its own civil-service classification review and appeal process; and Massachusetts's own Civil Service Commission (a state-level body, separate from Boston's municipal-level OHR process) hears classification-related appeals as one of several appeal types ([NY State Comptroller, Reclassifications](https://www.osc.ny.gov/state-agencies/payroll/payroll-manual/reclassifications); [OPM, What is a classification or job grading appeal?](https://www.opm.gov/frequently-asked-questions/classification-faq/general/what-is-a-classification-or-job-grading-appeal/); [Mass.gov, Learn more about the types of appeals heard by the Civil Service Commission](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/learn-about-types-of-civil-service-commission-appeals)). A common structural feature across these systems, described in the search results for federal and state-level classification appeals generally, is that a successful reclassification claim typically requires showing the employee spends a majority of work time performing the higher-graded position's distinguishing duties — a substantive standard, not merely a procedural one. **What generalizes:** the basic architecture (a classification-and-compensation office makes an initial determination; an appeal exists; the appeal typically turns on actual duties performed, not felt workload or general seniority) is a national civil-service norm. **What is Massachusetts-specific and untested nationally:** whether Boston's unusually narrow "arbitrary or capricious" arbitration standard (as opposed to a full merits review) is typical, unusual, or an outlier compared with how other cities structure the *final* dispute-resolution step of their own classification-appeal processes was not directly tested in this bounded scan and remains an open comparison.

**Pay compression / minimum-wage compression.** Restated from the existing multi-state Century Foundation estimate already cited in the clerical/admin mechanism memo (Massachusetts one of six jurisdictions analyzed, weighted average ~0.6 percent of payroll per year); no new national source pursued this session beyond confirming this estimate is explicitly multi-state, not Massachusetts-specific in its underlying data.

**Specialized municipal knowledge; workload/backlog absorption; automation/digitization/reorganization; part-time/shared-services/confidential-employee status.** No new national sources specific to these mechanisms were pursued this session beyond what the prior clerical/admin mechanism and corpus-scan memos already establish; the "confidential employee" bargaining exclusion documented for Massachusetts (via the MMA Select Board Handbook) was not tested this session for whether other states have a comparable category, though the general concept of excluding managerial/confidential employees from bargaining units is a standard feature of most states' public-sector labor law (a general inference from labor-law structure, not a source-verified national claim this session).

**Public salience.** No new national source found this session; remains untested empirically.

**Gendered occupational valuation — grounded further, still explicitly a hypothesis requiring evidence, not a conclusion.** This session found credible academic anchoring for the comparable-worth concept the prior clerical/admin memo flagged: the general research finding that female-dominated occupations tend to pay less than male-dominated occupations of comparable skill, effort, and responsibility is associated with sociologist Paula England's body of work on occupational feminization and wages, and is discussed at length in Institute for Women's Policy Research (IWPR) research on occupational segregation and the gender wage gap ([AAPSS, Paula England: Why female-dominated jobs systematically pay less](https://www.aapss.org/paula-england-why-female-dominated-jobs-systematically-pay-less/); [IWPR, Occupational Segregation and the Gender Wage Gap](https://iwpr.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/C419.pdf)). A Congressional Research Service report notes that "the comparable worth approach to wage determination has made the most headway in state and local governments" specifically, meaning some U.S. state/local governments have historically adopted formal comparable-worth wage-setting reforms as policy, not merely as an academic finding ([EveryCRSReport, The Gender Wage Gap and Pay Equity: Is Comparable Worth the Next Step?](https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/98-278.html)). **This remains, per the explicit task instruction, a hypothesis requiring dedicated evidence, not a conclusion about this project's specific Massachusetts cities.** Nothing found this session provides occupational gender-composition data matched to wage data for any of this project's specific cities, and nothing found this session should be read as asserting that gendered occupational valuation explains any of this project's actual findings. It is now better-anchored academically than before, which is useful for knowing what evidence *would* be needed (gender-composition data plus a skill/complexity comparison, in the comparable-worth research tradition) — it is not evidence that the mechanism is operating in this project's specific data.

**Collective bargaining/impasse variation across states.** The same general public-sector bargaining-rights variation described in Section 7 for DPW applies equally to clerical/admin: in states without public-sector collective bargaining rights, the entire premise of a CBA-based clerical/admin mechanism map does not apply, and clerical/admin pay would instead be governed entirely by unilateral personnel-bylaw-style administration — the same wage-setting channel already identified as the "confidential employee" mechanism for a narrower set of Massachusetts positions, but potentially the *default* channel for all clerical/admin employees in a state with no public-sector bargaining rights at all.

## 10. Source-document production effects

This section restates and extends, at the national level, a caution this project's mechanism memos have already applied to Massachusetts specifically: different source-document types systematically preserve different kinds of wage-setting reasoning, independent of how important that reasoning actually is to the underlying wage outcome. This is not new to the national scan, but the scan makes clear it is not a Massachusetts artifact either — it follows directly from *why* documents get written in the first place, which is a general feature of institutional processes, not a state-specific one.

- **Police/fire arbitration or JLMC-equivalent materials** preserve comparability, ability-to-pay, and staffing reasoning in unusual detail because the relevant statute (in Massachusetts, JLMC's enabling statute; in other states, their own equivalent police/fire arbitration statutes) directs the arbitrator to weigh specific named criteria and issue a written award applying them. This is true wherever a compulsory-arbitration statute exists, not only in Massachusetts.
- **Teacher school committee/budget/bargaining pages** are more likely to preserve salary-competitiveness and school-finance reasoning (because school budgets are subject to public hearings, override votes, and — per the national school-finance-formula finding in Section 6 — a state-mandated floor calculation that must be documented) than a teacher CBA itself, which (per this project's own Seekonk corpus example) may contain very little of that reasoning in its own text.
- **DPW CBAs** preserve classification, license, overtime, and contractor language in detail (as this project's own corpus scan documented extensively) but rarely preserve recruitment/retention rhetoric or explicit "shortage" framing — that framing, where it exists, is more likely to appear in national workforce-association materials (APWA, AWWA/WEF) or in a municipality's own budget narrative than in the CBA text itself.
- **Clerical/admin CBAs** preserve classification/reclassification and management-rights language (again, per this project's own Boston corpus example, in unusual procedural detail) far more than any public-facing wage narrative — clerical/admin bargaining rarely generates the kind of public budget-hearing or override-campaign documentation that teacher bargaining does, because clerical/admin budgets are less often the subject of a discrete public funding fight.
- **Budget books, HR reports, salary/compensation studies, job postings, and news** are likely necessary, for every occupation group, to observe mechanisms (vacancy rates, applicant counts, workload/backlog evidence, explicit recruitment/retention framing) that this project's own multiple corpus scans have now confirmed are **absent from CBA text as a general pattern**, not a Massachusetts-specific gap — DPW's and clerical/admin's own dedicated corpus-scan sessions each independently found the same absence (no recruitment/retention framing language, no peer-community comparability language) in every document reviewed.

The practical implication, restated for clarity: a future GABRIEL attribute or source-extraction effort targeting recruitment/retention pressure, workload/backlog absorption, or peer-comparability reasoning for any non-safety occupation should not expect to find that language in CBA text primarily, based on the consistent pattern across every corpus scan this project has run so far. It should instead prioritize budget documents, HR/workforce reports, and (for police/fire specifically) arbitration award text, where the relevant statutory or institutional requirement to document that reasoning actually exists.

## 12. Implications for future GABRIEL/source extraction

None of the following should be built into a live GABRIEL attribute in this session. They are recorded as design guidance for whenever source-extraction work resumes, organized by what appears nationally relevant versus Massachusetts-specific.

**Textual signals that appear nationally relevant** (should be captured generically, without assuming a Massachusetts-specific vehicle name applies):
- `interest arbitration`, `binding arbitration`, `impasse`, `mediation`, `fact-finding` — capture the *outcome* (compulsory vs. voluntary; binding vs. advisory) rather than assuming any specific named institution (JLMC or otherwise) will appear.
- `foundation budget`, `required local contribution`, `local share`, `state aid formula`, `equalization aid` — capture the *type* of school-finance formula language present, not only Chapter-70-specific phrasing.
- `reclassification`, `classification appeal`, `classification and compensation`, `arbitrary or capricious` — capture the presence and, where stated, the *scope* of the final appeal/arbitration standard, since this varies even where the general architecture is present everywhere.
- `volunteer firefighter`, `combination department`, `career conversion` — now nationally quantifiable (NFPA data), so extraction can reasonably expect to find this language in fire-service sources across states, not only Massachusetts.
- `CDL`, `equipment operator`, `water operator`, `wastewater operator` credential-to-pay language — capture whether pay is embedded in base classification versus paid as a separate stipend, a distinction already observed to vary even within this project's own Massachusetts cities (Franklin vs. Arlington), and plausibly variable across states too.

**Textual signals that are Massachusetts-specific and should not be assumed to transfer:**
- `JLMC`, `Chapter 589`, `Chapter 1078` — Massachusetts-specific statutory/institutional names; a multi-state extraction effort should search for each state's own equivalent statute by its own name.
- `Chapter 70`, `net school spending`, `Proposition 2½`, `82.5%` — Massachusetts-specific school-finance and levy-limit terminology; use general labels (`foundation aid`, `local levy limit`) for any cross-state coding scheme.
- `Massachusetts Department of Labor Relations`, `MMA Select Board Handbook`, `OHR Classification and Compensation Unit` (Boston-specific) — institution-specific names that will not appear in another state's or city's documents even where an equivalent institution exists under a different name.

**General extraction-design implication:** any future multi-state or national extraction effort should code the *institutional pattern* (compulsory vs. voluntary arbitration; foundation vs. guaranteed-tax-base school finance; classification-appeal-with-narrow-vs-broad-final-review) as the primary variable, with the specific named institution captured as a secondary, jurisdiction-identifying field — the reverse of treating "JLMC" or "Chapter 70" as if they were themselves the mechanism.

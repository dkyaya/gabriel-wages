# Police and Fire Workforce Context — Source Gap List

**Date:** 2026-07-03
**Scope:** desk-research planning only. Lists the highest-value evidence gaps surfaced by the workforce context refinement (`police_fire_workforce_context_refinement_2026-07-03.md`). No sources were fetched or downloaded to produce this list beyond what is already cited in the existing mechanism memo/bibliography; this is a punch list for future sessions, not a completed literature review.

For each gap: why it matters, likely source families, and a feasibility tag (`desk_research_feasible`, `data_build_feasible`, `likely_hard`).

## 1. Police applicant counts over time

**Why it matters:** distinguishes whether applicant-pool decline predates 2020 (a longer-run structural story) from a 2020-specific shock, and is the single most direct test of the recruitment/retention hypothesis (H1) and the post-2020 climate hypothesis (H2).

**Likely source families:** PERF/IACP staffing surveys (direct release, not the AP summary currently in the bibliography), individual large-department annual reports, academy class-size records, state POST/certification-board data on academy enrollment over time.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` for finding the direct PERF/IACP release; `data_build_feasible` if agency-level multi-year applicant series need to be assembled city by city; `likely_hard` for a nationally representative time series, since most public reporting is cross-sectional or survey-based rather than a consistent panel.

## 2. Police resignation/retirement series

**Why it matters:** needed to separate the legitimacy/scrutiny channel from pension-vintage retirement timing and general labor-market churn (Section 3 of the refinement memo).

**Likely source families:** state or municipal pension-system annual reports (often list retirements by year and sometimes by eligibility date), city HR/civil-service annual reports, PERF surveys, news coverage of specific department resignation waves.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` — public pension-system reports for this project's current Massachusetts cities are plausibly obtainable (PERAC-adjacent municipal retirement boards); assembling a comparable series would take real effort but is not blocked by access.

## 3. Firefighter applicant trend evidence

**Why it matters:** directly tests whether fire departments have higher or lower applicant-to-seat ratios than police departments in comparable cities, which bears on why median fire pay is lower than police pay (Section 4 of the refinement memo) without invoking a training-quality explanation.

**Likely source families:** NFPA/FPRF workforce studies, individual career-department civil-service exam registration counts, local news coverage of academy class sizes.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` for national-level NFPA/FPRF studies; `likely_hard` for a clean city-by-city applicant-to-seat comparison against police, since fire departments do not always publish exam registration counts as consistently as police departments do.

## 4. Firefighter volunteer recruitment/retention evidence

**Why it matters:** establishes the size and trend of the volunteer pipeline that, per Section 4 of the refinement memo, transmits into career fire-wage pressure only indirectly and with a lag. Needed before treating any volunteer-side evidence as relevant to a career CBA wage claim.

**Likely source families:** NVFC recruitment/retention program materials and surveys, NFPA's periodic U.S. Fire Department Profile survey (headcount of career vs. volunteer firefighters over time), state fire marshal or state fire-service association reports.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` — NFPA's fire department profile survey is a recurring public product and should be directly citable rather than relying on secondary summaries.

## 5. Training-cost and training-time evidence

**Why it matters:** needed to evaluate the credentialing/training-barrier hypothesis (H10) on its own terms — as a supply constraint and replacement-cost driver — without implying anything about relative training quality across occupations, which the task explicitly rules out as a claim.

**Likely source families:** state POST/fire-academy curriculum hour requirements, EMT/paramedic certification requirement schedules, municipal training-budget line items, academy attrition/pass-rate reporting where public.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` for state-level academy hour requirements (usually published by state training commissions); `data_build_feasible` for linking training-time/cost figures to specific cities in this project's corpus.

## 6. Overtime/vacancy budget reports with non-safety comparisons

**Why it matters:** the single most important gap for the non-safety-comparison discipline (Section 5 of the refinement memo). Without a same-city, same-period comparison of overtime and vacancy patterns across police/fire and at least one non-safety unit (nursing, transit, sanitation, public works), any public-safety-specific staffing-pressure claim remains an assertion rather than a comparison.

**Likely source families:** municipal budget documents (overtime line items by department), city council/finance-committee overtime reports, state auditor reports, local news investigations of departmental overtime spending.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible` — municipal budgets in this project's current Massachusetts cities are generally public and often itemize overtime by department, so a same-city cross-department comparison is a realistic near-term build, distinct from (and smaller than) the full OEWS/ASPEP wage-trend build that is explicitly out of scope for this session.

## 7. Arbitration award behavior / split-the-difference evidence

**Why it matters:** the only way to actually test the criteria-applying vs. splitting-behavior distinction laid out in Section 6 of the refinement memo is to observe award text that states both parties' final positions and the awarded figure.

**Likely source families:** published interest-arbitration awards (JLMC awards in Massachusetts, other states' PERB/PELRB award archives), labor-relations casebooks and law-review treatments of final-offer arbitration (e.g., the Farber/Bazerman line of arbitration-behavior research), state statutory text on conventional vs. final-offer arbitration.

**Feasibility:** `desk_research_feasible` for the academic/legal literature framing; `likely_hard` for assembling enough award-level (offer, offer, award) triples to run an actual bunching/centering test, since many awards are not systematically archived or may require FOIA/records requests per this project's existing intake rules.

## 8. Wage-trend baseline

**Why it matters:** the descriptive OEWS/ASPEP baseline is already planned in `police_fire_wage_trend_baseline_implementation_plan_2026-07-03.md` and is explicitly out of scope for this session, but is listed here for completeness as the largest single outstanding data-build gap connecting workforce mechanism context to observable wage trends.

**Likely source families:** BLS OEWS annual tables, Census ASPEP tables, as already scoped in the existing implementation plan.

**Feasibility:** `data_build_feasible`, per the existing implementation plan; not to be started from this session.

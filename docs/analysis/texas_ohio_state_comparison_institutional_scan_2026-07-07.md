# Texas and Ohio State-Comparison Institutional Scan

**Date:** 2026-07-07
**Type:** scoping / institutional-audit / source-target planning memo. Not a data-collection session. No GABRIEL run, no model/API calls, no Harvard Proxy calls, no ingestion, no corpus/inbox changes, no `data/contracts.csv` or `data/city_coverage.csv` edits, no causal claim.

## 1. Purpose and PI request

This memo responds directly to a PI request to look at public-safety-versus-non-safety wage-mechanism patterns outside Massachusetts. The PI's stated reasoning: "I think it is important to look at a state other than Mass, since Mass has this arbitration provision that many people say is responsible for crazy wages. Can you look at one or two other states?" The PI suggested Texas and Ohio by name.

**This is scoping and source-acquisition planning, not causal evidence.** No Texas or Ohio document has been collected, no city has been added to `data/contracts.csv` or `data/city_coverage.csv`, and nothing in this memo should be read as a finding about actual Texas or Ohio wage outcomes. It is a bounded institutional-law scan (state statutes, state labor-relations agencies, and a light pass over municipal CBA/labor-relations portals) intended to answer one question: **is Texas and/or Ohio a good next source-acquisition target for this project's design, and if so, on what hypotheses?**

Texas and Ohio are not, and should not be described as, "representative" of the United States. They are two additional, deliberately chosen contrast cases, selected because their public-sector labor-law architecture differs from Massachusetts's in different ways from each other.

## 2. Why Texas and Ohio are useful contrasts

The project's central Massachusetts finding is an institutional one: police and fire in Massachusetts have a *specific, compulsory arbitration institution* (the Joint Labor-Management Committee, JLMC, arising from Chapter 589 of the Acts of 1987 / historically cited in this project's corpus as "MA G.L. c. 1078") that channels wage pressure directly into binding outcomes, while every other municipal occupation in this project's corpus (teachers, DPW/public works, clerical/admin, library) uses the weaker, non-compulsory Chapter 150E Section 9 route (mediation, advisory fact-finding, rare voluntary arbitration). The open question the PI is raising is whether the safety/non-safety wage-mechanism gap this project has been documenting is a **general public-safety institutional phenomenon**, or whether it is closer to a **Massachusetts-JLMC-specific artifact**.

- **Texas** is useful because its default rule runs the opposite direction from Massachusetts's: Texas Government Code Chapter 617 broadly *prohibits* public-sector collective bargaining and union recognition for all public employees, safety and non-safety alike. Police and fire get a narrow, **locally optional** escape hatch — full collective bargaining under Local Government Code Chapter 174 (adopted city-by-city via referendum) or the lighter "meet and confer" process under Chapter 142/146 (adopted city-by-city via ordinance or petition) — while non-safety municipal employees generally remain under Chapter 617's bargaining prohibition, governed instead through civil-service classification and pay-plan systems. Texas therefore tests whether the safety/non-safety gap persists **even where the specific mechanism is "does collective bargaining exist at all," not "which arbitration track applies within an existing bargaining relationship."** This is a genuinely different lever than JLMC-vs-150E, and if the gap still shows up under this different institutional design, that is informative; if it does not, that too is informative.
- **Ohio** is useful because it is the reverse test: Ohio Revised Code Chapter 4117 is a **single, statewide, all-covered-employee** public-sector bargaining statute — teachers, clerical, public works, police, and fire are all covered by the same chapter, unlike Massachusetts's two-track statutory split. The safety/non-safety distinction in Ohio shows up *within* this single statute, at the impasse-resolution stage: employees statutorily barred from striking (police, firefighters, and several other named "safety forces" — see Section 5) are automatically routed to binding, final-offer conciliation (ORC 4117.14(D)(1) and (G)) if fact-finding fails, while employees who retain the statutory strike right (most other public employees, including teachers, clerical, and public works, subject to a 10-day notice) are not. Ohio therefore tests whether the JLMC-style "compulsory arbitration for safety, weaker impasse route for everyone else" pattern **replicates inside a single unified statute**, rather than across two entirely separate statutory regimes as in Massachusetts.
- **Massachusetts** remains the project's baseline, already heavily documented in this project's corpus and in `docs/analysis/public_sector_impasse_arbitration_state_law_citation_audit_2026-07-05.md`.

Together, Texas and Ohio let the project ask: does the safety/non-safety wage-mechanism gap persist (a) when the institutional lever is bargaining-rights *availability* rather than arbitration-track choice (Texas), and (b) when the safety/non-safety distinction is embedded within one statute rather than split across two (Ohio)? Neither state, alone or together, can answer the causal "why" question this project is ultimately interested in — that requires actual matched-city corpus text, which this run does not collect.

## 3. State institutional profiles

### Massachusetts (baseline)

- **Public-sector collective bargaining structure:** Chapter 150E covers essentially all Massachusetts public employees, including municipal employees. Police and fire have a second, additional layer: JLMC-ordered compulsory interest arbitration when Chapter 150E's ordinary bargaining process fails to produce an agreement ("an apparent exhaustion of the process of collective bargaining which constitutes a potential threat to public welfare" — quoted from this project's own Somerville corpus rows, e.g. `ma_somerville_police_spea_2012`).
- **Police/fire bargaining/impasse/arbitration:** compulsory, JLMC-invoked binding interest arbitration; statutory criteria include ability to pay, comparable-town wages/benefits, and cost of living (confirmed directly from this project's own corpus arbitration-award text).
- **Non-safety institutions:** Chapter 150E Section 9 — mediation, advisory (non-binding) fact-finding, and a rare voluntary-arbitration option; no compulsory arbitration backstop. Confirmed across this project's own teacher, DPW, and clerical/admin institutional-context memos (`ma_teacher_bargaining_school_finance_institutional_context_2026-07-04.md`, `ma_dpw_bargaining_impasse_context_2026-07-04.md`, `ma_clerical_admin_bargaining_impasse_context_2026-07-05.md`).
- **Strike restrictions:** strikes are prohibited for all Massachusetts public employees under Chapter 150E, not just safety; this project's corpus rows for both safety and non-safety units confirm `no_strike_clause_flag=1` where recorded.
- **Local-option/city variation:** none — both JLMC and Chapter 150E apply statewide by default; there is no local adoption step (a key structural difference from Texas, discussed below).
- **Fit as a comparison baseline:** already this project's central case; used here only as the point of comparison.
- **Source citations:** Chapter 589, Acts of 1987 (JLMC); M.G.L. c. 150E §9; this project's own corpus (`data/contracts.csv`); `docs/analysis/public_sector_impasse_arbitration_state_law_citation_audit_2026-07-05.md`.

### Texas

- **Public-sector collective bargaining structure:** the *default* rule, Texas Government Code Chapter 617, prohibits any political-subdivision official from entering a collective bargaining contract with a labor organization, or recognizing a labor organization as a bargaining agent, for any public employee — safety or non-safety. A contract made in violation is void. Public employees generally may not strike; a striking employee forfeits civil-service and reemployment rights (Tex. Gov't Code §617.002 et seq.).
- **Police/fire bargaining/impasse/arbitration:** two distinct, both **locally optional**, carve-outs from Chapter 617:
  - **Local Government Code Chapter 174** ("Fire and Police Employee Relations Act," 1973/1993): full collective bargaining rights for fire fighters and/or police officers, but **only in a political subdivision that has adopted the chapter by local referendum election** (Tex. Loc. Gov't Code §174.051 — triggered by a voter petition of the lesser of 20,000 signatures or 5% of the last general-election turnout; §174.053 allows a later repeal election). Arbitration under Chapter 174 is **not compulsory**: §174.163 states plainly, "This chapter does not require compulsory arbitration," and §174.153 confirms that even when one party requests an arbitration board, both parties must make a separate, written, mutual "election... to arbitrate" within 5 days — arbitration cannot be unilaterally imposed by either side. (One caveat: §174.1535, "Mandatory Arbitration," is referenced as an exception within §174.153(a) but was not independently retrieved and read this session — flagged as a follow-up in Section 8 of the companion citation audit CSV.)
  - **Local Government Code Chapter 142/146** ("meet and confer"): a lighter-weight, also locally adopted (by ordinance or petition, not necessarily a full referendum) process in which the city "meets and confers" with a recognized police or fire association; the statute explicitly states it "does not require a public employer or a recognized police officers association to meet and confer on any issue or reach an agreement." No compulsory arbitration is created by this route either. Chapter 146 extends a broadly similar local-option bargaining/meet-and-confer authorization to **non-safety** "covered employees" (defined as municipal employees other than department heads or Chapter 143-covered firefighters/police officers) in municipalities that choose to adopt it — a genuinely different, non-safety-inclusive local-option channel this project had not previously catalogued (see Section 4 and Section 6 caveats).
- **Non-safety municipal institutions:** where a city has not adopted Chapter 146, non-safety municipal employees (clerical, public works, sanitation) have **no collective-bargaining channel at all** under Texas law; their wages and conditions are instead set through municipal civil-service classification and pay-plan systems (e.g., Houston's classification/compensation system, confirmed directly from houstontx.gov: "This guide's contents apply to municipal employees only, and generally not to police officers or firefighters," administered through a classification/pay-grade system, not a negotiated CBA).
- **Strike restrictions:** uniform statewide strike prohibition under Chapter 617, safety and non-safety alike; Chapter 174 separately reiterates the prohibition for adopting cities (§174.202) with its own penalty structure.
- **Local-option/city-specific variation:** this is the defining feature of the Texas system. Adoption is a per-city, per-employee-group choice, confirmed to vary today: San Antonio (and, per secondary reporting, El Paso, Laredo, Corpus Christi among the 100 largest Texas cities) uses full Chapter 174 collective bargaining for police; Austin, Dallas, Fort Worth, and Houston instead use the lighter Chapter 142 meet-and-confer process for police and/or fire. San Antonio's police bargaining status was also the subject of a May 2021 ballot measure (Proposition B) to repeal Chapter 174 adoption for police, which reportedly failed — a live, contested local-politics dimension worth flagging as a caveat, not a stable, uncontested institutional fact (see Section 6).
- **Why this is/is not a clean comparison to Massachusetts:** it is not a clean like-for-like comparison, precisely because the lever is different — Texas's safety/non-safety split operates at the level of "does a bargaining relationship exist at all," while Massachusetts's operates at the level of "which impasse-resolution track applies within an existing bargaining relationship." This is a feature for the PI's question, not a defect: it lets the project ask whether the safety-wage effect depends on the *specific* JLMC arbitration mechanism, or shows up under a structurally different kind of safety-favoring institution (bargaining-rights availability itself).
- **Source citations:** Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 174 (§§174.003, 174.051, 174.053, 174.153, 174.163, 174.202) via texas.public.law's aggregation of the official Texas Statutes; Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Ch. 142/146 via statutes.capitol.texas.gov and law.justia.com aggregations; Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 617 (§617.002) via law.justia.com/FindLaw aggregations of the official Texas Statutes; Ballotpedia (San Antonio Prop B, 2021); city HR/labor-relations pages (sanantonio.gov, austintexas.gov, houstontx.gov). See the companion CSV (`texas_ohio_legal_source_audit_2026-07-07.csv`) for per-claim confidence ratings — none of this session's Texas statutory citations were pulled from a paid/authenticated legal database; all are public aggregator or official state-site copies of public statutes.

### Ohio

- **Public-sector collective bargaining structure:** a single statute, Ohio Revised Code Chapter 4117 ("Public Employees' Collective Bargaining," enacted 1983), covers essentially all Ohio public employees statewide — state, county, and municipal, safety and non-safety alike, teachers included — administered by a single state agency, the State Employment Relations Board (SERB). There is no local-adoption step comparable to Texas's Chapter 174/142 elections; Chapter 4117 applies by default wherever a bargaining unit is recognized under the statute.
- **Police/fire (and other named safety-forces) bargaining/impasse/arbitration:** the safety/non-safety distinction lives inside this single statute, at the *impasse* stage, not at the bargaining-rights stage. ORC 4117.14 requires SERB-supervised mediation and, if that fails (45 days before contract expiration), a three-member SERB fact-finding panel (weighing comparable-position pay, public welfare, and employer finances, per §4117.14(C)/(G)). If fact-finding recommendations are not accepted by both sides (each side votes, needing 3/5 approval to accept) within 7 days, employees who are **statutorily prohibited from striking** — named in §4117.14(D)(1) as police and firefighters, State Highway Patrol members, deputy sheriffs, dispatchers employed by police/fire/sheriff's departments, exclusive nurses' units, employees of state schools for the deaf/blind, State Employees Retirement System employees, and corrections/psychiatric-institution guards — proceed automatically to **binding, final-offer, issue-by-issue conciliation** under §4117.14(D)(1) and (G): a single conciliator (chosen by mutual agreement or SERB appointment) holds a hearing, receives each side's final offer on each unresolved issue, and must select one side's offer per issue, weighing prior agreements, comparable public/private compensation, public interest/welfare, and ability to pay.
- **Non-safety institutions:** the same Chapter 4117 mediation-then-fact-finding process applies, but employees who are **not** on the no-strike list (this project's non-safety comparison groups — teachers, clerical, public works — are not named in §4117.14(D)(1)) retain a **conditional statutory strike right**: after a 10-day notice, provided the work stoppage covers full consecutive workdays and is at least 10 workdays after any prior strike by the same unit. There is no compulsory-arbitration backstop for these units; the strike right itself is the pressure-release valve Ohio's non-safety statute relies on, structurally different from both Massachusetts's Chapter 150E route (no compulsory arbitration, no strike right either — mediation/fact-finding only) and Texas's non-safety route (no bargaining rights at all, absent local Chapter 146 adoption).
- **Strike restrictions:** the core Ohio institutional design *is* the strike-right/no-strike-right split — safety forces (as statutorily named) cannot strike and get compulsory conciliation instead; nearly everyone else can strike (conditionally) and does not get compulsory conciliation.
- **Local-option/city variation:** essentially none for the statutory framework itself — Chapter 4117 and SERB's process apply uniformly statewide; local variation shows up only in each city's own bargaining-unit structure and contract terms, not in whether the impasse mechanism exists.
- **Fit as a comparison to Massachusetts:** cleaner, in one specific sense, than Texas — Ohio's safety/non-safety institutional split is the closest structural analogue in this scan to Massachusetts's JLMC-vs-150E split (a compulsory-arbitration backstop for safety, a weaker process for everyone else), but embedded in one statute rather than two, and paired with an explicit strike-right trade-off for non-safety employees that Massachusetts's Chapter 150E does not offer.
- **Source citations:** ORC Chapter 4117, specifically §4117.14 (via codes.ohio.gov, the official Ohio Laws site, and Justia's aggregation); SERB's own conciliation-process guidance (serb.ohio.gov Conciliation Guidebook; a bounded secondary legal-blog summary, Brian J. Smith Esq., used only for process framing, not as a legal-authority citation); SERB's public document archive (serb.ohio.gov/view-document-archive) for fact-finding reports and conciliation awards since 2012.

## 4. Texas focus

- **Chapter 174 applicability:** confirmed local-option, not automatic — see §174.051 (Adoption Election) and §174.053 (Repeal Election) above. A city (or, per §174.003(4), any "political subdivision," which the statute defines to include municipalities) is not under Chapter 174 unless its voters have adopted it.
- **Arbitration/mediation/impasse provisions:** **not automatic**. §174.163 is unambiguous ("This chapter does not require compulsory arbitration"), and §174.153's arbitration-request process still requires both sides' separate written agreement to arbitrate within 5 days of a request — meaning even in a Chapter-174-adopting city, one side (typically the city) can decline to arbitrate and the parties are left without a forced resolution mechanism, a materially different design than Massachusetts's JLMC, which does not require the employer's consent to invoke arbitration. **Flagged, not resolved, this session:** §174.153(a)'s cross-reference to §174.1535 ("Mandatory Arbitration") suggests a narrower exception may exist; this was not independently retrieved and read, and should not be assumed either way before a city-specific acquisition decision is made.
- **Police/fire meet-and-confer or collective-bargaining examples:** San Antonio operates under full Chapter 174 collective bargaining for police (2022-2026 CBA publicly posted at sanantonio.gov); Austin, Dallas, Fort Worth, and Houston use the lighter Chapter 142 meet-and-confer process (Austin's police and fire meet-and-confer agreements are posted directly on austintexas.gov, including a 2024-2029 police agreement and a 2023-2025 fire agreement).
- **Non-safety municipal bargaining limits/alternate channels:** Local Government Code Chapter 146 provides a broadly parallel local-option bargaining/meet-and-confer channel for non-safety "covered employees" in municipalities that choose to adopt it — this is a genuinely new finding for this project (prior Massachusetts-only work had not needed to consider a non-safety Texas analogue) and should be checked city-by-city before assuming any given Texas city's non-safety workforce has no bargaining channel at all. Absent Chapter 146 adoption, non-safety wages are set through civil-service classification/pay-plan systems (confirmed for Houston).
- **Candidate cities for source acquisition:** Houston, Austin, San Antonio are the three strongest near-term candidates (see Section 8/Task B table for detail); Dallas and Fort Worth are plausible secondary candidates.
- **What Texas can test relative to Massachusetts:** whether the safety-wage-pressure/institutional-translation gap persists when the institutional lever is *bargaining-rights availability itself* (adopted vs. non-adopted city, safety vs. non-safety employee group) rather than *arbitration-track choice within an existing bargaining relationship*. It can also test whether San Antonio's full Chapter 174 collective bargaining (closer in form, though not in compulsion, to Massachusetts's model) produces different-looking safety-wage mechanism text than Austin/Houston's lighter meet-and-confer model — a within-Texas comparison this project had not previously had reason to consider.

## 5. Ohio focus

- **ORC Chapter 4117:** a single statute covering essentially all Ohio public employees; see Section 3 above for the full structure.
- **SERB process:** SERB is the sole state administrative agency for both certification/recognition disputes and impasse-resolution supervision (mediation assignment, fact-finding-panel appointment, conciliator-list maintenance) — a materially more centralized administrative structure than Massachusetts's split between the general Department of Labor Relations (Chapter 150E) and the JLMC (police/fire only), or Texas's near-total absence of a state administrative agency for locally adopted Chapter 174/142 bargaining (which is instead administered directly by each adopting city and, for Chapter 174 election disputes, potentially the American Arbitration Association).
- **Fact-finding and conciliation, especially for safety forces or strike-prohibited employees:** confirmed above (§4117.14(C) fact-finding; §4117.14(D)(1) naming the no-strike/safety-forces list; §4117.14(G) conciliation mechanics and criteria). SERB maintains a public online archive of fact-finding reports and conciliation awards since 2012 (serb.ohio.gov/view-document-archive), which is a genuinely useful, centralized public-records resource this project does not currently have an equivalent of for any Massachusetts city.
- **Non-safety municipal workers, institutional comparison:** covered by the same Chapter 4117 mediation/fact-finding process, but retain a conditional strike right (10-day notice, full-consecutive-workday requirement, 10-workday spacing from a prior strike) in place of compulsory conciliation — a structurally distinct trade-off from both Massachusetts's non-safety route (no strike right, no compulsory arbitration either) and Texas's non-safety route (no statutory bargaining rights absent local Chapter 146 adoption).
- **Candidate cities for source acquisition:** Columbus is the strongest near-term candidate (a centralized city labor-relations portal at columbus.gov hosting "all six" of the city's CBAs, including a 2023-2026 FOP police contract); Cincinnati (cincinnati-oh.gov hosts multiple current CBAs, e.g. a CODE — Cincinnati Organized and Dedicated Employees — 2025-2028 contract and a Teamsters 2025-2028 contract) and Cleveland (a dedicated collective-bargaining-agreements page at clevelandohio.gov) are also strong candidates. Dayton and Akron did not surface a comparably centralized public labor-relations portal in this session's bounded search and are lower-confidence candidates pending a more targeted follow-up.
- **What Ohio can test relative to Massachusetts:** whether the compulsory-arbitration-for-safety/weaker-process-for-non-safety pattern this project has documented in Massachusetts replicates when embedded in a single unified statute administered by one state agency, rather than split across two separate statutory regimes (JLMC vs. Chapter 150E) as in Massachusetts — and whether Ohio's explicit strike-right trade-off for non-safety employees produces a different kind of wage-mechanism text (e.g., more prominent references to strike leverage or strike history) than Massachusetts's non-safety CBAs, which operate under a uniform no-strike rule for everyone.

## 7. Implications for project hypotheses

- **Arbitration/impasse backstop (this project's central mechanism, e.g. H6 in the hypothesis-disposition audit):** Texas and Ohio each offer a genuinely different test of this mechanism. Ohio most directly replicates the "compulsory arbitration for safety, no compulsion for everyone else" pattern, but via a single statute and a strike-right trade-off Massachusetts does not offer non-safety employees — meaning an Ohio finding of "similar wage-mechanism text" would need to account for the fact that non-safety Ohio employees have a real pressure-release valve (the strike right) that non-safety Massachusetts employees lack entirely. Texas is a sharper test of whether the safety/non-safety gap requires compulsory arbitration specifically, since Texas safety bargaining (where adopted) is not compulsorily arbitrated either — if Texas safety wages still show a distinct pressure-to-wage translation advantage over non-safety, that would suggest the advantage comes from *having a real bargaining relationship at all* (or its associated political leverage), not specifically from a compulsory-arbitration backstop.
- **Wage comparability:** both states' impasse or arbitration criteria reference comparable-position/comparable-employer pay (Ohio's fact-finding and conciliation criteria explicitly include "comparable" public/private compensation; Massachusetts's JLMC criteria include "comparable towns"). Texas's Chapter 174, being non-compulsory, does not have an equivalent state-mandated comparability criterion to test against — any comparability clause found in a Texas CBA would be a negotiated, not statutorily mandated, feature, a useful contrast in itself.
- **Staffing/overtime:** not addressed by this institutional scan; would require actual CBA text from acquired Texas/Ohio cities, not undertaken this session.
- **Public salience:** San Antonio's 2021 Prop B ballot fight over repealing Chapter 174 police bargaining is a concrete, dated example of public-safety bargaining rights themselves becoming a live political controversy — a genuinely different kind of public-salience event than anything documented in this project's Massachusetts corpus to date, worth flagging as a possible discourse-corpus target if that corpus is ever developed for Texas.
- **Non-safety wage restraint:** Texas's near-total absence of non-safety bargaining rights (absent Chapter 146 adoption) is the starkest non-safety-wage-restraint institutional finding in this scan — restraint here would not operate through weak arbitration criteria (as in Massachusetts's Chapter 150E, or as in Iowa's post-2017 reform documented in the existing state-law citation audit) but through the complete absence of a negotiated-wage channel, with civil-service classification systems substituting entirely.
- **Governance/source-design issues:** Ohio's centralized SERB document archive (fact-finding reports and conciliation awards since 2012, publicly searchable) is a materially better source-acquisition environment than this project has for any Massachusetts city — worth noting for future source-acquisition planning regardless of whether Ohio cities are prioritized. Texas's per-city, per-adoption-status variation means source acquisition would need to be planned city-by-city with explicit attention to which chapter (174 vs. 142 vs. 146, if adopted) applies before assuming a uniform Texas institutional story.
- **Separating "public safety" from "Massachusetts JLMC":** this is the PI's core question, and the honest answer from this scoping-only pass is: **not yet answerable from institutional law alone.** Both Texas and Ohio show that some form of safety-favoring institutional asymmetry recurs (echoing this project's own prior 14-state citation audit), but the *specific form* differs enough — Texas's bargaining-rights-availability lever, Ohio's strike-right/no-strike-right lever, Massachusetts's arbitration-track lever — that only actual matched-city CBA/award text, collected and reviewed with the same verbatim-capture discipline used for Massachusetts, could show whether the wage-mechanism *language* (comparability clauses, staffing/overtime provisions, arbitration criteria) looks similar or different across these three distinct institutional designs. This memo narrows the acquisition question; it does not resolve the substantive one.

## 8. Recommended next step

**Recommendation: do a source-acquisition plan for Texas and Ohio, but do not begin collecting documents yet.** Specifically:

1. **Pick one Texas city and one Ohio city** as first-pass acquisition targets once source acquisition is separately authorized. Based on this scan: **Houston** (Texas) for its combination of an already-located, publicly posted Fire CBA, a Chapter 142 meet-and-confer police process, and a well-documented civil-service classification system for non-safety employees (making the "no bargaining rights, civil-service pay-plan instead" contrast cleanly observable); **Columbus** (Ohio) for its centralized city labor-relations portal hosting all six of its union contracts, including a current FOP police CBA, alongside Ohio's centralized SERB archive as a backstop source. San Antonio is a strong second-choice Texas candidate specifically if the project wants the closer-to-Massachusetts full-collective-bargaining (Chapter 174) model rather than Houston's meet-and-confer model — see the candidate-target table (Task B) for the full comparison.
2. **Collect matched public-safety/non-safety documents, not public-safety-only**, consistent with this project's own cross-occupation design discipline (`CLAUDE.md`'s core matching requirement). For a Chapter 146-non-adopting Texas city, "matched non-safety" may need to mean a civil-service classification/pay-plan document rather than a CBA — this is a design question to resolve explicitly before acquisition, not an incidental detail.
3. **Do a small amount of additional legal-citation-audit work first**, specifically: (a) directly read §174.1535 ("Mandatory Arbitration") to resolve whether it creates any true compulsory-arbitration exception within Chapter 174; (b) confirm which, if any, of the candidate Texas cities have adopted Chapter 146 for non-safety employees; (c) confirm Ohio's ORC 4117.14(D)(1) no-strike list precisely before treating "police and fire" as the clean boundary in any Ohio city (dispatchers and some nurses are also on that list, which is directly relevant to this project's own already-developed dispatcher public-safety-adjacent category). These are bounded, citation-only follow-ups, not a full acquisition effort.
4. Defer any Wisconsin-, Illinois-, or Iowa-style broader state expansion until the PI has reviewed this Texas/Ohio scan and the companion candidate-target and legal-audit tables.

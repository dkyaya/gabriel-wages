## Scope

This checklist does not make causal claims about why police/fire wages rise faster than other municipal occupations. It tracks hypotheses and their evidentiary status, not conclusions. It reflects the state of this project's desk research and internal corpus review as of the session that most recently updated each row — always check the `verification_status` and `next_action` columns rather than assuming a mechanism is settled because it appears here. This file does not replace the underlying occupation-specific memos; it points to them and summarizes their findings at a glance. When a claim needs full context (a citation, a quoted contract clause, a specific statutory reference), follow the pointer in `notes` back to the source memo rather than treating this checklist's cell text as the full evidentiary record.

## How to use this checklist

- **Scanning for a mechanism:** find it by occupation group (Sections 3-9) or, if it could apply to more than one group, check Section 10 (cross-cutting) first.
- **Planning a source-acquisition or verification session:** start from Section 11 (source/document types) to see which document family is best suited to the mechanism you're chasing, then check the mechanism's own `next_action` cell.
- **Before recommending a new GABRIEL attribute:** check whether the mechanism's `verification_status` is at least `confirmed in current corpus` or `confirmed in external sources` for more than one occupation group — attributes built on `not searched` or `weak evidence` mechanisms are premature.
- **Before citing a hypothesis to the PI or in a synthesis memo:** check `current_repo_evidence` for whether the claim is corpus-grounded or still desk-research-only, and check Section 13 for any metadata caveat attached to the specific rows that would support it.
- **After any new work** (a national qualitative scan, a metadata-cleanup pass, a new corpus scan, an OEWS/DESE/BLS descriptive baseline, a GABRIEL extraction run): update the relevant rows' `verification_status`, `current_repo_evidence`, and `next_action` cells, and add a dated note to `notes` if the finding is worth preserving verbatim. See Section 14 (Update protocol) for the discipline to follow.

## Status vocabulary

Use exactly these values in `verification_status`. Do not invent new ones without adding them here first.

- **not searched** — no desk research or corpus review has been done on this specific mechanism yet.
- **partially searched** — some desk research or corpus review has been done, but not enough to reach a confirmed or contradicted status (e.g., only national background found, no Massachusetts-specific or corpus-specific check yet).
- **confirmed in current corpus** — this project's own already-collected `data/contracts.csv` rows and corpus files contain direct textual evidence of the mechanism.
- **confirmed in external sources** — a credible external source (government agency, academic/policy research organization, primary legal source) has been found and cited, independent of whether this project's own corpus shows it.
- **weak evidence** — some evidence exists but is thin, indirect, from a low-authority source (e.g., industry/vendor marketing material), or applies only to a single illustrative example rather than a pattern.
- **not found in current corpus** — this project's corpus was reviewed specifically for this mechanism and the relevant language was absent. This is itself a finding, not merely an unexplored gap — record it as distinct from "not searched."
- **contradicted** — evidence found runs counter to the mechanism as originally framed (e.g., the BLS finding that there is no evidence of a secular truck-driver shortage, which contradicts a naive CDL-scarcity claim).
- **needs metadata cleanup** — the mechanism's evidentiary status is entangled with a known data-quality issue in `data/contracts.csv` or the corpus (see Section 11); do not treat current repo evidence at face value until the cleanup issue is resolved.
- **not applicable** — the mechanism does not apply to this occupation group by construction (e.g., JLMC eligibility does not apply to any non-safety group).

## 1. Overview

The project's central empirical question — why police/fire wages may rise faster than other municipal occupations — has two sides, and this checklist tracks both:

**(a) Upward pressure on police/fire wages.** Mechanisms that could push police and/or fire compensation up faster than other municipal occupations: recruitment/retention strain, minimum-staffing and overtime dynamics, credentialing barriers, hazard/risk premia, political salience, comparator/peer-community ratchets, and Massachusetts's JLMC compulsory-interest-arbitration backstop.

**(b) Weaker upward pressure, alternate wage-translation channels, or wage restraint among non-safety occupations.** Mechanisms that could explain why teachers, DPW, clerical/admin, and library wages grow more slowly even when they face comparable underlying labor-market or workload pressure: classification-and-reclassification systems that absorb pressure administratively rather than through bargaining, shortage-buffering channels that substitute cheaper labor or degraded service rather than raising wages, budget structures specific to each occupation (Chapter 70/net school spending for teachers; enterprise funds and prevailing-wage law for DPW; confidential-employee exclusions for some clerical/admin roles; the MBLC Municipal Appropriation Requirement for library), and the shared finding — now independently verified four times, for teachers, DPW, clerical/admin, and library — that none of these four non-safety groups has JLMC-equivalent compulsory interest arbitration.

The gap between police/fire wage growth and non-safety wage growth could widen because (a) intensifies, because (b) intensifies, or both. This checklist is organized so that every mechanism on either side can be checked, sourced, and updated independently, rather than assuming the answer lies predominantly on one side.

## 14. Current corpus evidence summary

- **v9 (`comparability_emphasis`) evidence** remains concentrated in safety-side arbitration/award-style documents (Somerville SPSOA/SPEA JLMC awards), with CBA evidence for comparability language remaining limited across the corpus generally — the same source-type asymmetry flagged since the earliest mechanism memos: explicit comparator reasoning shows up where an institution (JLMC) forces it onto the page, not necessarily wherever comparator logic actually operates in bargaining.
- **DPW current corpus evidence** is comparatively rich: classification-tied credential pay (CDL, hoisting, water/wastewater licenses), overtime/snow/storm-specific rules, and contractor-substitution language are all directly confirmed across the seven `public_works` rows, per the dedicated DPW corpus-scan session. Worcester's and Seekonk's clerical/admin documents are comparatively thin (a short successor MOA and a simple school-based unit contract, respectively). Peer-community comparability and pay-compression language are genuine, confirmed absences here too.

## 16. Update protocol

Future sessions should update this checklist rather than create a new one. Specifically:

- **After the national qualitative scan** (the next planned step per `non_safety_comparison_roadmap_2026-07-04.md`): add a note to each mechanism row where the national scan found the Massachusetts-specific institutional pattern (JLMC for police/fire; Chapter 150E Section 9 for teachers/DPW/clerical-admin/library) to be similar or different in other states. Consider adding a `geographic_scope` note in `notes` rather than a new column, unless the pattern proves to need its own column across many rows.
- **After a metadata-cleanup review** (audit-first, not direct-edit-first, per Section 13): update the affected mechanism rows' `verification_status` away from `needs metadata cleanup` (or add that status where a newly-discovered issue warrants it) once the underlying `data/contracts.csv` or corpus issue is actually resolved, and update Section 13 to mark the issue as closed rather than deleting the historical record of what was found and when.
- **After future corpus scans** (e.g., if additional cities are added to the `public_works`, `clerical_admin`, or `teacher` occupation classes, or if a police/fire-focused corpus-scan session is run to mirror the non-safety ones already completed): update `current_repo_evidence` and `verification_status` for the affected rows, and add any newly-discovered mechanisms as new rows with new `mechanism_id` values (continue the existing numbering scheme per occupation group; do not renumber existing rows).
- **After OEWS/DESE/BLS descriptive baselines are eventually built** (not yet authorized as of this checklist's creation): update the composition-effect rows (TC06, XC13) and any wage-level rows with a pointer to the specific descriptive dataset, and be explicit in `notes` about what the baseline can and cannot show (occupation-level, not bargaining-unit-level, per the existing trend-baseline implementation plan's own limitations section).
- **After future GABRIEL extraction runs:** update `verification_status` toward `confirmed in current corpus` (if the attribute finds real signal in already-collected documents) or add a new `contradicted`/`weak evidence` note if the run's findings complicate a mechanism's current framing. Record the specific GABRIEL attribute name and run date in `notes` so a future reader can trace the evidentiary chain back to a specific run.
- **General discipline:** update statuses, `current_repo_evidence`, and `next_action` in place; do not delete a row's prior finding when updating it — append a dated note to `notes` instead (e.g., "2026-08-01: national scan confirmed X") so this file keeps functioning as a history of what was found and when, not just a snapshot of the current belief.

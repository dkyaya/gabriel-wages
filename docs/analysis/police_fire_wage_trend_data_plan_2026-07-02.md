# Police and Fire Wage Trend Data Plan

**Date:** 2026-07-02  
**Scope:** descriptive data-planning memo only

## 1. Purpose

This note lays out what wage trends can realistically be measured for police and fire using public sources, and what cannot. The main distinction is between:

- occupation-level descriptive trends; and
- bargaining-unit-level negotiated wage trends.

Those are not the same thing and should not be blended.

## 2. What can be measured with BLS OEWS

The BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics program produces annual employment and wage estimates for occupations at the national, state, and metropolitan/nonmetropolitan-area levels ([OEWS](https://www.bls.gov/oes/)).

Realistic uses:

- national police-versus-fire wage trend comparison;
- state-level police-versus-fire wage comparison;
- metro/nonmetro descriptive comparison where geography is relevant;
- comparison to other municipal-relevant occupations such as teachers, librarians, clerical, transit, public works proxies, and nurses where occupational mappings are acceptable.

Advantages:

- standardized;
- public;
- repeatable;
- strong for broad descriptive wage comparisons.

Limits:

- not bargaining-unit-specific;
- not municipality-specific in a clean way;
- mixes employers unless carefully filtered;
- compensation is occupational wage data, not negotiated schedule data.

## 3. What can be measured with Census ASPEP

ASPEP provides state and local government employment and gross March payroll by government function ([ASPEP](https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/apes.html)).

Realistic uses:

- public-sector employment/payroll context for police protection, fire protection, and other government functions;
- broad staffing/payroll shifts in state and local government;
- descriptive public-sector context for whether police/fire payrolls are rising in tandem with or differently from other functions.

Advantages:

- public-sector specific;
- function-based rather than all-employer occupation-based.

Limits:

- function, not bargaining unit;
- gross March payroll, not annualized negotiated base wage;
- often better for context than for precise wage comparison.

## 4. What could be measured from state payroll or pension transparency

Some states publish employee-level or title-level payroll data, overtime, and pension information.

Potential uses:

- city- or county-specific pay distributions;
- overtime intensity;
- retirement patterns;
- job-title wage clustering.

Advantages:

- closer to realized pay than contract schedules;
- often captures overtime, detail, and premium pay.

Limits:

- titles may not map cleanly to bargaining units;
- base pay and overtime may be bundled;
- inconsistent availability across states;
- public release formats vary widely.

## 5. What requires local payroll data

The following usually require municipal payroll exports, transparency portals, or local budget appendices:

- realized earnings by unit;
- overtime/staffing spirals;
- callback burden;
- sick-leave backfill pressure;
- concentration of high earners within police/fire relative to other units.

This layer is valuable for mechanism discovery but is not nationally standardized.

## 6. Is county-level measurement realistic?

Only partly.

County-level occupational wage trends are not the natural strength of OEWS for this project. OEWS is strongest at nation/state/metro/nonmetro levels. Some state transparency sources may allow county or county-equivalent analyses, but those are state-specific, not generalizable.

So:

- county-level descriptive work may be possible in selected states;
- a clean, comparable county-level national panel is not realistic from the core public sources listed here.

## 7. Why bargaining-unit wages are usually unavailable in national datasets

National datasets like OEWS and ASPEP do not identify local bargaining units such as "Cambridge police patrol" or "Franklin library."

That means they cannot directly recover:

- negotiated step schedules;
- unit-specific COLA patterns;
- longevity schedules;
- differential treatment of patrol versus sergeants;
- fire suppression versus other fire personnel distinctions where contract structures matter.

For this reason, bargaining-unit-level wage trends are usually a contract-schedule or local payroll problem, not a national-dataset problem.

## 8. Can contract wage schedules be converted into unit-level wage trends?

Yes, but selectively.

Where public CBAs or MOAs include clear wage tables, they can often be converted into:

- top-step trend series;
- step-grid snapshots;
- across-the-board increase series;
- comparable cycle-by-cycle nominal wage changes.

Main limits:

- schedule format changes across contracts;
- side letters or MOAs may update wages without fully restating the schedule;
- step advancement and longevity complicate worker-level interpretation;
- schedules capture negotiated base pay, not realized earnings.

Still, this is likely the best route to real bargaining-unit trend measurement where public contracts already exist.

## 9. Staged plan

### Stage 1. National/state/metro descriptive trend

Use OEWS to build:

- police wage trend;
- firefighter wage trend;
- selected non-safety comparison occupations;
- national, Massachusetts, and selected metro comparisons.

This is the fastest public baseline.

### Stage 2. Massachusetts municipal payroll or salary schedules if public

If public sources are readily available, pull:

- municipal payroll exports;
- salary ordinances;
- public compensation appendices;
- budget overtime tables.

Use this for descriptive within-state context, not for a full national claim.

### Stage 3. Bargaining-unit contract wage schedules only where already in corpus or clearly public

Convert public contract wage tables into:

- cycle-level negotiated wage schedules;
- top-step series;
- unit-level comparisons within cities.

Do not broaden into a new acquisition push at this stage.

### Stage 4. Broader-state source snippets for mechanism discovery

Use a small curated snippet set from outside Massachusetts to discover whether:

- arbitration rules differ;
- comparator logic differs;
- staffing/overtime pressure differs;
- volunteer-to-career transition pressure appears in fire outside the current state focus.

This stage is for mechanism discovery, not production measurement.

## 10. Recommendation

Start with OEWS plus ASPEP because they are public, standardized, and descriptive. Treat unit-level wage trends as a second-stage contract/payload task. Do not promise county-level or bargaining-unit-level national measurement from BLS/Census alone.
